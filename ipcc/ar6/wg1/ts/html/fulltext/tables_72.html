2 Since AR5, further modelling work has been conducted on aerosol-based solar radiation modification (SRM) options\nsuch as stratospheric aerosol injection, marine cloud brightening, and cirrus cloud thinning21 and their climate and\nbiogeochemical effects. These investigations have consistently shown that SRM could offset some of the effects of\nincreasing greenhouse gases on global and regional climate, including the carbon and water cycles (high confidence).\nHowever, there would be substantial residual or overcompensating climate change at the regional scales and seasonal\ntime scales (high confidence), and large uncertainties associated with aerosol–cloud–radiation interactions persist.\nThe cooling caused by SRM would increase the global land and ocean CO sinks (medium confidence), but this\n2\nwould not stop CO from increasing in the atmosphere or affect the resulting ocean acidification under continued\n2\nanthropogenic emissions (high confidence). It is likely that abrupt water cycle changes will occur if SRM techniques\nare implemented rapidly. A sudden and sustained termination of SRM in a high CO emissions scenario would cause\n2\nrapid climate change (high confidence). However, a gradual phase-out of SRM combined with emissions reduction and\ncarbon dioxide removal (CDR) would avoid these termination effects (medium confidence). {4.6.3, 5.6.3. 6.4.6, 8.6.3}.
