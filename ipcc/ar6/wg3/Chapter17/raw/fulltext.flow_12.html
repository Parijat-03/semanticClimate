17.2.2 Institutions, Governance, and Political Economy 18
This subsection focuses on institutions, governance and the political economy. Institutional and
19
governance arrangements can influence which actors possess authority, as well as how motivated they
20
are to cooperate in transition processes that are directed at finding solutions to climate change and other
21
sustainability challenges. Often cooperation is enabled when policy frameworks or institutions align
22
climate change with the political and economic interests of national governments, cities or businesses,
23
and when institutional and governance arguments that support that alignment expand the scale of the
24
transitions. However, there may also be political and economic interests and structures that can lock in
25
unsustainable development patterns, frustrate this alignment and slow down transitions (Haas 2021;
26
Mattioli et al. 2020; Newell and Mulvaney 2013; Power 2016).
27
28
An extensive literature has examined how the international climate agreements and architecture
29
influence collaboration across countries regarding climate and sustainable development to support a
30
transition (Bradley 2005). For example, international institutions offer opportunities for governments
31
and other actors to share new perspectives on integrated solutions (Cole 2015). For some observers,
32
however, decades of difficulties in crafting a comprehensive climate-change agreement and the
33
resulting fragmented climate-policy landscape have been inimical to the collaboration needed for a
34
transition (Chapter 1 and 13; Nasiritousi and Bäckstrand 2019; van Asselt 2014). Yet others see the
35
potential for more incremental cooperation across countries, even without a single, integrated forms of
36
climate governance (Keohane and Victor 2016).
37
38
A related argument suggests that fragmentation at the global level provides opportunities for
39
cooperation at the national level (Kanie and Biermann 2017). For example, in contrast to the relatively
40
top-down Kyoto Protocol, the bottom-up pledge and review architecture of the Paris Agreement has
41
prompted national governments to integrate climate change with other sustainable development
42
priorities (Nachmany and Setzer 2018; Townshend et al. 2013). Concrete examples included
43
incorporating the SDGs into the NDCs as an international response to climate change (The Energy and
44
Resources Institute 2017) or bringing climate into sustainable development strategies and so-called
45
voluntary national reviews (VNRs) as part of the SDG and 2030 Agenda process (Elder and King 2018;
46
Elder and Bartalini 2019).
