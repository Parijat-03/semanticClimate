17.3.2.3 Stranded assets, inequality and just transitions
7
As the momentum towards achieving carbon neutrality grows, the risk of certain assets becoming
8
stranded is on the increase. International policies and the push for low-carbon technologies in the
9
context of climate change are reducing the demand for and value of fossil-fuel products. Stranded assets
10
become devalued before the end of their economic life or can no longer be monetised due to changes in
11
policies and regulatory frameworks, technological change, security, or environmental disruption. In
12
short, stranded assets are “assets that have suffered from unanticipated or premature write-down,
13
devaluations or conversions to liabilities” (Caldecott et al. 2013).
14
15
Stranded assets are likely to “lose economic value ahead of their anticipated useful life” (Bos and Gupta
16
2019). They are often described as creative when they become stranded because of innovation,
17
competition or economic growth (Gupta et al. 2020). Divestment refers to “the action or process of
18
selling off subsidiary business interests or investments.’’ This often occurs due to changing social norms
19
and perceptions of climate change.
20
21
Indeed, pressure is mounting on fossil-fuel industries to remove their capital from heavy carbon
22
industries. As the former Governor of the Bank of England, Mark Carney, remarked, a wholesale
23
reassessment of prospects, especially if it were to occur suddenly, could potentially destabilise markets,
24
sparking a pro-cyclical crystallisation of losses and a persistent tightening of financial conditions. In
25
other words, an abrupt resolution to the tragedy of horizons itself poses a risk to financial stability
26
(OECD 2015). The divestment narrative is also based on the view that a shift away from intensive
27
carbon resources will be significant, as the “less value will be destroyed, […] the more can be re-
28
invested in low carbon infrastructure’(OECD 2015). Social movements are critical to triggering rapid
29
transformational change and moving away from dangerous levels of climate change (Mckibben 2012).
30
Although divestment is hailed as a necessary action to decouple fossil fuel from growth and force
31
carbon-intensive industries to go out of business, there is the sense that there is no shortage of investors
32
who are willing to buy shares, so that such resources are not stranded, but simply relocated. Criticism
33
has been levelled at the divestment movement for not having a significant impact on funding fossil fuels
34
and not being sufficiently in tune with other wide-ranging complexities that go beyond the moral
35
dimensions (Bergman 2018). Despite being labelled a ‘moral entrepreneur’, the divestment movement
36
has the potential to disrupt current practices in the fossil-fuel industry, shape a ‘disruptive innovation’
37
and contribute to a strategy for decarbonising economies globally (Bergman 2018). Divestment is
38
contributing to the political situation that is ‘weakening the political and economic stronghold of the
39
fossil fuel industry’ (Grady-Benson and Sarathy 2016).
40
41
The risks attached to the stranding of fossil-fuel assets have increased with the recent and sustained
42
plunge in oil prices because of the global health pandemic (COVID-19) and the concomitant economic
43
downturn, forcing demand to plummet to unprecedentedly low levels. (Oil prices have recently
44
increased). Many economies in transition and countries dependent on fossil fuels are going through
45
turbulent times where asset and transition management will be critical (UNEP/SEI 2020). However,
46
COVID-19 provides a foretaste of what a low-carbon transition could look like, especially if assets
47
become stranded in an effort to respond to the call for action in ‘building back better’ and putting clean
48
energy jobs and the just transition at the heart of the post-COVID-19 recovery (IEA 2020; United
49
Nations General Assembly 2021). COVID-19 provides a useful proxy for issuing two alerts. First, it is
50
a reminder of the urgency of addressing climate change, given that delaying the move away from
51
stranded assets will further worsen climate change. Second, failure to recognize the threat from stranded
52
assets will result in new assets becoming stranded (Rempel and Gupta 2021). Hence, the momentum
53
towards a transformational push is resting on a new opportunity ushered in by COVID-19 to emphasize
54
the urgency for a new departure towards rapid emissions reductions (Cronin et al. 2021).
