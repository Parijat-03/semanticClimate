The global benefits of pathways likely to limit warming to 2°C outweigh global mitigation costs 4 over the 21stcentury, if aggregated economic impacts of climate change are at the moderate to 5 high end of the assessed range, and a weight consistent with economic theory is given to economic 6 impacts over the long-term. This holds true even without accounting for benefits in other 7 sustainable development dimensions or non-market damages from climate change (medium 8 confidence). The aggregate global economic repercussions of mitigation pathways include: the 9 macroeconomic impacts of investments in low-carbon solutions and structural changes away from 10 emitting activities; co-benefits and adverse side effects of mitigation; avoided climate change impacts; 11 and, reduced adaptation costs. Existing quantifications of the global aggregate economic impacts show 12 a strong dependence on socioeconomic development conditions, as these shape exposure and 13 vulnerability and adaptation opportunities and responses. Avoided impacts for poorer households and 14 poorer countries represent a smaller share in aggregate economic quantifications expressed in GDP or 15 monetary terms, whereas their well-being and welfare effects are comparatively larger. When aggregate 16 economic benefits from avoided climate change impacts are accounted for, mitigation is a welfare-17 enhancing strategy (high confidence). {3.6.2} 18
The economic benefits on human health from air quality improvement arising from mitigation 19 action can be of the same order of magnitude as mitigation costs, and potentially even larger 20 (medium confidence). {3.6.3} 21
Differences in aggregate employment between mitigation pathways and reference scenarios are 22 relatively small, although there may be substantial reallocations across sectors, with job creation 23 in some sectors and job losses in others (medium confidence). The net employment effect (and 24 whether employment increases or decreases) depends on the scenario assumptions, modelling 25 framework, and modelled policy design. Mitigation has implications for employment through multiple 26 channels, each of which impacts geographies, sectors and skill-categories differently. {3.6.4} 27
The economic repercussions of mitigation vary widely across regions and households, depending 28 on policy design and the level of international cooperation (high confidence). Delayed global 29 cooperation increases policy costs across regions, especially in those that are relatively carbon intensive 30 at present (high confidence). Pathways with uniform carbon values show higher mitigation costs in 31 more carbon-intensive regions, in fossil-fuels exporting regions, and in poorer regions (high 32 confidence). Aggregate quantifications expressed in GDP or monetary terms undervalue the economic 33 effects on households in poorer countries; the actual effects on welfare and well-being are 34 comparatively larger (high confidence). Mitigation at the speed and scale required to likely limit 35 warming to 2°C or below implies deep economic and structural changes, thereby raising multiple types 36 of distributional concerns across regions, income classes, and sectors (high confidence). Box TS.6: Understanding net zero CO2 and net zero GHG emissions 41
Reaching net zero CO2 emissions* globally along with reductions in other GHG emissions is necessary 42 to halt global warming at any level. At the point of net zero, the amount of CO2 human activity is putting 43 into the atmosphere equals the amount of CO2 human activity is removing from the atmosphere. 44 Reaching and sustaining net zero CO2 emissions globally would stabilise CO2-induced warming. 45
