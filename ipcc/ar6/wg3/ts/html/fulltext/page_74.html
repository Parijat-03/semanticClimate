Global GHG emissions from buildings were 12 GtCO2-eq in 2019, equivalent to 21% of global 2 GHG emissions. Of this, 57% (6.8 GtCO2-eq) were indirect emissions from offsite generation of 3 electricity and heat, 24% (2.9 GtCO2-eq) direct emissions produced onsite and 18% (2.2 GtCO2-4 eq) were embodied emissions from the production of cement and steel used in buildings (high 5 confidence). Most building sector emissions are CO2. Final energy demand from buildings reached 128 6 EJ globally in 2019 (around 31% of global final energy demand), and electricity demand from buildings 7 was slightly above 43 EJ globally (around 18% of global electricity demand). Residential buildings 8 consumed 70% (90 EJ) of the global final energy demand from buildings. Over the period 1990-2019, 9 global CO2 emissions from buildings increased by 50%, global final energy demand from buildings 10 grew by 38% and global final electricity demand increased by 161%. {9.3} 11
In most regions, historical improvements in efficiency have been approximately matched by 12 growth in floor area per capita (high confidence). At the global level, building specific drivers of 13 GHG emissions include: (i) population growth, especially in developing countries; (ii) increasing floor 14 area per capita, driven by the increasing size of dwellings while the size of households kept decreasing, 15 especially in developed countries; (iii) the inefficiency of newly constructed buildings, especially in 16 developing countries, and the low renovation rates and low ambition level in developed countries when 17 existing buildings are renovated; iv) the increase in use, number and size of appliances and equipment, 18 especially Information and Communication Technologies (ICT) and cooling, driven by income; and, 19 (v) the continued reliance on carbon intensive electricity and heat. These factors taken together are 20 projected to continue driving increased GHG emissions in the building sector in the future. {9.3, 9.6, 21 9.9} 22
Building sector GHG emissions were assessed using the Sufficiency, Efficiency, Renewable (SER) 23 framework. Sufficiency measures tackle the causes of GHG emissions by limiting the demand for 24 energy and materials over the lifecycle of buildings and appliances (high confidence). In Chapter 25 9 of this report, sufficiency differs from efficiency. Sufficiency is about long-term actions driven by non-26 technological solutions, which consume less energy in absolute terms. Efficiency, in contrast is about 27 continuous short-term marginal technological improvements. Use of the SER framework reduces the 28 cost of constructing and using buildings without reducing occupant’s well-being and comfort. {9.1, 9.4, 29 9.5, 9.9} 30
Sufficiency interventions do not consume energy during the use phase of buildings and do not 31 require maintenance nor replacement over the lifetime of buildings. Density, compacity, 32 bioclimatic design to optimise the use of nature-based solutions, multi-functionality of space through 33 shared space and to allow for adjusting the size of buildings to the evolving needs of households, 34 circular use of materials and repurposing unused existing buildings to avoid using virgin materials, 35 optimisation of the use of buildings through lifestyle changes, use of the thermal mass of buildings to 36 reduce thermal needs, moving from ownership to usership of appliances are among the sufficiency 37 interventions implemented in leading municipalities (high confidence). At a global level, up to 17% of 38 the mitigation potential in the buildings sector could be captured by 2050 through sufficiency 39 interventions (medium confidence).
