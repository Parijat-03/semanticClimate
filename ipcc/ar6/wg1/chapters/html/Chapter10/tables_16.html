1 Special Report on Climate Change and Land (SRCCL; IPCC, 2019a)
2 The SRCCL (Jia et al., 2019) assessed that there is robust evidence and high agreement that land cover and land use or management
3 exert significant influence on atmospheric states (e.g., temperature, rainfall, wind intensity) and phenomena (e.g., monsoons), at various\nspatial and temporal scales, through their biophysical influences on climate. There is robust evidence that dry soil moisture anomalies\nfavour summer heatwaves. Part of the projected increase in heatwaves and droughts can be attributed to soil moisture feedbacks\nin regions where evapotranspiration is limited by moisture availability (medium confidence). Vegetation changes can also amplify\nor dampen extreme events through changes in albedo and evapotranspiration, which will influence future trends in extreme events\n(medium confidence).
4 The influence of different changes in land use (e.g., afforestation, urbanization), on the local climate depends on the background\nclimate (robust evidence, high agreement). There is high confidence that regional climate change can be dampened or enhanced by\nchanges in local land cover and land use, with sign and magnitude depending on region and season.
5 Water management and irrigation were generally not accounted for by CMIP5 global models available at the time of SRCCL.\nAdditional water can modify regional energy and moisture balance particularly in areas with highly productive agricultural crops\nwith high rate of evapotranspiration. Urbanization increases the risks associated with extreme events (high confidence). Urbanization\nsuppresses evaporative cooling and amplifies heatwave intensity (high confidence) with a strong influence on minimum temperatures\n(high confidence).
6 Special Report on the Ocean and Cryosphere in a Changing Climate (SROCC; IPCC, 2019b)
7 The SROCC (IPCC, 2019b) stated that observations and models for assessing changes in the ocean and the cryosphere have
8 been developed considerably during the past century but observations in some key regions remain under-sampled and were very\nshort relative to the time scales of natural variability and anthropogenic changes. Retreat of mountain glaciers and thawing of\nmountain permafrost continues and will continue due to significant warming in those regions, where it is likely to exceed global\ntemperature increase.
9 The SROCC assessed that it is virtually certain that Antarctica and Greenland have lost mass over the past decade and observed glacier\nmass loss over the last decades is attributable to anthropogenic climate change (high confidence). It is virtually certain that projected\nwarming will result in continued loss in Arctic sea ice in summer, but there is low confidence in climate model projections of Antarctic\nsea ice change because of model biases and disagreement with observed trends. Knowledge and observations of the polar regions\nwere sparse compared to many other regions, due to remoteness and challenges of operating in them.
10 The sensitivity of small islands and coastal areas to increased sea levels differs between emissions scenarios and regionally,\nand a consideration of local processes is critical for projections of sea level influences at local scales.
11 Special Report on Global Warming of 1.5°C (SR1.5; IPCC, 2018b)
12 The SR1.5 (Hoegh-Guldberg et al., 2018) assessed that most land regions were experiencing greater warming than the global average,
13 with annual average warming already exceeding 1.5°C in many regions. Over one quarter of the global population live in regions that\nhave already experienced more than 1.5°C of warming in at least one season. Land regions will warm more than ocean regions over\nthe coming decades (transient climate conditions).
14 Transient climate projections reveal observable differences between 1.5°C and 2°C global warming in terms of mean temperature and\nextremes, both at a global scale and for most land regions. Such studies also reveal detectable differences between 1.5°C and 2°C\nprecipitation extremes in many land regions. For mean precipitation and various drought measures there is substantially lower risk for\nhuman systems and ecosystems in the Mediterranean region at 1.5°C compared to 2°C.
15 The different pathways to a 1.5°C warmer world may involve a transition through 1.5°C, with both short- and long-term stabilization\n(without overshoot), or a temporary rise and fall over decades and centuries (overshoot). The influence of these pathways is small for\nsome climate variables at the regional scale (e.g., regional temperature and precipitation extremes) but can be very large for others\n(e.g., sea level).
