13

14

15

16

17

18

19

20

21

Despite their efforts in deploying renewable energies, many developing countries are still contending
23
with problems related to the immaturity of the current technologies and the challenges of battery
24
storage. In short, the transition to a low carbon development must consider the challenges of renewable
25
energy penetration and existing energy-related vulnerabilities and inequalities. There are power
26
asymmetries between first-comers and latecomers, especially in cases where mature technologies can
27
be located in countries with less stringent laws and standards. Carbon leakage has implications for just
28
transitions, as carbon-intensive industries can move their dirty industries to developing countries as a
29
way of outsourcing the production of carbon (Bos and Gupta 2019; UNU-INRA 2020, Denton et al,
30
2021). When the challenge of climate mitigation is transferred to developing countries in the form of
31
carbon leakage, the risks of carbon lock-in for developing countries are heightened (Bos and Gupta
32
2019).

Overcoming the carbon lock-in is not simply a matter of the right policies or switching to low-carbon
34
technologies. Indeed, it would mean a radical change in the existing power relations between fossil-fuel
35
industries and their governments and social structural behaviour (Seto et al. 2016). Some actions to fix
36
the climate change problem can themselves create injustices, thereby challenging sustainable
37
development (Cronin et al. 2021). Not paying sufficient attention to perceptions of injustice related to
38
the rights to development, energy and resource sovereignty can further create resistance to climate
39
action (Cronin et al. 2021).
