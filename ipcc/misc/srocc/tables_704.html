4 Sustainable land management (SLM) makes strong social and economic sense. Early action in implementing SLM for climate change\nadaptation and mitigation provides distinct societal advantages. Understanding the full scope of what is at stake from climate change\npresents challenges because of inadequate accounting of the degree and scale at which climate change and land interactions impact\nsociety, and the importance society places on those impacts (Santos et al. 2016) (Sections 7.2.2, 5.3.1, 5.3.2 and 4.1). The consequences\nof inaction and delay bring significant risks, including irreversible change and loss in land ecosystem services (ES) – including food\nsecurity – with potentially substantial economic damage to many countries in many regions of the world (high confidence).
5 This cross-chapter box brings together the salient economic concepts underpinning the assessments of SLM and mitigation options\npresented in this report. Four critical concepts are required to help assess the social and economic implications of land-based climate\naction:
6 i. Value to society\nii. Damages from climate and land-induced interventions on land ecosystems\niii. Costs of action and inaction\niv. Decision-making under uncertainty
7 i. Value to society
8 Healthy functioning land and ecosystems are essential for human health, food and livelihood security. Land derives its value to
9 humans from being a finite resource and vital for life, providing important ES from water recycling, food, feed, fuel, biodiversity\nand carbon storage and sequestration.
10 Many of these ES may be difficult to estimate in monetary terms, including when they hold high symbolic value, linked to ancestral\nhistory, or traditional and indigenous knowledge systems (Boillat and Berkes 2013). Such incommensurable values of land are core to\nsocial cohesion – social norms and institutions, trust that enables all interactions, and sense of community.

12 ii. Damages from climate and land-induced interventions on land ecosystems
13 Values of many land-based ES and their potential loss under land–climate change interaction can be considerable: in 2011, the global
14 value of ES was 125 trillion USD per year and the annual loss due to land-use change was between 4.3 and 20.2 trillion USD per\nyear from 2007 (Costanza et al. 2014; Rockström et al. 2009). The annual costs of land degradation are estimated to be about\n231 billion USD per year or about 0.41% of the global GDP of 56.49 trillion USD in 2007 (Nkonya et al. 2016) (Sections 4.4.1 and 4.4.2).
