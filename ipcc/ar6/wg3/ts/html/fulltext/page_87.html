TS. 5.6 Agriculture, forestry and other land uses, and food systems 1
TS. 5.6.1 AFOLU 2
The Agriculture, Forestry and Other Land Uses (AFOLU)19sector encompasses managed 3 ecosystems and offers significant mitigation opportunities while providing food, wood and other 4 renewable resources as well as biodiversity conservation, provided the sector adapts to climate 5 change. Land-based mitigation measures can reduce GHG emissions within the AFOLU sector, deliver 6 CDR and provide biomass thereby enabling emission reductions in other sectors.20The rapid 7 deployment of AFOLU measures features in all pathways that l limit global warming to 1.5°C. Where 8 carefully and appropriately implemented, AFOLU mitigation measures are positioned to deliver 9 substantial co-benefits and help address many of the wider challenges associated with land 10 management. If AFOLU measures are deployed badly then, when taken together with the increasing 11 need to produce sufficient food, feed, fuel and wood, they may exacerbate trade-offs with the 12 conservation of habitats, adaptation, biodiversity and other services. At the same time the capacity of 13 the land to support these functions may be threatened by climate change. (high confidence) {WG I 14 Figure SPM7; WG II, 7.1, 7.6} 15
The AFOLU sector, on average, accounted for 13-21% of global total anthropogenic GHG 16 emissions in the period 2010-2019. At the same time managed and natural terrestrial ecosystems 17 were a carbon sink, absorbing around one third of anthropogenic CO2 emissions (medium 18 confidence). Estimated anthropogenic net CO2 emissions from AFOLU (based on bookkeeping models) 19 result in a net source of +5.9 ± 4.1 GtCO2 yr-1between 2010 and 2019 with an unclear trend. Based on 20 FAOSTAT or national GHG inventories, the net CO2 emissions from AFOLU were 0.0 to +0.8 GtCO2 21 yr-1over the same period. There is a discrepancy in the reported CO2 AFOLU emissions magnitude 22 because alternative methodological approaches that incorporate different assumptions are used {7.2.2}. 23 If the responses of all managed and natural land to both anthropogenic environmental change and 24 natural climate variability, estimated to be a gross sink of -12.5 ± 3.2 GtCO2 yr-1for the period 2010–25 2019, are added to land-use emissions, then land overall constituted a net sink of -6.6 ± 5.2 GtCO2 yr-126 in terms of CO2 emissions (medium confidence). Land use change drives net AFOLU CO2 emission fluxes. The rate of deforestation, which 28 accounts for 45% of total AFOLU emissions, has generally declined, while global tree cover and 29 global forest growing stock levels are likely increasing (medium confidence). There are substantial 30 regional differences, with losses of carbon generally observed in tropical regions and gains in temperate 31 and boreal regions. Agricultural CH4 and N2O emissions are estimated to average 157 ± 47.1 MtCH4 32 yr-1and 6.6 ± 4.0 MtN2O yr-1or 4.2 ± 1.3 and 1.8 ± 1.1 GtCO2-eq yr-1(using IPCC AR6 GWP100 33 values for CH4 and N2O) respectively between 2010 and 2019 {7.2.1, 7.2.3}. FOOTNOTE 19AFOLU is a sector in the 2019 Refinement to the 2006 IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories. AFOLU anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions and removals by sinks reported by governments under the UNFCCC are defined as all those occurring on ‘managed land’. Managed land is land where human interventions and practices have been applied to perform production, ecological or social functions.
FOOTNOTE 20For example: in the 2019 Refinement to the 2006 IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories, CO2emissions from biomass used for energy are reported in the AFOLU sector, calculated as an implicit component of carbon stock changes. In the energy sector, CO2emissions from biomass combustion for energy are recorded as an information item that is not included in the sectoral total emissions for the that sector.
