The Working Group III (WG III) contribution to the IPCC’s Sixth Assessment Report (AR6) assesses 2 the current state of knowledge on the scientific, technological, environmental, economic and social 3 aspects of climate change mitigation. It builds on previous IPCC reports, including the WG III 4 contribution to the IPCC’s Fifth Assessment Report (AR5) and the three Special Reports of the Sixth 5 Assessment cycle on: Global warming of 1.5°C (SR 1.5°C); Climate Change and Land (SRCCL); and, 6 the Ocean and Cryosphere in a Changing Climate (SROCC).17
The report assesses new literature, methodological and recent developments, and changes in approaches 8 towards climate change mitigation since the IPCC AR5 report was published in 2014. 9
The global science and policy landscape on climate change mitigation has evolved since AR5. The 10 development of the literature reflects, among other factors, the UN Framework Convention on Climate 11 Change (UNFCCC), the outcomes of its Kyoto Protocol and the goals of the Paris Agreement {13, 14, 12 15}, and the UN 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development {1, 4, 17}. Literature further highlights the 13 growing role of non-state and sub-national actors in the global effort to address climate change, 14 including cities, businesses, citizens, transnational initiatives and public-private entities {5, 8, 13}. It 15 draws attention to the decreasing cost of some low emission technologies {2, 6, 12} and the evolving 16 role of international cooperation {14}, finance {15} and innovation {16}. Emerging literature examines 17 the global spread of climate policies, strengthened mitigation actions in developing countries, sustained 18 reductions in greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in some developed countries and the continuing 19 challenges for mitigation. {2, 13} 20
There are ever closer linkages between climate change mitigation, development pathways and the 21 pursuit of sustainable development goals. Development pathways largely drive GHG emissions and 22 hence shape the mitigation challenge and the portfolio of available responses {4}. The co-benefits and 23 risks of mitigation responses also differ according to stages of development and national capabilities 24 {1, 2, 3, 4, 13}. Climate change mitigation framed in the context of sustainable development, equity, 25 and poverty eradication, and rooted in the development aspirations of the society within which they 26 take place, will be more acceptable, durable and effective. {1, 4, 17} 27
This report includes new assessment approaches that go beyond those evaluated in the previous IPCC 28 WG III reports. In addition to sectoral and systems chapters {6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11}, this report includes, for 29 the first time, chapters dedicated to cross-sectoral perspectives {12} demand, services and social aspects 30 of mitigation (Box TS.11) {5} and innovation, technology development and transfer {16}. The 31 assessment of future pathways combines a forward-looking assessment of near- to medium-term 32 perspectives up to 2050, including ways of shifting development pathways towards sustainability {4}, 33 with an assessment of long-term outcome-oriented pathways up to 2100 {3}. Collaboration between 34 the IPCC Working Groups is reflected in Cross-Working Group boxes which address topics such as the 35 economic benefits from avoided impacts along mitigation pathways {Cross-Working Group Box 1 in 36 Chapter 3}, climate change and urban areas {Cross-Working Group Box 2 in Chapter 8}, mitigation 37 and adaptation through the bioeconomy {Cross-Working Group Box 3 in Chapter 12} and Solar 38 Radiation Modification {Cross-Working Group Box 4 in Chapter 14}.
