A social science perspective is important in two ways. By adding new actors and perspectives, it (i) 6 provides more options for climate mitigation; and (ii) helps to identify and address important social and 7 cultural barriers and opportunities to socioeconomic, technological, and institutional change. Demand-8 side mitigation involves five sets of social actors: individuals (e.g., consumption choices, habits), 9 groups and collectives (e.g., social movements, values), corporate actors (e.g., investments, 10 advertising), institutions (e.g., political agency, regulations), and infrastructure actors (e.g., very long-11 term investments and financing). Actors either contribute to the status-quo of a global high-carbon, 12 consumption, and GDP growth-oriented economy, or help generate the desired change to a low-carbon 13 energy-services, well-being, and equity-oriented economy. Each set of actors has novel implications for 14 the design and implementation of both demand- and supply-side mitigation policies. They show 15 important synergies, making energy demand mitigation a dynamic problem where the packaging and/or 16 sequencing of different policies play a role in their effectiveness {5.5, 5.6}. Incremental interventions 17 change social practices, simultaneously affecting emissions and well-being. The transformative change 18 requires coordinated action across all five sets of actors (Table 5.4), using social science insights about 19 intersection of behaviour, culture, institutional and infrastructural changes for policy design and 20 implementation. Avoid, Shift, and Improve choices by individuals, households and communities support 21 mitigation {5.3.1.1, Table 5.1}. They are instigated by role models, changing social norms driven by 22 policies and social movements. They also require appropriate infrastructures designed by urban 23 planners and building and transport professionals, corresponding investments, and a political culture 24 supportive of demand side mitigation action. 25
