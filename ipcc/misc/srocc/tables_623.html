1 Increased food\nproductivity These estimates assume that increased food production is implemented sustainably (e.g., through sustainable\nintensification: Garnett et al. 2013; Pretty et al. 2018) rather than through increasing external inputs, which can have\na range of negative impacts. Mitigation: Large benefits (Table 6.13). Adaptation: Large benefits (Campbell et al. 2014)\n(Chapter 2 and Table 6.21). Desertification: Large benefits (Dai 20100 (Chapter 3 and Table 6.29). Land degradation:\nLarge benefits (Clay et al. 1995) (Chapter 4 and Table 6.37). Food security: Large benefits (Godfray et al. 2010;\nGodfray and Garnett 2014; Tilman et al. 2011) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.45).
2 Improved cropland\nmanagement Mitigation: Moderate benefits by reducing GHG emissions and creating soil carbon sinks (Smith et al. 2008, 2014) (Chapter 2\nand Table 6.13). Adaptation: Large benefits by improving the resilience of food crop production systems to future climate\nchange (Porter et al. 2014) (Chapter 2 and Table 6.21). Desertification: Large benefits by improving sustainable use of land\nin dry areas (Bryan et al. 2009; Chen et al. 2010) (Chapter 3 and Table 6.29). Land degradation: Large benefits by forming\na major component of sustainable land management (Labrière et al. 2015) (Chapter 4 and Table 6.37). Food security: Large\nbenefits by improving agricultural productivity for food production (Porter et al. 2014) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.45).
3 Improved grazing\nland management Mitigation: Moderate benefits by increasing soil carbon sinks and reducing GHG emissions (Herrero et al. 2016) (Chapter 2\nand Table 6.13). Adaptation: Moderate benefits by improving the resilience of grazing lands to future climate change\n(Porter et al. 2014) (Chapter 2 and Table 6.21). Desertification: Moderate benefits by tackling overgrazing in dry areas\nto reduce desertification (Archer et al. 2011) (Chapter 3 and Table 6.29). Land degradation: Large benefits by optimising\nstocking density to reduce land degradation (Tighe et al. 2012) (Chapter 4, Table 6.37 and Table 6.45). Food security: Large\nbenefits by improving livestock sector productivity to increase food production (Herrero et al. 2016) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.45).
4 Improved livestock\nmanagement Mitigation: Moderate benefits by reducing GHG emissions, particularly from enteric methane and manure management\n(Smith et al. 2008, 2014) (Chapter 2 and Table 6.13). Adaptation: Moderate benefits by improving resilience of livestock\nproduction systems to climate change (Porter et al. 2014) (Chapter 2 and Table 6.21). Desertification: Moderate benefits\nby tackling overgrazing in dry areas (Archer et al. 2011) (Chapter 3 and Table 6.29). Land degradation: Large benefits\nby reducing overstocking which can reduce land degradation (Tighe et al. 2012) (Chapter 4, Table 6.37 and Table 6.45).\nFood security: Large benefits by improving livestock sector productivity to increase food production (Herrero et al. 2016)\n(Chapter 5 and Table 6.45).
5 Agroforestry Mitigation: Large benefits by increasing carbon sinks in vegetation and soils (Delgado 2010; Mbow et al. 2014a;\nGriscom et al. 2017) (Chapter 2 and Table 6.13). Adaptation: Large benefits by improving the resilience of agricultural\nlands to climate change (Mbow et al. 2014a) (Chapter 2 and Table 6.21). Desertification: Large benefits through,\nfor example, providing perennial vegetation in dry areas (Nair et al. 2010; Lal 2001) (Chapter 3 and Table 6.29).\nLand degradation: Large benefits by stabilising soils through perennial vegetation (Narain et al. 1997; Lal 2001)\n(Chapter 4 and Table 6.37). Food production: Large benefits since well-planned agroforestry can enhance productivity\n(Bustamante et al. 2014; Sascha et al. 2017) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.45).
6 Agricultural\ndiversification Agricultural diversification is a collection of practices aimed at deriving more crops or products per unit of area\n(e.g., intercropping) or unit of time (e.g., double cropping, ratoon crops, etc.). Mitigation: Limited benefits (Table 6.13).\nAdaptation: Large benefits through improved household income (Pellegrini and Tasciotti 2014) (Table 6.21).\nDesertification: Moderate benefits, limited by global dryland cropped area (Table 6.29). Land degradation: Large benefits\nby reducing pressure on land (Lambin and Meyfroidt 2011) (Table 6.37). Food security: Large benefits for food security by\nprovision of more diverse foods (Birthal et al. 2015; Massawe et al. 2016; Waha et al. 2018) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.45).
7 Reduced grassland\nconversion to\ncropland ND Mitigation: Moderate benefits by retaining soil carbon stocks that might otherwise be lost. Historical losses of soil\ncarbon have been in the order of 500 GtCO2 (Sanderman et al. 2017) (Table 6.13). Mean annual global cropland conver-\nsion rates (1961–2003) have been 0.36% per year (Krause et al. 2017), that is, around 47,000 km2 yr–1 – so preventing\nconversion could potentially save moderate emissions of CO2. Adaptation: No literature (Table 6.21). Desertification:\nLimited benefits by shifting from annual crops to permanent vegetation cover under grass in dry areas (Table 6.29)\n(Chapter 3). Land degradation: Limited benefits by shifting from annual crops to permanent vegetation cover under\ngrass (Chapter 4 and Table 6.37). Food security: Moderate negative impacts, since more land is required to produce\nhuman food from livestock products on grassland than from crops on cropland, meaning that a shift to grassland could\nreduce total productivity and threaten food security (Clark and Tilman 2017) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.45).
8 Integrated water\nmanagement Mitigation: Moderate benefits by reducing GHG emissions mainly in cropland and rice cultivation (Smith et al. 2008, 2014)\n(Chapter 2 and Table 6.13). Adaptation: Large benefits by improving the resilience of food crop production systems to\nfuture climate change (Porter et al. 2014) (Chapter 2 and Table 6.21). Desertification: Limited benefits by improving sus-\ntainable use of land in dry areas (Chapter 3 and Table 6.29). Land degradation: Limited benefits by forming a major com-\nponent of sustainable land and water management (Chapter 4 and Table 6.37). Food security: Large benefits by improving\nagricultural productivity for food production (Godfray and Garnett 2014; Tilman et al. 2011) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.45).
