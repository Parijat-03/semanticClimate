Evidence of rapid energy transitions exists in some case studies (medium confidence). Emerging 3 evidence since AR5 on past energy transitions identifies a growing number of cases of accelerated 4 technology diffusion at sub-global scales and describes mechanisms by which future energy transitions 5 may occur more quickly than those in the past. Important drivers include technology transfer and 6 cooperation, international policy and financial support, and harnessing synergies among technologies 7 within a sustainable energy system perspective (medium confidence). A fast global low-carbon energy 8 transition enabled by finance to facilitate low-carbon technology adoption in developing and 9 particularly in least developed countries can facilitate achieving climate stabilisation targets (high 10 confidence). {2.5.2, Table 2.5} 11
Multiple low-carbon technologies have shown rapid progress since AR5 – in cost, performance, 12 and adoption – enhancing the feasibility of rapid energy transitions (high confidence). The rapid 13 deployment and unit cost decrease of modular technologies like solar, wind, and batteries have occurred 14 much faster than anticipated by experts and modelled in previous mitigation scenarios, as shown in 15 Figure TS.7 (high confidence). The political, economic, social, and technical feasibility of solar energy, 16 wind energy and electricity storage technologies has improved dramatically over the past few years. In 17 contrast, the adoption of nuclear energy and CO2 capture and storage (CCS) in the electricity sector has 18 been slower than the growth rates anticipated in stabilisation scenarios. Emerging evidence since AR5 19 indicates that small-scale technologies (e.g., solar, batteries) tend to improve faster and be adopted more 20 quickly than large-scale technologies (nuclear, CCS) (medium confidence).
