Agriculture, food and land-use is the sector where most climate policy options can simultaneously
10
generate impacts on mitigation, adaptation and the SDGs (Locatelli et al. 2015; Kongsager et al. 2016).
11
Bryan et al. (2013) identified a range of synergies and trade-offs across adaptation, mitigation and the
12
SDGs in Kenya, given the diversity of its climatic and ecological conditions. Improved management of
13
soil fertility and improved livestock-feeding practices could provide benefits to both climate-change
14
mitigation and adaptation, as well as increase income generation from farming. However, other
15
improvements to agricultural management in Kenya, for example, soil water conservation, could only
16
provide benefits across all three domains in some specific sub-regions.

Conservation agriculture can yield mitigation co-benefits through improved fertiliser use or the efficient
19
use of machinery and fossil fuels (Cui et al. 2019; Harvey et al. 2014; Pradhan et al. 2018). Climate-
20
smart agriculture (CSA) ties mitigation to adaptation through its three pillars of increased productivity,
21
mitigation and adaptation (Lipper et al. 2014), although managing trade-offs among the three pillars
22
requires care (Kongsager et al. 2016; Thornton and Comberti 2017; Soussana et al. 2019). Sustainable
23
intensification also complements CSA (Campbell et al. 2014). Enhanced sustainable adaption can lead
24
to effective emission-reduction benefits, such as climate-smart agricultural technologies (Nefzaoui et
25
al. 2012; Poudel 2014) and ecosystem-based adaptation. Berry, P et al. (2015); Geneletti and Zardo
26
(2016); and Warmenbol and Smith (2018) have shown how increases in livelihoods can contribute to
27
climate change mitigation in Europe.

Agroforestry can sustain or increase food production in some systems and increase farmers’ resilience
30
to climate change (Jones et al. 2013). Some sustainable agricultural practices have trade-offs, and their
31
implementation can have negative effects on adaptation or other ecosystem services. Agricultural
32
practices can aid both mitigation and adaptation on the ground, but yields may be lower, so there may
33
be a trade-off between resilience to climate change and efficiency. Interconnections within the global
34
agricultural system may also lead to deforestation elsewhere (Erb et al. 2016). Implementation of
35
sustainable agriculture can increase or decrease yields, depending on context (Pretty et al. 2006)
36
(Chapter 4).

Land-based mitigation and adaptation will not only help reduce greenhouse gas emissions in the
39
AFOLU sector, but also help augment the sector’s role as a carbon sink by increasing forest and tree
40
cover through afforestation and agroforestry activities and other eco-system-based approaches. Some
41
of these options, however, can also have negative impacts on GHG emissions in the form of indirect
42
impacts on land use (for a more detailed discussion, see Chapter 7). If managed and regulated
43
appropriately, the land use, land-use change and forestry (LULUCF) sector could play a key role in
44
mitigation and be a key sector for emissions reductions beyond 2025 instead of contributing
45
substantially to emissions reductions beyond 2025 ( Keramidas et al. 2018). However, the large-scale
46
deployment of intensive bioenergy plantations, including monocultures, replacing natural forests and
47
subsistence farmlands are likely to have negative impacts on biodiversity and can threaten food and
48
water security, as well as local livelihoods, partly by intensifying social conflicts, partly by reducing
49
resilience (Díaz et al. 2019). Expansion on to abandoned or unused croplands and pastures nonetheless
50
present significant global potential, and will avoid the sustainability risks of expanding agriculture into
51
natural vegetation (Næss et al. 2021).
