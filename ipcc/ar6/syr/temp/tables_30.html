3 Socio-economic Development, Scenarios, and Pathways
4 The five Shared Socio-economic Pathways (SSP1 to SSP5) were designed to span a range of challenges to
5 climate change mitigation and adaptation. For the assessment of climate impacts, risk and adaptation, the SSPs
6 are used for future exposure, vulnerability and challenges to adaptation. Depending on levels of GHG
7 mitigation, modelled emissions scenarios based on the SSPs can be consistent with low or high warming
8 levels47. There are many different mitigation strategies that could be consistent with different levels of global
9 warming in 2100 (see Figure 4.1). {WGI Box SPM.1; WGII Box SPM.1; WGIII Box SPM.1, WGIII Box
10 TS.5, WGIII Annex III; SRCCL Box SPM.1, Figure SPM.2}

12 WGI assessed the climate response to five illustrative scenarios based on SSPs48 that cover the range of
13 possible future development of anthropogenic drivers of climate change found in the literature. These scenarios
14 combine socio-economic assumptions, levels of climate mitigation, land use and air pollution controls for
15 aerosols and non-CH ozone precursors. The high and very high GHG emissions scenarios (SSP3-7.0 and\n4
16 SSP5-8.5) have CO emissions that roughly double from current levels by 2100 and 2050, respectively49. The\n2
17 intermediate GHG emissions scenario (SSP2-4.5) has CO emissions remaining around current levels until the\n2
18 middle of the century. The very low and low GHG emissions scenarios (SSP1-1.9 and SSP1-2.6) have CO\n2
19 emissions declining to net zero around 2050 and 2070, respectively, followed by varying levels of net negative
20 CO emissions. In addition, Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs)50 were used by WGI and WGII to\n2
21 assess regional climate changes, impacts and risks. {WGI Box SPM.1} (Cross-Section Box.2, Figure 1)

23 In WGIII, a large number of global modelled emissions pathways were assessed, of which 1202 pathways
24 were categorised based on their projected global warming over the 21st century, with categories ranging from
25 pathways that limit warming to 1.5°C with more than 50% likelihood51 with no or limited overshoot (C1) to
26 pathways that exceed 4°C (C8). Methods to project global warming associated with the modelled pathways
27 were updated to ensure consistency with the AR6 WGI assessment of the climate system response52. {WGIII
28 Box SPM.1, WGIII Table 3.1} (Table 3.1, Cross-Section Box.2, Figure 1)

30 Global Warming Levels (GWLs)
31 For many climate and risk variables, the geographical patterns of changes in climatic impact-drivers53 and
32 climate impacts for a level of global warming54 are common to all scenarios considered and independent of
33 timing when that level is reached. This motivates the use of GWLs as a dimension of integration. {WGI Box
