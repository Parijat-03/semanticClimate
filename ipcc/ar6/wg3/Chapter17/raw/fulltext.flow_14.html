.2.3 Psychology, Individual Beliefs and Social Change
22
This subsection draws on value- and action-oriented research that employs inter- or transdisciplinary
23
methods such as transactional psychology, transformative science and similarly focused disciplines
24
(Wamsler et al. 2021). These approaches frequently encourage researchers to participate in transitions
25
that induce changes in the researcher’s own beliefs while triggering wider shifts in social norms
26
(including human stewardship for the natural environment) (Adger et al. 2013; Hulme 2009; Ives et al.
27
2019; O’Brien 2018). This research also emphasizes how changes in individual beliefs could lead to
28
climate actions that contribute to more sustainable, equitable and just societies (see e.g. “the mind- &
29
paradigm shifts” (Göpel et al. 2016; Meadows 2008). They further suggest the potential for virtuous
30
cycles of individual-level and wider social changes that ultimately benefit the climate (Banks 2007;
31
Day et al. 2014; Lockhart 2011; Montuori and Donnelly 2018; Power 2016).
32
33
The starting point for this virtuous circle are inner transitions. Inner transitions occur within individuals,
34
organizations and even larger jurisdictions that alter beliefs and actions involving climate change
35
(Woiwode et al. 2021). An inner transition within an individual (see e.g., Parodi and Tamm 2018)
36
typically involves a person gaining a deepening sense of peace and a willingness to help others, as well
37
as protecting the climate and the planet (see e.g., Banks 2007; Power 2016). Inner transition can imply
38
that individuals become sympathetic to concerns that include climate issues and values connected to
39
nature. For instance, they may include a desire to become a steward of nature (Buijs et al. 2018), “live
40
according to the principles of integrated sustainability” (Schweizer-Ries 2018), “achieve the good life”
41
(see Section 1.6.2 in Chapter 1; Asara et al. 2015; Escobar 2015; Kallis 2017; Latouche 2018; Chapter
42
5) or protect the well-being of other living creatures (Section 1.6.3.1 in Chapter 1 and Chapter 5).
43
44
Examples have also been seen in relation to a similar set of inner transitions to individuals, organizations
45
and societies, which involves embracing post-development, de-growth, or non-material values that
46
challenge carbon-intensive lifestyles and development models (Kothari 2019; Neuteleers and Engelen
47
2015; Paech 2017; Sklair 2016). These shifts in values can occur when humans reconnect with nature,
48
deepen their consciousness and take responsibility for protecting the planet and its climate (Cross et al.
49
2019; Martinez-Juarez et al. 2015; Speldewinde et al. 2015). Changes in both values and beliefs may
50
also emerge through consciousness-raising processes where people cooperate in ways that would
51
protect the climate (see Section 1.6.4 in Chapter 1; Banks 2007; Hedlund-de Witt et al. 2014; Woiwode
52
and Woiwode 2019).
