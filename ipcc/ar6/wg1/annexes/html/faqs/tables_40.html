2 FAQ 7.1 | What Is the Earth’s Energy Budget, and What Does It Tell Us About Climate Change?
3
4 The Earth’s energy budget describes the flow of energy within the climate system. Since at least 1970 there\nhas been a persistent imbalance in the energy flows that has led to excess energy being absorbed by the\nclimate system. By measuring and understanding these energy flows and the role that human activities play in\nchanging them, we are better able to understand the causes of climate change and project future climate change
5 more accurately.\nOur planet receives vast amounts of energy every day in the form of sunlight. Around a third of the sunlight is
6 reflected back to space by clouds, by tiny particles called aerosols, and by bright surfaces such as snow and ice.\nThe rest is absorbed by the ocean, land, ice and atmosphere. The planet then emits energy back out to space in\nthe form of thermal radiation. In a world that was not warming or cooling, these energy flows would balance.
7 Human activity has caused an imbalance in these energy flows.\nWe measure the influence of various human and natural factors on the energy flows at the top of our atmosphere
8 in terms of radiative forcings, where a positive radiative forcing has a warming effect and a negative radiative\nforcing has a cooling effect. In response to these forcings, the Earth system will either warm or cool, so as to\nrestore balance through changes in the amount of outgoing thermal radiation (the warmer the Earth, the more\nradiation it emits). Changes in Earth’s temperature in turn lead to additional changes in the climate system\n(known as climate feedbacks) that either amplify or dampen the original effect. For example, Arctic sea ice has\nbeen melting as the Earth warms, reducing the amount of reflected sunlight and adding to the initial warming\n(an amplifying feedback). The most uncertain of those climate feedbacks are clouds, as they respond to warming in\ncomplex ways that affect both the emission of thermal radiation and the reflection of sunlight. However, we are
9 now more confident that cloud changes, taken together, will amplify climate warming (see FAQ 7.2).\nHuman activities have unbalanced these energy flows in two main ways. First, increases in greenhouse gas levels
10 have led to more of the emitted thermal radiation being absorbed by the atmosphere, instead of being released to\nspace. Second, increases in pollutants have increased the amount of aerosols such as sulphates in the atmosphere\n(see FAQ 6.1). This has led to more incoming sunlight being reflected away, by the aerosols themselves and
11 through the formation of more cloud drops, which increases the reflectivity of clouds (see FAQ 7.2).\nAltogether, the global energy flow imbalance since the 1970s has been just over half a watt per square metre
12 of the Earth’s surface. This sounds small, but because the imbalance is persistent and because Earth’s surface is\nlarge, this adds up to about 25 times the total amount of primary energy consumed by human society, compared\nover 1971 to 2018. Compared to the IPCC Fifth Assessment Report (AR5), we are now better able to quantify\nand track these energy flows from multiple lines of evidence, including satellite data, direct measurements of\nocean temperatures, and a wide variety of other Earth system observations (see FAQ 1.1). We also have a better\nunderstanding of the processes contributing to this imbalance, including the complex interactions between
13 aerosols, clouds and radiation.\nResearch has shown that the excess energy since the 1970s has mainly gone into warming the ocean (91%),
14 followed by the warming of land (5%) and the melting of ice sheets and glaciers (3%). The atmosphere has\nwarmed substantially since 1970, but because it is comprised of thin gases it has absorbed only 1% of the excess\nenergy (FAQ 7.1, Figure 1). As the ocean has absorbed the vast majority of the excess energy, especially within\nits top two kilometres, the deep ocean is expected to continue to warm and expand for centuries to millennia,\nleading to long-term sea level rise – even if atmospheric greenhouse gas levels were to decline (see FAQ 5.3).
15 This is in addition to the sea level rise expected from melting ice sheets and glaciers.\nUnderstanding the Earth’s energy budget al.o helps to narrow uncertainty in future projections of climate.
16 By testing climate models against what we know about the Earth’s energy budget, we can make more confident\nprojections of surface temperature changes we might expect this century and beyond.
