TS. 5.8 Demand-side aspects of mitigation 1
The assessment of the social science literature and regional case studies reveals how social norms, 2 culture, and individual choices interact with infrastructure and other structural changes over time. This 3 provides new insight into climate change mitigation strategies, and how economic and social activity 4 might be organised across sectors to support emission reductions. To enhance well-being, people 5 demand services and not primary energy and physical resources per se. Focusing on demand for services 6 and the different social and political roles people play broadens the participation in climate action. (Box 7 TS.11) 8
Demand-side mitigation and new ways of providing services can help Avoid and Shift final service 9 demands and Improve service delivery. Rapid and deep changes in demand make it easier for 10 every sector to reduce GHG emissions in the short and medium term (high confidence). {5.2, 5.3} 11
The indicative potential of demand-side strategies across all sectors to reduce emissions is 40-70% 12 by 2050 (high confidence). Technical mitigation potentials compared to the International Energy 13 Agency’s 2020 World Energy Outlook STEPS (Stated Policy Scenarios) baseline are up to 5.7 GtCO2-eq for 14 building use and construction, 8 GtCO2-eq for food demand, 6.5 GtCO2-eq for land transport, and 5.2 15 GtCO2-eq for industry. Mitigation strategies can be classified as Avoid-Shift-Improve (ASI) options, 16 that reflect opportunities for socio-cultural, infrastructural, and technological change. The greatest 17 Avoid potential comes from reducing long-haul aviation and providing short-distance low-carbon urban 18 infrastructures. The greatest Shift potential would come from switching to plant-based diets. The 19 greatest Improve potential comes from within the building sector, and in particular increased use of 20 energy efficient end-use technologies and passive housing. Socio-cultural and lifestyle changes can accelerate climate change mitigation (medium 23 confidence). Among 60 identified actions that could change individual consumption, individual 24 mobility choices have the largest potential to reduce carbon footprints. Prioritising car-free mobility by 25 walking and cycling and adoption of electric mobility could save 2 tCO2-eq cap-1 yr-1. Other options 26 with high mitigation potential include reducing air travel, cooling setpoint adjustments, reduced 27 appliance use, shifts to public transit, and shifting consumption towards plant-based diets. {5.3.1, 28 5.3.1.2, Figure 5.8} 29

The scope, theories, and evidence for such an 35 assessment are addressed in Sections 5.1 and 5.4 within Chapter 5 and a Social Science Primer as an 36 Appendix to Chapter 5. 37
The literature on social science – from sociology, psychology, gender studies and political science for 38 example – and climate change mitigation is growing rapidly. A bibliometric search of the literature 39 identified 99,065 peer-reviewed academic papers, based on 34 search queries with content relevant to 40 Chapter 5. This literature is expanding by 15% per year, with twice as many publications in the AR6 41 period (2014-2020) as in all previous years. 42
