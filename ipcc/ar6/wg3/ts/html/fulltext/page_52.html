Moving to net negative CO2 emissions globally would reduce peak cumulative net CO2 emissions – 1 which occurs at the time of reaching net zero CO2 emissions – and lead to a peak and decline in CO2-2 induced warming. {Cross-Chapter Box 3 in Chapter 3} 3
Reaching net zero CO2 emissions sooner can reduce cumulative CO2 emissions and result in less human-4 induced global warming. Overall human-induced warming depends not only on CO2 emissions but also 5 on the contribution from other anthropogenic climate forcers, including aerosols and other GHGs (e.g. 6 CH4 and F-gases). To halt total human-induced warming, emissions of other GHG, in particular CH4, 7 need to be strongly reduced. 8
In the AR6 scenario database, global emissions pathways limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited 9 overshoot reach net zero CO2 emissions between 2055-2060 (2035-2090) (median and 5-95th percentile 10 ranges; 100% of pathways); pathways likely to limit warming to 2°C reach net zero CO2 emissions 11 between 2070-2075 (2055-2100) (median and 5-95th percentile ranges; 90% of pathways). This is later 12 than assessed in the AR6 SR1.5 primarily due to more pathways in the literature that approach net-zero 13 CO2 emissions more gradually after a rapid decline of emissions until 2040. It does not mean that the world has more time for emissions reductions while still limiting warming to 15 1.5C than reported in the SR15. It only means that the exact timing of reaching net zero CO2 after a 16 steep decline of CO2 emissions until 2040 can show some variation. The SR1.5 median value of 2050 17 is still close to the middle of the current range. If emissions are reduced less rapidly in the period up to 18 2030, an earlier net-zero year is needed. 19
Reaching net zero GHG emissions requires net negative CO2 emissions to balance residual CH4, N2O 20 and F-gas emissions. If achieved globally, net zero GHG emissions would reduce global warming from 21 an earlier peak. Around half global emission pathways limiting warming to 1.5°C, and a third of 22 pathways likely to limit warming to 2°C, reach net zero GHG emissions (based on GWP100) in the 23 second half of the century, around 10-40 years later than net zero CO2 emissions. They show warming 24 being halted at some peak value followed by a gradual decline towards the end of the century. The 25 remainder of the pathways do not reach net zero GHG emissions during the 21st century and show little 26 decline of warming after it stabilised. 27
Global net zero CO2 or GHG emissions can be achieved even while some sectors and regions continue 28 to be net emitters, provided that others achieve net GHG removal. Sectors and regions have different 29 potentials and costs to achieve net zero or even net GHG removal. The adoption and implementation of 30 net zero emission targets by countries and regions depends on multiple factors, including equity and 31 capacity criteria and international and cross-sectoral mechanisms to balance emissions and removals. 32 The formulation of net zero pathways by countries will benefit from clarity on scope, plans-of-action, 33 and fairness. Achieving net zero emission targets relies on policies, institutions and milestones against 34 which to track progress. 35
