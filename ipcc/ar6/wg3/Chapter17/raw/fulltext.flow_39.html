Within the WEFN, the implementation of policies to achieve low stabilisation targets is strongly linked
12
to sustainable development within the water sector with regard to water management and water
13
conservation, indicating that additional coherence in policies affecting the water, energy and food
14
sectors (among others) will be critical in achieving the SDGs see also Chapter 7). Subsidized fertilizers,
15
energy and crops can drive unsustainable levels of water usage and pollution in agriculture. More than
16
half the world’s population, roughly 4.3 billion people in 2016, live in areas where the demand for water
17
resources outstrips sustainable supplies for at least part of the year. Irrigated agriculture is already using
18
around 70% of the available freshwater, and the large seasonal variations in water supply and the needs
19
of different crops can create conflicts between water needs across sectors at different time scales (Wada
20
et al. 2016). However, as there is little potential for increasing irrigation or expanding cropland (Steffen
21
et al. 2015), gaps in food production gaps must be closed by increasing productivity and cropping
22
densities on currently harvested land by increasing either rain-fed yields or water-use efficiency
23
(Alexandratos and Bruinsma 2012).

It has been argued that applying an integrated approach to water-energy-climate-food resource
26
management and policy-making is highly beneficial in properly addressing the co-benefits and trade-
27
offs (Brouwer et al. 2018; Howells et al. 2013), accommodating the SDGs (Rasul 2016) and in general
28
assessing enabling strategies to improve resource efficiency (Dai et al. 2018). For an integrated
29
approach to analysing the WEFN, a number of modelling approaches, tools and frameworks have been
30
proposed (Brouwer et al. 2018; de Strasser et al. 2016; Gao et al. 2019; Larsen et al. 2019; Smajgl et al.
31
2016), often involving multi-objective calibration. Such tools enable decision-makers to evaluate the
32
optimal water-allocation and energy-saving solutions for the specific geography in question. As an
33
example, (Scott 2011) found the higher transportability of electricity, compared to water, pivotal in
34
water-energy adaptation solutions in the USA, while arguing for the additional coordination of water
35
and energy policies as a key instrument in balancing the trade-offs.

Common to all these integrated efforts is the challenge involved in making comparisons across studies
38
due to the combined complexities of assumptions, model codes, regions, variables, forcings etc. To
39
accommodate these challenges, (Larsen et al. 2019) suggest employing shared criteria and forcing data
40
to enable cross-model comparisons and uncertainty estimates, as also highlighted by (Brouwer et al.
41
2018). Other limitations in current WEFN research are partial system descriptions, the failure to address
42
uncertainties, system boundaries, and evaluation methods and metrics (Zhang et al. 2018b). The lack of
43
proper access to WEFN data and data quality has been highlighted by (D’Odorico et al. 2018; Larsen
44
et al. 2019). Furthermore, gaps have been identified between theory and end-user applications in the
45
lack of any focus on food nutritional values as opposed to calories alone, in the understanding of water
46
availability in relation to management practices, in integrating new energy technologies and in the
47
resulting environmental issues (D’Odorico et al. 2018).
