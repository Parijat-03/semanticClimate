Measures required to likely limit warming to 2°C or below can result in large scale transformation 3 of the land surface (high confidence). These pathways are projected to reach net zero CO2 emissions 4 in the AFOLU sector between the 2020s and 2070. 5
Pathways limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot show an increase in forest cover 6 of about 322 million ha (-67 to 890 million ha) in 2050 (high confidence). In these pathways the 7 cropland area to supply biomass for bioenergy (including bioenergy with carbon capture and storage 8 (BECCS)) is around 199 (56-482) million ha in 2100. The use of bioenergy can lead to either increased 9 or reduced emissions, depending on the scale of deployment, conversion technology, fuel displaced, 10 and how, and where, the biomass is produced (high confidence). {3.4} 11
Pathways likely to limit warming to 2°C or 1.5°C require some amount of CDR to compensate 12 for residual GHG emissions, even after substantial direct emissions reductions are achieved in all 13 sectors and regions (high confidence). CDR deployment in pathways serves multiple purposes: 14 accelerating the pace of emissions reductions, offsetting residual emissions, and creating the option for 15 net negative CO2 emissions in case temperature reductions need to be achieved in the long term (high 16 confidence). CDR options in pathways are mostly limited to BECCS, afforestation and direct air CO2 17 capture and storage (DACCS). CDR through some measures in AFOLU can be maintained for decades 18 but not over the very long term because these sinks will ultimately saturate (high confidence). {3.4} 19
Mitigation pathways show reductions in energy demand, relative to reference scenarios that 20 assume continuation of current policies, through a diverse set of demand-side interventions (high 21 confidence). Bottom-up and non-IAM studies show significant potential for demand-side mitigation. A 22 stronger emphasis on demand-side mitigation implies less dependence on CDR and, consequently, 23 reduced pressure on land and biodiversity. {3.4, 3.7} 24
Limiting warming requires shifting energy investments away from fossil-fuels and towards low-25 carbon technologies (high confidence). The bulk of investments are needed in medium- and low-26 income regions. Investment needs in the electricity sector are on average 2.3 trillion USD2015 yr-1over 27 2023-2052 for pathways limiting temperature to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot, and 1.7 trillion 28 USD2015 yr-1for pathways likely limiting warming to 2°C. {3.6.1} 29
Pathways likely to avoid overshoot of 2°C warming require more rapid near-term 30 transformations and are associated with higher up-front transition costs, but at the same time 31 bring long-term gains for the economy as well as earlier benefits in avoided climate change 32 impacts (high confidence). This conclusion is independent of the discount rate applied, though the 33 modelled cost-optimal balance of mitigation action over time does depend on the discount rate. Lower 34 discount rates favour earlier mitigation, reducing reliance on CDR and temperature overshoot. {3.6.1, 35 3.8} 36
