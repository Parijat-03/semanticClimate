TS. 6.5 Innovation, technology development and transfer 1
Innovation in climate mitigation technologies has seen enormous activity and significant progress 2 in recent years. Innovation has also led to, and exacerbated, trade-offs in relation to sustainable 3 development. Innovation can leverage action to mitigate climate change by reinforcing other 4 interventions. In conjunction with other enabling conditions innovation can support system transitions 5 to limit warming and help shift development pathways. The currently widespread implementation of 6 solar PV and LED lighting, for instance, could not have happened without technological innovation. 7 Technological innovation can also bring about new and improved ways of delivering services that are 8 essential to human well-being (high confidence) {16.1, 16.3, 16.4, 16.6}. At the same time as delivering 9 benefits, innovation can result in trade-offs that undermine both progress on mitigation and progress 10 towards other sustainable development goals. Trade-offs include negative externalities – for instance 11 greater environmental pollution and social inequalities – rebound effects leading to lower net emission 12 reductions or even increases in emissions, and increased dependency on foreign knowledge and 13 providers (high confidence). Effective governance and policy have the potential to avoid and minimise 14 such misalignments (medium evidence, high agreement). {16.2, 16.3, 16.4, 16.5.1, 16.6} 15
A systemic view of innovation to direct and organize the processes has grown over the last decade. 16 This systemic view of innovation takes into account the role of actors, institutions, and their 17 interactions and can inform how innovation systems that vary across technologies, sectors and 18 countries, can be strengthened (high confidence) {16.2, 16.3, 16.5}. Where a systemic view of 19 innovation has been taken, it has enabled the development and implementation of indicators that are 20 better able to provide insights in innovation processes. This, in turn, has enabled the analysis and 21 strengthening of innovation systems. Traditional quantitative innovation indicators mainly include 22 R&D investments and patents. Figure TS.26 illustrates that energy-related RD&D has risen slowly in 23 the last two decades, and that there has been a reorientation of the portfolio of funded energy 24 technologies. Systemic indicators of innovation, however, go well beyond these approaches. They 25 include structural innovation system elements including actors and networks, as well as indicators for 26 how innovation systems function, such as access to finance, employment in relevant sectors, and 27 lobbying activities {16.3.4, Table 16.7}. For example, in Latin America, monitoring systemic 28 innovation indicators for the effectiveness of agroecological mitigation approaches has provided 29 insights on the appropriateness and social alignment of new technologies and practices {Box 16.5}. 30 Climate-energy-economy models, including integrated assessment models, generally employ a stylised 31 and necessarily incomplete view of innovation, and have yet to incorporate a systemic representation 32 of innovation systems. {16.2.4, Box 16.1} 33
