2 FAQ CCP2.1 | Why are coastal cities and settlements by the sea especially at risk in a changing climate, and which
3 cities are most at risk?\nCoastal cities and settlements (C&S) by the sea face a much greater risk than comparable inland C&S because they concentrate a large
4 proportion of the global population and economic activity, whilst being exposed and vulnerable to a range of climate- and ocean-compounded\nhazard risks driven by climate change. Coastal C&S range from small settlements along waterways and estuaries, to small island states with\nmaritime populations and/or beaches and atolls that are major tourist attractions, large cities that are major transport and financial hubs in
5 coastal deltas, to megacities and even megaregions with several coastal megacities.\nThe concentration of people, economic activity and infrastructure dynamically interacts with coast-specific hazards to
6 magnify the exposure of these C&S to climate risks. While large inland cities and coastal settlements can be exposed\nto climate-driven hazards, such as urban heat islands and air pollution, the latter are also subject to distinctive\nocean-driven hazards, such as sea level rise (SLR), exposure to tropical cyclones and storm surges, flooding from\nextreme tides and land subsidence from decreased sediment deposition along coastal deltas and estuaries. With\nclimate change increasing, the intensity and frequency of hazards under all future warming levels and thus the risks
7 to lives, livelihoods and property are especially acute in C&S by the sea.\nCoastal cities are diverse in shape, size, growth patterns and trajectories, and in terms of access to cultural, financial
8 and ecosystem resources and services. Along deltaic and estuarine archetypes, cities most vulnerable to a changing\nclimate have relatively high levels of poverty and inequality in terms of access to resources and ecosystem services,
9 with large populations and dense built environments translating into higher exposure to coastal climate risks.\nThese climate risks at the coast can also be magnified by compounding and cascading effects due to non-climate
10 drivers directly affecting vulnerable peri- and ex-urban areas inland. These risks include disruption to transport\nsupply chains and energy infrastructure from airports and power plants sited along the coastline, as occurred in\nNew York City, USA, during Hurricane Sandy in 2012. The impacts can be felt around the world through globalised
11 economic and geopolitical linkages, for example through maritime trade and port linkages.\nFor open coasts, settlements on low-lying small island states and the Arctic are especially vulnerable to climate
12 change, and SLR impacts in particular, well before 2100. While the economic risks may not compare to the scale\nof those faced in coastal megacities with high per capita GDP, the existential risks to some nations and an array of\ndistinctive livelihoods, cultural heritage and ways of life in these settlements are great, even with modest SLR.
