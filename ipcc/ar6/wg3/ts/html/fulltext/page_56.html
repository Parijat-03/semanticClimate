If investments in coal and other fossil infrastructure continue, energy systems will be locked-in to 17 higher emissions, making it harder to limit warming to 2°C or 1.5°C (high confidence). Box TS. 8: Stranded Assets 29
Limiting warming to 2°C or 1.5°C is expected to result in the “stranding” of carbon-intensive 30 assets. Stranded assets can be broadly defined as assets which “suffer from unanticipated or premature 31 write-offs, downward revaluations or conversion to liabilities”. Climate policies, other policies and 32 regulations, innovation in competing technologies, and shifts in fuel prices could all lead to stranded 33 assets. The loss of wealth from stranded assets would create risks for financial market 34 stability, reduce fiscal revenue for hydrocarbon dependent economies, in turn affecting macro-35 economic stability and the prospects for a just transition. (Box TS.4) {6.7, 15.6, Chapter 17} 36
Two types of assets are at risk of being stranded: i) in-ground fossil resources and ii) human-made 37 capital assets (e.g., power plants, cars). About 30% of oil, 50% of gas, and 80% of coal reserves will 38 remain unburnable if warming is limited to 2°C. {6.7, Box 6.11} 39
Practically all long-lived technologies and investments that cannot be adapted to low-carbon and zero-40 emission modes could face stranding under climate policy – depending on their current age and 41 expected lifetimes. Scenario evidence suggests that without carbon capture, the worldwide fleet of coal- 42 and gas power plants would need to retire about 23 and 17 years earlier than expected lifetimes, 43 respectively in order to limit global warming to 1.5°C and 2°C {2.7}.
