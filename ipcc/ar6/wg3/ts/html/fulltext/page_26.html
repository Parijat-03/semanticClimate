Globally, GHG emissions continued to rise across all sectors and subsectors, and most rapidly in 12 transport and industry (high confidence). In 2019, 34% (20 GtCO2-eq) of global GHG emissions 13 came from the energy sector, 24% (14 GtCO2-eq) from industry, 22% (13 GtCO2-eq) from agriculture, 14 forestry and other land use (AFOLU), 15% (8.7 GtCO2-eq) from transport, and 5.6% (3.3 GtCO2-eq) 15 from buildings. There is a discrepancy, equating to 5.5 GtCO2 yr-1, between alternative methods of accounting for 22 anthropogenic land CO2 fluxes. Accounting for this discrepancy would assist in assessing 23 collective progress in a global stocktake (high confidence). The principal accounting approaches are 24 National GHG inventories (NGHGI) and global modelling9approaches. NGHGI, based on IPCC 25 guidelines, consider a much larger area of forest to be under human management than global models. 26 NGHGI consider the fluxes due to human-induced environmental change on this area to be 27 anthropogenic and are thus reported. Global models, in contrast, consider these fluxes to be natural and 28 are excluded from the total reported anthropogenic land CO2 flux. The accounting method used will 29 affect the assessment of collective progress in a global stocktake {Cross-Chapter Box 6 in Chapter 7} 30 (medium confidence). In the absence of these adjustments, allowing a like with like comparison, 31 collective progress would appear better than it is. {7.2} 32
This accounting discrepancy also applies to Integrated Assessment Models (IAMs), with the 33 consequence that anthropogenic land CO2 fluxes reported in IAM pathways cannot be compared 34 directly with those reported in national GHG inventories (high confidence). Methodologies 35 enabling a more like-for-like comparison between models’ and countries’ approaches would 36 support more accurate assessment of the collective progress achieved under the Paris Agreement. {3.4, 37 7.2.2} 38
