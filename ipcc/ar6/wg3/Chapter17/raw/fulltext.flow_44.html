17.3.3.5
Mitigation-adaptation relations
17
The section will consider the links between mitigation and adaptation options in the context of
18
sustainable development and the associated synergies and trade-offs. Cross-cutting conclusions will be
19
drawn based on Chapter 3 and the sectoral chapters of AR6 WGIII and Chapter 18 of AR6 WGII. The
20
focus will be on the following sectors: agriculture, food and land use; water-energy-food; industry and
21
the circular economy; and urban areas.
22
23
IPCC, WG II, concludes that coherent and integrated policy-planning is needed in order to support
24
integrated climate change adaptation and mitigation policies and that this is a key component of climate-
25
resilient development pathways. Section 4.5.2 in Chapter 4 assesses development pathways and the
26
specific links between mitigation and adaptation, concluding that there can be co-benefits, and trade-
27
offs, where mitigation implies maladaptation. However, adaptation can also be a prerequisite for
28
mitigation. It is therefore concluded that making development pathways more sustainable can build the
29
capacity for both mitigation and adaptation.

31
Climate actions, including climate-change mitigation and adaptation, are highly scale-dependent, and
32
solutions are very context-specific. Especially in developing countries, a strong link exists between
33
sustainable development, vulnerability and climate risks, as limited economic, social and institutional
34
resources often result in low adaptive capacities and high vulnerability. Similarly, the limitations in
35
resources also constitute key elements weakening the capacity for climate-change mitigation (Jakob et
36
al. 2014). The change to climate-resilient societies requires transformational or systemic changes, which
37
also have important implications for the suite of available sustainable-development pathways (Kates et
38
al. 2012; Lemos et al. 2013). Thornton and Comberti (2017) point to the need for social-ecological
39
transformations to take place if synergies between mitigation and adaptation are to be captured, based
40
on the argument that incremental adaptation will not be sufficient when climate-change impacts can be
41
extreme or rapid and when deep decarbonization simultaneously involves social change (Chapter 18 in
42
AR6 WG II).
