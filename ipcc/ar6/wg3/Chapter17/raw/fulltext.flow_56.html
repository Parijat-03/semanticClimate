17.4.1.1 Social movements and education
6
Sustainable development and deep decarbonization will involve people and communities being
7
connected locally through various means – including globally via the internet and digital technologies
8
(Bradbury 2015; Scharmer 2018; Scharmer, C, Kaufer 2015) –in ways that form social fields that allow
9
sustainability to unfold (see also Gillard et al. 2016) and that prompt other shifts in thinking and
10
behaviour that are consistent with the 1.5°C goal (O’Brien 2018; Veciana and Ottmar 2018). Indeed,
11
social movements serve to develop collective identities, foster collective learning and accelerate
12
collective action ranging from energy justice (see Section 17.4.5) (Campos and Marín-González 2020)
13
to restricting fossil-fuel extraction and supply (Piggot 2018). This does not apply only to adults: as seen
14
in the “Fridays for Future” marches, the young are also involving themselves politically (Peterson et al.
15
2019). Many initiatives have started with these marches, including “science for future” and new forms
16
of sustainability science (Shrivastava et al. 2020).

It was Theory-U (Scharmer 2018, building on the work of scholars like Schein, Lewin or Senge) that
19
inspired a so-called “massive open online course” (MOOC) jointly initiated by the Bhutan Happiness
20
Institute and German Technical Assistance (GIZ) in 2015, since when it has been developed further and
21
adapted to transform business, society and self as one example of how social movements can go together
22
with science and education. It brings together people from different professions, cultures and continents
23
in shared discussions and practices of sustainability. It also included marginalised communities and is
24
shifting towards more sustainable lifestyles in all sectors (Nikas et al. 2020), including climate action.

Moreover, approaches like the “Art of Hosting” (Sandfort and Quick 2015) and qualitative research
27
methods like storytelling and first-person research, as well as second-person inquiries, for example
28
(Scharmer, C, Kaufer 2015; Trullen and Torbert 2004; Varela 1999), have been employed to bridge
29
differences in cultures and sciences, as well as to forge connections between those working on climate
30
change and sustainable development. Likewise, experiential tools, simulations and role-playing games
31
have been shown to increase knowledge of the causes and consequences of climate change, the sense
32
of urgency around action and the desire to pursue further learning (Ahamer 2013; Eisenack and Reckien
33
2013; Hallinger et al. 2020; Rooney-Varga et al. 2020).

38
colleagues (Goldman-Schuyler et al. 2017) and forming, for example, a very practical u.lab hub, which
39
involves following the MIT-u.lab course with a local community and is accompanied scientifically

41

42

43
as ways of generating the shared understandings that are central to inner and outer transitions, as well
44
as the broader development of social movements. In some cases, these networks build on principles like
45
permaculture to encourage people to “observe and interact,” “produce no waste” and “design from
46
patterns to details”, not only in agriculture and gardening, but also in sustainable businesses and
47
technologies to reduce CO
