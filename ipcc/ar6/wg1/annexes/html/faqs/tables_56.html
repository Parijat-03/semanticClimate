2 FAQ 9.3 | Will the Gulf Stream Shut Down?
3
4 The Gulf Stream is part of two circulation patterns in the North Atlantic: the Atlantic Meridional Overturning\nCirculation (AMOC) and the North Atlantic subtropical gyre. Based on models and theory, scientific studies\nindicate that, while the AMOC is expected to slow in a warming climate, the Gulf Stream will not change much\nand would not shut down totally, even if the AMOC did. Most climate models project that the AMOC slows in the\nlater 21st century under most emissions scenarios, with some models showing it slowing even sooner. The Gulf\nStream affects the weather and sea level, so if it slows, North America will see higher sea levels and Europe’s
5 weather and rate of relative warming will be affected.\nThe Gulf Stream is the biggest current in the North Atlantic Ocean. It transports about 30 billion kilograms
6 of water per second northward past points on the east coast of North America. It is a warm current, with\ntemperatures 5°C to 15°C warmer than surrounding waters, so it carries warmer water (thermal energy) from its
7 southern origins and releases warmth to the atmosphere and surrounding water.\nThe Gulf Stream is part of two major circulation patterns, the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC)
8 and the North Atlantic Subtropical Gyre (FAQ 9.3, Figure 1). The rotation of the Earth causes the big currents in\nboth circulations to stay on the western side of their basin, which in the Atlantic means the circulations combine\nto form the Gulf Stream. Other large currents contribute to gyres, such as the Kuroshio in the North Pacific\nand the East Australian Current in the South Pacific, but the Gulf Stream is special in its dual role. There is no\ncomparable deep overturning circulation in the North Pacific to the AMOC, so the Kuroshio plays only one role
9 as part of a gyre.\nThe gyres circulate surface waters and result primarily from winds driving the circulation. These winds are not
10 expected to change much and so neither will the gyres, which means the gyre portion of the Gulf Stream and the\nKuroshio will continue to transport thermal energy poleward from the equator much as they do now. The gyre
11 contribution to the Gulf Stream is 2 to 10 times larger than the AMOC contribution.\nThe Gulf Stream’s role in the AMOC is supplying surface source water that cools, becomes denser and sinks to
12 form cold, deep waters that travel back equatorward, spilling over features on the ocean floor and mixing with\nother deep Atlantic waters to form a southward current at a depth of about 1500 metres beneath the Gulf\nStream. This overturning flow is the AMOC, with the Gulf Stream in the upper kilometre flowing northward,
13 and the colder deep water flowing southward.\nThe AMOC is expected to slow over the coming centuries. One reason why is freshening of the ocean waters:
14 by meltwater from Greenland, changing Arctic sea ice, and increased precipitation over warmer northern seas.\nAn array of moorings across the Atlantic has been monitoring the AMOC since 2004, with recently expanded\ncapabilities. The monitoring of the AMOC has not been long enough for a trend to emerge from variability\nand detect long-term changes that may be underway (see FAQ 1.2). Other indirect signs may indicate slowing\noverturning – for example, slower warming where the Gulf Stream’s surface waters sink. Climate models show\nthat this ‘cold spot’ of slower-than-average warming occurs as the AMOC weakens, and they project that this\nwill continue. Paleoclimate evidence indicates that the AMOC changed significantly in the past, especially during
15 transitions from colder climates to warmer ones, but that it has been stable for 8000 years.\nWhat happens if the AMOC slows in a warming world? The atmosphere adjusts somewhat by carrying more heat,
16 compensating partly for the decreases in heat carried by AMOC. But the ‘cold spot’ makes parts of Europe warm\nmore slowly. Models indicate that weather patterns in Greenland and around the Atlantic will be affected, with\nreduced precipitation in the mid-latitudes, changing strong precipitation patterns in the tropics and Europe, and\nstronger storms in the North Atlantic storm track. The slowing of this current combined with the rotation of the
17 Earth means that sea level along North America rises as the AMOC contribution to the Gulf Stream slows.\nThe North Atlantic is not the only site of sensitive meridional overturning. Around Antarctica, the world’s densest
18 seawater is formed by freezing into sea ice, leaving behind salty, cold water that sinks to the bottom and spreads\nnorthward. Recent studies show that melting of the Antarctic Ice Sheet and changing winds over the Southern\nOcean can affect this southern meridional overturning, affecting regional weather.
