Electrification is emerging as a key mitigation option for industry (high confidence). Using 8 electricity directly, or indirectly via hydrogen from electrolysis for high temperature and chemical 9 feedstock requirements, offers many options to reduce emissions. It also can provide substantial grid 10 balancing services, for example through electrolysis and storage of hydrogen for chemical process use 11 or demand response. (Box TS.9) {11.3.5} 12
Carbon is a key building block in organic chemicals, fuels and materials and will remain 13 important (high confidence). In order to reach net zero CO2 emissions for the carbon needed in society 14 (e.g., plastics, wood, aviation fuels, solvents, etc.), it is important to close the use loops for carbon and 15 carbon dioxide through increased circularity with mechanical and chemical recycling, more efficient 16 use of biomass feedstock with addition of low GHG hydrogen to increase product yields (e.g., for 17 biomethane and methanol), and potentially direct air capture of CO2 as a new carbon source. {11.3, 18 11.4.1} 19
Production costs for very low to zero emissions basic materials may be high but the cost for final 20 consumers and the general economy will be low (medium confidence). Costs and emissions 21 reductions potential in industry, and especially heavy industry, are highly contingent on innovation, 22 commercialisation, and market uptake policies. Technologies exist to take all industry sectors to very 23 low or zero emissions, but require 5–15 years of intensive innovation, commercialisation, and policy to 24 ensure uptake. Mitigation costs are in the rough range of USD50–150 tCO2-eq-1, with wide variation 25 within and outside this band. This affects competitiveness and requires supporting policy. Although 26 production cost increases can be significant, they translate to very small increases in the costs for final 27 products, typically less than a few percent depending on product, assumptions, and system boundaries. 28 (Figure TS.17) {11.4.1.5} 29
Several technological options exist for very low to zero emissions steel, but their uptake will 30 require integrated material efficiency, recycling, and production decarbonisation policies (high 31 confidence). Material efficiency can potentially reduce steel demand by up to 40% based on design for 32 less steel use, long life, reuse, constructability, and low contamination recycling. Secondary production 33 through high quality recycling must be maximised. Production decarbonisation will also be required, 34 starting with the retrofitting of existing facilities for partial fuel switching (e.g., to biomass or hydrogen), 35 CCU and CCS, followed by very low and zero emissions production based on high-capture CCS or 36 direct hydrogen, or electrolytic iron ore reduction followed by an electric arc furnace. {11.3.2, 11.4.1.1} 37
Several current and emerging options can significantly reduce cement and concrete emissions. 38 Producer, user, and regulator education, as well as innovation and commercialisation policy are 39 needed (medium confidence). Cement and concrete are currently overused because they are 40 inexpensive, durable, and ubiquitous, and consumption decisions typically do not give weight to their 41 production emissions. Basic material efficiency efforts to use only well-made concrete thoughtfully and 42 only where needed (e.g., using right-sized, prefabricated components) could reduce emissions by 24–43 50% through lower demand for clinker. Cementitious material substitution with various materials (e.g., 44 ground limestone and calcined clays) can reduce process calcination emissions by up to 50% and 45 occasionally much more.
