2 FAQ 6.2 | What Are the Links Between Limiting Climate Change and Improving\nAir Quality?
3
4 Climate change and air quality are intimately linked. Many of the human activities that produce long-lived\ngreenhouse gases also emit air pollutants, and many of these air pollutants are also ‘short-lived climate forcers’\nthat affect the climate. Therefore, many options for improving air quality may also serve to limit climate change\nand vice versa. However, some options for improving air quality cause additional climate warming, and some
5 actions that address climate change can worsen air quality.\nClimate change and air pollution are both critical environmental issues that are already affecting humanity.
6 In 2016, the World Health Organization attributed 4.2 million deaths worldwide every year to ambient (outdoor)\nair pollution. Meanwhile, climate change impacts water resources, food production, human health, extreme
7 events, coastal erosion, wildfires, and many other phenomena.\nMost human activities, including energy production, agriculture, transportation, industrial processes, waste
8 management and residential heating and cooling, result in emissions of gaseous and particulate pollutants that\nmodify the composition of the atmosphere, leading to degradation of air quality as well as to climate change.\nThese air pollutants are also short-lived climate forcers – substances that affect the climate but remain in the\natmosphere for shorter periods (days to decades) than long-lived greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide (see\nFAQ 6.1). While this means that the issues of air pollution and climate change are intimately connected, air\npollutants and greenhouse gases are often defined, investigated and regulated independently of one another in
9 both the scientific and policy arenas.\nMany sources simultaneously emit carbon dioxide and air pollutants. When we drive our fossil fuel vehicles
10 or light a fire in the fireplace, it is not just carbon dioxide or air pollutants that are emitted, but always both.\nIt is therefore not possible to separate emissions into two clearly distinct groups. As a result, policies aiming at
11 addressing climate change may have benefits or side effects for air quality, and vice versa.\nFor example, some short-term ‘win-win’ policies that simultaneously improve air quality and limit climate change
12 include the implementation of energy efficiency measures, methane capture and recovery from solid-waste\nmanagement and the oil and gas industry, zero-emissions vehicles, efficient and clean stoves for heating and\ncooking, filtering of soot (particulate matter) for diesel vehicles, cleaner brick-kiln technology, practices that
13 reduce burning of agricultural waste, and the eradication of burning of kerosene for lighting.\nThere are, however, also ‘win-lose’ actions. For example, wood burning is defined as carbon neutral because
14 a tree accumulates the same amount of carbon dioxide throughout its lifetime as is released when wood from\nthat tree is burned. However, burning wood can also result in significant emissions of air pollutants, including\ncarbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, volatile organic compounds, and particulate matter, that locally or regionally\naffect the climate, human health and ecosystems (FAQ 6.2, Figure 1). Alternatively, decreasing the amount of\nsulphate aerosols produced by power and industrial plants, and from maritime transport, improves air quality\nbut results in a warming influence on the climate, because those sulphate aerosols contribute to cooling the
15 atmosphere by blocking incoming sunlight.\nAir quality and climate change represent two sides of the same coin, and addressing both issues together could
16 lead to significant synergies and economic benefits while avoiding policy actions that mitigate one of the two\nissues but worsen the other.
