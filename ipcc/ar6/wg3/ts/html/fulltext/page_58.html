To use energy, it must be “carried” from where it was produced – at a power plant, for example, or a 4 refinery, or a coal mine – to where it is used. As countries reduce CO2 emissions, they will need to 5 switch from gasoline and other petroleum-based fuels, natural gas, coal, and electricity produced from 6 these fossil fuels to energy carriers with little or no carbon footprint. An important question is which 7 new energy carriers will emerge to support low-carbon transitions. 8
Low-carbon energy systems are expected to rely heavily on end-use electrification, where electricity 9 produced with low GHG emissions is used for building and industrial heating, transport and other 10 applications that rely heavily on fossil fuels at present. But not all end-uses are expected to be 11 commercially electrifiable in the short to medium term {11.3.5}, and many will require low GHG liquid 12 and gaseous fuels, i.e., hydrogen, ammonia, and biogenic and synthetic low GHG hydrocarbons made 13 from low GHG hydrogen, oxygen and carbon sources (the latter from CCU16, biomass, or direct air 14 capture {11.3.6}). The future role of hydrogen and hydrogen derivatives will depend on how quickly 15 and how far production technology improves, i.e. from electrolysis (“green”), biogasification, and fossil 16 fuel reforming with CCS (“blue”) sources. As a general rule, and across all sectors, it is more efficient 17 to use electricity directly and avoid the progressively larger conversion losses from producing hydrogen, 18 ammonia, or constructed low GHG hydrocarbons. What hydrogen does do, however, is add time and 19 space option value to electricity produced using variable clean sources, for use as hydrogen, as stored 20 future electricity via a fuel cell or turbine, or as an industrial feedstock. Furthermore, electrification and 21 hydrogen involve a symbiotic range of general-purpose technologies, such as electric motors, power 22 electronics, heat pumps, batteries, electrolysis, fuel cells etc., that have different applications across 23 sectors but cumulative economies of innovation and production scale benefits. Finally, neither 24 electrification nor hydrogen produce local air pollutants at point of end-use. 25
For almost 140 years we have primarily produced electricity by burning coal, oil, and gas to drive steam 26 turbines connected to electricity generators. When switching to low-carbon energy sources – renewable 27 sources, nuclear power, and fossil or bioenergy with CCS – electricity is expected to become a more 28 pervasive energy carrier. Electricity is a versatile energy carrier, with much higher end-use efficiencies 29 than fuels, and it can be used directly to avoid conversion losses. 30
An increasing reliance on electricity from variable renewable sources, notably wind and solar power, 31 disrupts old concepts and makes many existing guidelines obsolete for power system planning, e.g., 32 that specific generation types are needed for baseload, intermediate load, and peak load to follow and 33 meet demand. In future power systems with high shares of variable electricity from renewable sources, 34 system planning and markets will focus more on demand flexibility, grid infrastructure and 35 interconnections, storage on various timelines (on the minute, hourly, overnight and seasonal scale), 36 and increased coupling between the energy sector and the building, transport and industrial sectors. This 37 shifts the focus to energy systems that can handle variable supply rather than always follow demand. 38 Hydrogen may prove valuable to improve the resilience of electricity systems with high penetration of 39 variable renewable electricity. Flexible hydrogen electrolysis, hydrogen power plants and long-duration 40 hydrogen storage may all improve resilience. Electricity-to-hydrogen-to-electricity round-trip 41 efficiencies are projected to reach up to 50% by 2030. {6.4.3} 42
FOOTNOTE16Carbon dioxide capture and utilisation (CCU) refers to a process in which CO2is captured and the carbon is then used in a product. The climate effect of CCU depends on the product lifetime, the product it displaces, and the CO2source (fossil, biomass or atmosphere). CCU is sometimes referred to as Carbon Dioxide Capture and Use, or Carbon Capture and Utilisation.
