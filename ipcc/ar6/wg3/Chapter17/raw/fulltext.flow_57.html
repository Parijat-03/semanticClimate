17.4.1.2 Habits, values and awareness
12
Many of the cases that explore transitions to sustainable development point to engrained habits, values
13
and awareness levels as the most persistent yet least visible barriers to a transition. For example, in the
14
transport sector, individuals can quickly become accustomed to personal vehicles, making it difficult
15
for them to transition to sustainable, low-carbon modes of public transport. Demand for high-carbon
16
transportation may also be locked in, and habits reinforced, if low-cost housing (for instance) is not
17
sufficiently served by more sustainable (i.e. mass transit, safe cycling and walking infrastructure)
18
transportation options (Mattioli et al. 2020).
19
20
This is made all the more challenging because car-manufacturing “incumbents” utilize information
21
campaigns directed at the public, pursue lobbying and consulting with policy-makers, and set technical
22
standards that privilege the status quo and prevent the entry of more sustainable innovations (Smink et
23
al. 2015; Turnheim and Nykvist 2019). Tools such as congestion pricing, however, have been shown to
24
be effective in motivating the switch from single-occupancy vehicle use to public transit, thus
25
improving air quality and reducing traffic delays in dense city centres (Baghestani et al. 2020).
26
27
Complicating the problem further is that even well-intentioned top-down programmes initiated by an
28
external actor may in some cases ultimately hinder transformative change (Breukers et al. 2017). For
29
instance, in Delhi, India, attempts to introduce ostensibly more sustainable bus rapid transit (BRT)
30
systems failed in part due to an arguably top-down approach that had limited public support. It may
31
nonetheless be difficult to win public support (Bachus and Vanswijgenhoven 2018), and even grassroots
32
initiatives may themselves be contested and dynamic, making it difficult to generate the collective push
33
to drive a bottom-up transition forward (Hakansson 2018).
34
35
However, dominant, top-down approaches and local, grassroots "alternative" approaches and values do
36
overlap and interact. For example, in Manchester, UK, dominant and alternative discourses interact with
37
each other to create sustainable transformations through re-scaling (decentralizing) energy generation,
38
creating local engagement with sustainability, supporting green infrastructure to reduce costs, re-
39
claiming local land, transforming industrial infrastructure and creating examples of sustainable living
40
(Hodson et al. 2017).
41
42
Embedding local values in higher-level policy frameworks is also significant for forest communities in
43
Nepal and Uganda. Even so, policy intermediaries are not confident that these values will be advanced
44
due largely to an emphasis on carbon accounting and the distribution of benefits (Reckien et al. 2018).
45
In this case, however, norm entrepreneurs were able to promote the importance of local values through
46
the formation of grassroots associations, media campaigns and international support networks (Reckien
47
et al. 2018).

Technological and social innovation 50
Individuals and organizations, like institutional entrepreneurs, can function to build transformative
51
capacity through collective action (Brodnik and Brown 2018). The transition from a traditional water-
52
management system to the Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD) model in Melbourne offers an
53
illustration of how whole systems can be changed in an urban system.
