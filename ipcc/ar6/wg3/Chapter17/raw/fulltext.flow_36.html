17.3.3.2 Water-Energy-Food-Nexus
16
This section addresses the links between water, energy and food in the context of sustainable
17
development and the associated synergies and trade-offs, with links to related chapters. The focus
18
outline includes scoping and the relationship with the SDGs, general climate-change impacts on global
19
water resources, energy-system impacts and the relationship to renewables, enabling strategies, trade-
20
offs and cross-sectoral implications (see also Chapter 12), nexus-management tools and strategies, and
21
a box with examples from India and South Africa.
22
23
The continually increasing pressures on natural resources, such as land and water, due to the rising
24
demands from increases in population and living standards, which also require more energy, emphasises
25
the need to integrate sustainable planning and exploitation (Bleischwitz et al. 2018).
26
The water-energy-food nexus is at the epicentre of these challenges, which are of global relevance and
27
are the focus of policies and planning at all levels and sectors of the global society. The nexus between
28
water, energy and food (WEFN) (Zhang et al. 2018b) is tight and complex, and needs careful attention
29
and deciphering across spatio-temporal scales, sectors and interests to balance proper management and
30
trade-offs and to pursue sustainable development (Biggs et al. 2015; Dai et al. 2018; Hamiche et al.
31
2016). The WEFN touches upon the majority of the UN’s SDGs, such as 2, 6-7 and 11-15 (Bleischwitz
32
et al. 2018), and deals with basic commodities, thus guaranteeing the basic livelihoods of the global
33
population.
34
35
The task of gaining an improved understanding of WEFN processes across disciplines such as the
36
natural sciences, economics, the social sciences and politics has been further exacerbated by climate
37
change, population growth and resource depletion. In light of the system of interlinkages involved, the
38
WEFN concept essentially also covers land (Ringler et al. 2013) and climate (Brouwer et al. 2018;
39
Sušnik et al. 2018) and can be further assessed in light of the relevant economic, ecological, social and
40
SDG aspects (Fan et al. 2019a). Fan et al. (2019b) specifically, SDGs 2 (food), 6 (water), (7) energy,
41
11 (cities) and 12 (production and consumption) are considered essential to the WEFN (Bleischwitz et
42
al. 2018). The nexus approach was introduced in the early 2010s, when it was argued that advantages
43
could be gained by adopting a nexus approach with regard to cross-sectoral and human–nature
44
dependencies and by taking externalities into account (Hoffmann 2011). Hence, within the nexus,
45
obvious trade-offs exist with competing interests, such as water availability versus food production.
46
