0 Box TS.11 | Climate Services
1
2 Climate services involve providing climate information to assist decision-making, for example, about how extreme\nrainfall will change to inform improvements in urban drainage. Since AR5, there has been a significant increase in the\nrange and diversity of climate service activities (very high confidence). The level of user-engagement, co-design and\nco-production are factors determining the utility of climate services, while resource limitations for these activities\nconstrain their full potential. {12.6, Cross-Chapter Box 12.2}
3
4 Climate services include engagement from users and providers and an effective access mechanism; they are responsive to user needs\nand based on integrating scientifically credible information and relevant expertise. Climate services are being developed across\nregions, sectors, time scales and user-groups and include a range of knowledge brokerage and integration activities. These involve\nidentifying knowledge needs; compiling, translating and disseminating knowledge; coordinating networks and building capacity\nthrough informed decision-making; analysis, evaluation and development of policy; and personal consultation.
5
6 Since AR5, climate change information produced in climate service contexts has increased significantly due to scientific and\ntechnological advancements and growing user awareness, requirements and demand (very high confidence). Climate services are\ngrowing rapidly and are highly diverse in their practices and products. The decision-making context, level of user engagement and co-\nproduction between scientists, practitioners and intended users are important determinants of the type of climate service developed\nand their utility for supporting adaptation, mitigation and risk management decisions. They require different types of user–producer\nengagement depending on what the service aims to deliver (high confidence), and these fall into three broad categories: website-\nbased services, interactive group activities and focused relationships.
7 Realization of the full potential of climate services is often hindered by limited resources for the co-design and co-production process,\nincluding sustained engagement between scientists, service providers and users (high confidence). Further challenges relate to the\ndevelopment and provision of climate services, generation of climate service products, communication with users, and evaluation of\ntheir quality and socio-economic benefit. 1
2 A key novel element in the AR6 is the Working Group I Atlas, which includes the Interactive Atlas (https://interactive-\natlas.ipcc.ch/). The Interactive Atlas provides the ability to explore much of the observational and climate model data\nused as lines of evidence in this assessment to generate regional climate information. {Atlas.2}
3
4 A significant innovation in the AR6 WGI Report is the Atlas. Part of its remit is to provide region-by-region assessment on changes in\nmean climate and to link with other WGI chapters to generate climate change information for the regions. An important component\nis the new online interactive tool, the Interactive Atlas, with flexible spatial and temporal analyses of much of the observed, simulated\npast and projected future climate change data underpinning the WGI assessment. This includes the ability to generate global maps\nand a number of regionally aggregated products (time series, scatter plots, tables, etc.) for a range of observations and ensemble\nclimate change projections of variables (such as changes in the climatic impact-drivers summarized in Table TS.5) from the Coupled\nModel Intercomparison Project Phases 5 and 6 (CMIP5, CMIP6) and the Coordinated Regional Climate Downscaling Experiment\n(CORDEX). The data can be displayed and summarized under a range of SSP-RCP scenarios and future time slices and also for different\nglobal warming levels, relative to several different baseline periods. The maps and various statistics can be generated for annual mean\ntrends and changes or for any user-specified season. A new set of WGI reference regions is used for the regional summary statistics\nand applied widely throughout the report (with the regions, along with aggregated datasets and the code to generate these, available\nat the ATLAS GitHub: https://github.com/IPCC-WG1/Atlas).
5
6 Box TS.12, Figure 1 shows how the Interactive Atlas products, together with other lines of evidence, can be used to generate climate\ninformation for an illustrative example of the Mediterranean summer warming. The lines of evidence include the understanding of\nrelevant mechanisms, dynamic and thermodynamic processes and the effect of aerosols in this case (Box TS.12, Figure 1a); trends\nin observational datasets (which can have different spatial and temporal coverage; Box TS.12, Figure 1b, c); and attribution of these\ntrends and temperature projections from global and regional climate models at different resolutions, including single-model initial-\ncondition large ensembles (SMILEs; Box TS.12, Figure 1d, e).
