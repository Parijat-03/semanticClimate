TS. 6 Implementation and enabling conditions 1
Chapters 13-16 address the enabling conditions that can accelerate or impede rapid progress on 2 mitigation. Chapters 13 and 14 focus on policy, governance and institutional capacity, and international 3 cooperation, respectively taking a national and international perspective; Chapter 15 focusses on 4 investment and finance; and Chapter 16 focusses on innovation and technology. The assessment of 5 social aspects of mitigation draws on material assessed in Chapter 5. 6
TS. 6.1 Policy and Institutions 7
Long-term deep emission reductions, including the reduction of emissions to net zero, is best 8 achieved through institutions and governance that nurture new mitigation policies, while at the 9 same time reconsidering existing policies that support the continued emission of GHGs (high 10 confidence). To do so effectively, the scope of climate governance needs to include both direct efforts 11 to target GHG emissions and indirect opportunities to tackle GHG emissions that result from efforts 12 directed towards other policy objectives. {13.2, 13.5, 13.6, 13.7, 13.9} 13
Institutions and governance underpin mitigation by providing the legal basis for action. This 14 includes setting up implementing organisations and the frameworks through which diverse actors 15 interact (medium evidence, high agreement). Institutions can create mitigation and sectoral policy 16 instruments; policy packages for low-carbon system transition; and economy wide measures for 17 systemic restructuring. {13.2, 13.7, 13.9} 18
Policies have had a discernible impact on mitigation for specific countries, sectors, and 19 technologies (high confidence), avoiding emissions of several GtCO2-eq yr-1(medium confidence). 20 Both market-based and regulatory policies have distinct, but complementary roles. The share of global 21 GHG emissions subject to mitigation policy has increased rapidly in recent years, but big gaps remain 22 in policy coverage, and the stringency of many policies falls short of what is needed to achieve the 23 desired mitigation outcomes. (Box TS.13) {13.6, Cross-Chapter Box 10 in Chapter 14} 24
Climate laws enable mitigation action by signalling the direction of travel, setting targets, 25 mainstreaming mitigation into sector policies, enhancing regulatory certainty, creating law-26 backed agencies, creating focal points for social mobilisation, and attracting international finance 27 (medium evidence, high agreement). By 2020, ‘direct’ climate laws primarily focused on GHG 28 reductions were present in 56 countries covering 53% of global emissions (see Figure TS.24). More 29 than 690 laws, including ‘indirect’ laws, however, may also have an effect on mitigation. Among direct 30 laws, ‘framework’ laws set an overarching legal basis for mitigation either by pursuing a target and 31 implementation approach, or by seeking to mainstream climate objectives through sectoral plans and 32 integrative institutions. (Figure TS.24) {13.2} 33
Institutions can enable improved governance by coordinating across sectors, scales and actors, 34 building consensus for action, and setting strategies (medium evidence, high agreement). 35 Institutions are more stable and effective when they are congruent with national contexts, leading to 36 mitigation-focused institutions in some countries and the pursuit of multiple objectives in others. Sub-37 national institutions play a complementary role to national institutions by developing locally-relevant 38 visions and plans, addressing policy gaps or limits in national institutions, building local administrative 39 structures and convening actors for place-based decarbonisation. {13.2} 40
Mitigation strategies, instruments and policies that fit with dominant ideas, values and belief 41 systems within a country or within a sector are more easily adopted and implemented (medium 42 confidence). Ideas, values and beliefs may change over time. Policies that bring perceived direct 43 benefits, such as subsidies, usually receive greater support. The awareness of co-benefits for the public 44 increases support of climate policies (high confidence). {13.2, 13.3, 13.4} 45
