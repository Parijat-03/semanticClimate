17.2
Accelerating Transitions in the Context of Sustainable Development:
16
Definitions and Theories
17
This section focuses on how different theoretical frameworks can help us understand and explain what
18
is meant by accelerating transitions in the context of sustainable development. As suggested in sect.
19
17.1, the reference to “in the context of sustainable development” suggests that sustainable transitions
20
require more than speed, also necessitating removing the underlying drivers of vulnerability and high
21
emissions (quality and depth of transitions) while also aligning the interests of different individuals,
22
communities, sectors, stakeholders and cultures (scale and breadth of transitions).
23
24
The outcome of sustainable transitions is a sustainable transformation. While transitions involve
25
processes that shift development pathways and reorient energy, transport, urban and other subsystems
26
(Loorbach et al. 2017; Chapter 16), transformation is the resulting fundamental reorganization of large-
27
scale socioeconomic systems (Hölscher et al. 2018). Such a fundamental reorganization often requires
28
dynamic multi-stage transition processes that change everything from public policies and prevailing
29
technologies to individual lifestyles, and social norms to governance arrangements and institutions of
30
political economy. This set of factors can lock in development pathways and prevent transitions from
31
gathering the momentum needed for transformations. Chapter 16 (above) provides an overview of the
32
multistage transition dynamics involved in moving from experimentation to commercialization to
33
integration to stabilization. That overview describes how transitions can break through lock-ins and
34
result in a transformation.
35
36
While there may be a relatively consistent set of transition dynamics for all countries, pathways are
37
likely to vary across and even within countries. This variation is due to different development levels,
38
starting points, capacities, agencies, geographies, power dynamics, political economies, ecosystems and
39
other contextual factors. Given the diversity of contributing factors, a sustainable transition is likely to
40
be a complex and multi-faceted process which cannot be reduced to a single dimension (Köhler et al.
41
2019). Even with this multi-dimensionality, transition processes are likely to gain speed and become
42
more sustainable as decision-makers adopt targeted policies and other interventions. Many disciplines
43
have reflected on the roles of and relative influence on the policies and interventions that can drive
44
transitions. The following discussion describes this diversity of views with a survey of how prominent
45
lines of economic, psychological, institutional and systems thinking explain transitions. Though these
46
disciplines differ greatly, they often stress that leveraging synergies and managing trade-offs between
47
climate change and sustainable development can help advance a transition.
