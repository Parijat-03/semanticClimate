Box TS.12: Circular Economy (CE) 19
In AR6, the Circular Economy (CE) concept {Annex I} is highlighted as an increasingly important 20 mitigation approach that can help deliver human well-being by minimising waste of energy and 21 resources. While definitions of CE vary, its essence is to shift away from linear “make and dispose” 22 economic models to those that emphasize product longevity, reuse, refurbishment, recycling, and 23 material efficiency, thereby enabling more circular material systems that reduce embodied energy and 24 emissions. {5.3.4, 8.4, 8.5, 9.5, 11.3.3} 25
Whereas IPCC AR4 {WG III, Chapter 10} included a separate chapter on waste sector emissions and 26 waste management practices, and AR5 {WG III, Chapter 10} reviewed the importance of “reduce, 27 reuse, recycle” and related policies, AR6 focuses on how CE can reduce waste in materials production 28 and consumption by optimising materials’ end-use service utility. Specific examples of CE 29 implementations, policies, and mitigation potentials are included in chapters 5, 8, 9, 11 and 12. {5.3, 30 8.4, 9.5, 11.3, 12.6} 31
CE is shown to empower new social actors in mitigation actions, given that it relies on the synergistic 32 actions of producers, sellers, and consumers {11.3.3}. As an energy and resource demand-reduction 33 strategy, it is consistent with high levels of human well-being {5.3.4.3} and ensures better 34 environmental quality (Figure TS.22) {5.2.1}. It also creates jobs through increased sharing, reuse, 35 refurbishment, and recycling activities. Therefore, CE contributes to several SDGs, including Clean 36 Water and Sanitation (SDG6), Affordable Energy and Clean Energy (SDG7), Decent Work and 37 Economic Growth (SDG8), Responsible Production and Consumption (SDG12) and Climate Action 38 (SDG13). {11.5.3.2} 39
