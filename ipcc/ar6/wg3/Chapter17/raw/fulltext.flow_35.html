7

8

10

12

13

In 2007, Britain launched a nationwide initiative to reduce household food waste, which achieved a 21
29
percent reduction within five years (FAO 2019). The basis of this initiative was the “Love Food, Hate
30
Waste” radio, TV, print and online media campaign run by a non-profit organization, the Waste and
31
Resources Action Programme (WRAP). The campaign raised awareness among consumers about how
32
much food they waste, how it affects their household budgets and what they can do about it. This
33
initiative collaborated with food manufacturers and retailers to stimulate innovation, such as re-sealable
34
packaging, shared meal-planning and food-storage tips. The total implementation costs during the five-
35
year period were estimated at GBP 26 million, from which it was households that derived the most
36
benefit, estimated to be worth GBP 6.5 billion. Local authorities also realized a substantial GBP 86
37
million worth of savings in food-waste disposal costs. As for the private sector, the benefits took the
38
form of increased product shelf lives and reduced product loss. While households started to consume
39
more efficiently and companies may have experienced a decline in food sales, the latter also stated that
40
the non-financial benefits, such as strengthened consumer relationships, had offset the costs.
