TS. 5.7 Carbon dioxide removal (CDR) 1
CDR is a key element in scenarios that likely limit warming to 2°C or 1.5°C by 2100 (high 2 confidence). Implementation strategies need to reflect that CDR methods differ in terms of removal 3 process, timescale of carbon storage, technological maturity, mitigation potential, cost, co-benefits, 4 adverse side-effects, and governance requirements. Box TS.10: Carbon Dioxide Removal 31
Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR) is necessary to achieve net zero CO2 and GHG emissions both globally 32 and nationally, counterbalancing ‘hard-to-abate’ residual emissions. CDR is also an essential element 33 of scenarios that limit warming to 1.5°C or likely below 2°C by 2100, regardless of whether global 34 emissions reach near zero, net zero or net negative levels. While national mitigation portfolios aiming 35 at net zero emissions or lower will need to include some level of CDR, the choice of methods and the 36 scale and timing of their deployment will depend on the achievement of gross emission reductions, and 37 managing multiple sustainability and feasibility constraints, including political preferences and social 38 acceptability. 39
CDR refers to anthropogenic activities removing CO2 from the atmosphere and durably storing it in 40 geological, terrestrial, or ocean reservoirs, or in products. It includes existing and potential 41 anthropogenic enhancement of biological, geochemical or chemical CO2 sinks, but excludes natural 42 CO2 uptake not directly caused by human activities (Annex I).
