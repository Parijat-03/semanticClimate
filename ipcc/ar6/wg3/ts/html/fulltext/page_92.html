Diets high in plant protein and low in meat and dairy are associated with lower GHG emissions 5 (high confidence). Ruminant meat shows the highest GHG intensity. Beef from dairy systems has lower 6 emissions intensity than beef from beef herds (8-23 and 17-94 kgCO2-eq (100g protein)-1, respectively) 7 when some emissions are allocated to dairy products. The wide variation in emissions reflects 8 differences in production systems, which range from intensive feedlots with stock raised largely on 9 grains through to rangeland and transhumance production systems. Where appropriate, a shift to diets 10 with a higher share of plant protein, moderate intake of animal-source foods and reduced intake of 11 saturated fats could lead to substantial decreases in GHG emissions. Benefits would also include 12 reduced land occupation and nutrient losses to the surrounding environment, while at the same time 13 providing health benefits and reducing mortality from diet-related non-communicable diseases. Emerging food technologies such as cellular fermentation, cultured meat, plant-based 16 alternatives to animal-based food products, and controlled environment agriculture, can bring 17 substantial reduction in direct GHG emissions from food production (limited evidence, high 18 agreement). These technologies have lower land, water, and nutrient footprints, and address concerns 19 over animal welfare. Realising the full mitigation potential depends on access to low-carbon energy as 20 some emerging technologies are relatively more energy intensive. This also holds for deployment of 21 cold chain and packaging technologies, which can help reduce food loss and waste, but increase energy 22 and materials use in the food system.
