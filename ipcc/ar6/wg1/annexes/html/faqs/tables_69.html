2 FAQ 12.2 | What Are Climatic Thresholds and Why Are They Important?
3
4 Climatic thresholds tell us about the tolerance of society and ecosystems so that we can better scrutinize the types\nof climate changes that are expected to impact things we care about. Many systems have natural or structural\nthresholds. If conditions exceed those thresholds, the result can be sudden changes or even collapses in health,\nproductivity, utility or behaviour. Adaptation and risk management efforts can change these thresholds, altering
5 the profile of climate conditions that would be problematic and increasing overall system resilience.\nDecision makers have long observed that certain weather and climate conditions can be problematic, or
6 hazardous, for things they care about (i.e., things with socio-economic, cultural or intrinsic value). Many elements\nof society and ecosystems operate in a suitable climate zone selected naturally or by stakeholders considering\nthe expected climate conditions. However, as climate change moves conditions beyond expected ranges, they\nmay cross a climatic ‘threshold’ – a level beyond which there are either gradual changes in system behaviour or
7 abrupt, non-linear and potentially irreversible impacts.\nClimatic thresholds can be associated with either natural or structural tolerance levels. Natural thresholds, for
8 instance, include heat and humidity conditions above which humans cannot regulate their internal temperatures\nthrough sweat, drought durations that heighten competition between species, and winter temperatures that\nare lethal for pests or disease-carrying vector species. Structural thresholds include engineered limits of drainage\nsystems, extreme wind speeds that limit wind turbine operation, the height of coastal protection infrastructure,
9 and the locations of irrigation infrastructure or tropical cyclone sheltering facilities.\nThresholds may be defined according to raw values (such as maximum temperature exceeding 35°C) or percentiles
10 (such as the local 99th percentile daily rainfall total). They also often have strong seasonal dependence\n(see FAQ 12.3). For example, the amount of snowfall that a deciduous tree can withstand depends on whether\nthe snowfall occurs before or after the tree sheds its leaves. Most systems respond to changes in complex ways,\nand those responses are not determined solely or precisely by specific thresholds of a single climate variable.\nNonetheless, thresholds can be useful indicators of system behaviours, and an understanding of these thresholds
11 can help inform risk management decisions.\nFAQ 12.2 Figure 1 illustrates how threshold conditions can help us understand climate conditions that are
12 suitable for normal system operation and the thresholds beyond which impacts occur. Crops tend to grow most\noptimally within a suitable range of daily temperatures that is influenced by the varieties being cultivated and\nthe way the farm is managed. As daily temperatures rise above a ‘critical’ temperature threshold, plants begin\nto experience heat stress that reduces growth and may lower resulting yields. If temperatures reach a higher\n‘limiting’ temperature threshold, crops may suffer leaf loss, pollen sterility, or tissue damage that can lead to\ncrop failure. Farmers typically select a cropping system with some consideration to the probability of extreme\ntemperature events that may occur within a typical season, and so identifying hot temperature thresholds helps\nfarmers select their seed and field management strategies as part of their overall risk management. Climate\nexperts may therefore aim to assist farm planning by providing information about the climate change-induced
13 shifts to the expected frequency of daily heat extremes that exceed crop tolerance thresholds.\nAdaptation and other changes in societies and environment can shift climatic thresholds by modifying vulnerability
14 and exposure. For example, adaptation efforts may include breeding new crops with higher heat tolerance levels\nso that corresponding dangerous thresholds occur less frequently. Likewise, increasing the height of a flood\nembankment protecting a given community can increase the level of river flow that may be tolerated without\nflooding, reducing the frequency of damaging floods. Stakeholders therefore benefit from climate services that\nare based on a co-development process, with scientists identifying system-relevant thresholds and developing\ntailored climatic impact-driver indices that represent these thresholds (FAQ 12.1). These thresholds help focus the\nprovision of action-relevant climate information for adaptation and risk management.
