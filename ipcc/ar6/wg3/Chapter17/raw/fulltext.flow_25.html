Presently, coal-fired power plants play a key role in the German energy system, providing almost 46%
15
of the electricity consumed in Germany. These coal power plants play a crucial role in balancing
16
fluctuations in producing electricity form renewables (Parra et al. 2019). Political and economic
17
considerations, at least regionally, are also of great importance in the coal sector due to the
18
approximately 35,000 people employed within it (including coal-mining and the power stations
19
themselves). For a long time, coal-fired power plants were able to protect their position in Germany,
20
but against the background of decreasing public acceptance, economic problems resulting from the
21
growing use of renewables and ambitious GHG reduction targets, the sector cannot resist the political
22
pressures against them any longer. The governing parties have agreed to establish a commission called
23
“Growth, structural change and employment” to develop a strategy for phasing out coal-fired power
24
plants (E3G Annual Review 2018). This Commission consists of experts and stakeholders from
25
industry, associations, unions, the scientific community, pressure groups and politicians. Its
26
establishment shows that the phasing-out process deserves close attention and that management policies
27
must be implemented to ensure a soft landing for the electricity sector.

The transition towards a high-penetration renewable system also raises concerns over the availability
31
of rare metals for batteries like lithium and cobalt. While metal reserves are unlikely to limit the growth
32
rate or total amount of solar and wind energy, used battery technologies and the known reserves
33
currently being exploited are not compatible with the transition scenario due to insufficient cobalt and
34
lithium reserves (Månberger and Stenqvist 2018). Global lithium production rose by roughly 13 percent
35
from 2016 to 2017 to 43,000 MT in 2018 (Golberg 2021). Africa has rich reserves of lithium and is
36
expected to produce 15% of the world’s supply soon (Rosenberg et al. 2019). Such reserves are found
37
in Zimbabwe, Botswana, Mozambique, Namibia, South Africa (Steenkamp 2017) and the Democratic
38
Republic of Congo (Roker 2018).
39
40
The demand for these resources as ingredients in rechargeable batteries is growing rapidly, with global
41
demand for cobalt set to quadruple to over 190,000tn by 2026. The DRC is a mineral-rich country
42
(Smith et al. 2019a) with rich reserves of fossil fuels (coal and oil) (Buzananakova 2015). The extraction
43
of lithium and cobalt can be environmentally and socially damaging, though its use as a principal
44
component in most rechargeable batteries for electric vehicles and electronic smart grids affords it high
45
sustainability value. Chapter 10 includes a more detailed assessment of the issues with mining these
46
rare metals, as well as the associated social problems, including exploitative working conditions and
47
child labour, the latter a major issue that needs to be taken into consideration in transitions. Recycling
48
batteries is also highlighted as a major supplementary policy if negative environmental side impacts are
49
to be avoided (Rosendahl and Rubiano 2019). In the future, more attention should be paid to reducing
50
vulnerability through subsidizing R&D in rare metals recycling, establishing systems to incentivize the
51
collection of rare-metal waste and promoting technological progress using abundant metals as a
52
replacement for rare metals (Rosendahl and Rubiano 2019).
