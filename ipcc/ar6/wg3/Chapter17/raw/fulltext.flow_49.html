The TWI2050 2019 report (IIASA 2019) assessed both the positive and negative impacts of
1
digitalization in the context of sustainable development. It found that efficiency improvements, reduced
2
resource-consumption and new services can support the SDGs, but also that there were challenges,
3
including in relation to equality, facing the least developed and developing countries because of their
4
low level of access to technologies. The necessary preconditions for successful digital transformation
5
include prosperity, social inclusion, environmental sustainability, protection of jobs and good
6
governance of sustainability transitions. One negative impact of digitalisation could be the rebound
7
effects, where easier access to services could increase demand and with it GHG emissions.
8
Digitalization in the manufacturing sector could also provide a comparative advantage to developed
9
countries due to the falling importance of labour costs, while the barriers to emerging economies
10
seeking to enter global markets could accordingly be increased.

In respect of governance, Krishnan et al. (2020) point out that the creation of synergies between
13
sustainable development and low-emission urbanization based on digitalization could face barriers in
14
the form of inadequate knowledge of structures and value creation through ecosystems that would need
15
to be addressed by means of smart digitalizing, requiring organizational measures to support
16
transformation processes.

Urban areas are one of the main arenas for new digital solutions due to rapid urbanization rates and high
19
concentrations of settlements, businesses and supply systems, which offer great potential for large-scale
20
digital systems. The emergence of smart cities has supported the uptake of smart integrated energy,
21
transportation, water and waste-management systems, while synergies have been created in terms of
22
more flexible and efficient systems. In its 2018 Policy and Action document, the Japanese Business
23
Federation (Keidanren) launched Society 5.0, which include plans for smart city development (Carraz
24
and Yuko 2019; Narvaez Rojas et al. 2021). To achieve smart cities, Society 5.0 aimed to facilitate
25
diverse lifestyles and business success, while the quality of life offered by these options will be
26
enhanced. It also aims to offer high-standard medical and educational services. Autonomous vehicles
27
will be available and integrated with smart grid systems in order to facilitate mobility and flexibility in
28
energy supply with a high share of renewable energy. The energy system will include microgrids,
29
renewable with demand-side controls aligned with local conditions.

Chapter 6 of this report on “Energy Systems” points out that there are many smart energy options with
32
the potential to support sustainable development by facilitating the integration of high shares of
33
fluctuating renewable energy in electricity systems, potentially storing energy in EV batteries or fuel
34
cells, and applying load shifting by varying prices over time. It is concluded that very large efficiency
35
gains are expected to emerge from digitalization in the energy sector (Figure 6.18 in Chapter 6).

Section 9.9.2 in Chapter 9 concludes that the improved energy efficiency and falling costs in the
38
building sector that could result from digitalization could have rebound effects in increasing both energy
39
consumption and comfort levels. Increasing GHG emissions could be the result, but if low-income
40
consumers are given faster access to affordable energy, this could agree with the SDGs, making it
41
desirable to integrate policies targeting mitigation.

Section 10.1.2 in Chapter 10 discusses how the sharing economy, which, for example, could be
44
facilitated by ICT platforms, could influence both mitigation and the SDGs. On the one hand, sharing
45
has the potential to save transport emissions, especially if EVs are supplied with decarbonised grid
46
electricity. However, an increase in transport emissions could result from this if increasing demand and
47
higher comfort levels are facilitated, for example, by making access to EVs relatively easy compared
48
with mass transit. Another possible trade-off is that the supply of public transport services would be
49
limited to the elderly and other user groups.
