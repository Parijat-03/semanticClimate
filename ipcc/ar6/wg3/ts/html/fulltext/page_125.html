TS. 6.4 Investment and finance 1
Finance to reduce net GHG emissions and enhance resilience to climate impacts is a critical 2 enabling factor for the low carbon transition. Fundamental inequities in access to finance as well 3 as finance terms and conditions, and countries’ exposure to physical impacts of climate change 4 overall, result in a worsening outlook for a global just transition (high confidence). Decarbonising 5 the economy requires global action to address fundamental economic inequities and overcome the 6 climate investment trap that exists for many developing countries. For these countries the costs and 7 risks of financing often represent a significant challenge for stakeholders at all levels. This challenge is 8 exacerbated by these countries’ general economic vulnerability and indebtedness. The rising public 9 fiscal costs of mitigation, and of adapting to climate shocks, is affecting many countries and worsening 10 public indebtedness and country credit ratings at a time when there were already significant stresses on 11 public finances. The COVID-19 pandemic has made these stresses worse and tightened public finances 12 still further. Other major challenges for commercial climate finance include: the mismatch between 13 capital and investment needs, home bias23considerations, differences in risk perceptions for regions, as 14 well as limited institutional capacity to ensure safeguards are effective. (high confidence) {15.2, 15.6.3} 15
Investors, central banks, and financial regulators are driving increased awareness of climate risk. 16 This increased awareness can support climate policy development and implementation (high 17 confidence). {15.2, 15.6} Climate-related financial risks arise from physical impacts of climate change 18 (already relevant in the short term), and from a disorderly transition to a low carbon economy. 19 Awareness of these risks is increasing, leading also to concerns about financial stability. Financial 20 regulators and institutions have responded with multiple regulatory and voluntary initiatives to assess 21 and address these risks. Yet despite these initiatives, climate-related financial risks remain greatly 22 underestimated by financial institutions and markets, limiting the capital reallocation needed for the 23 low-carbon transition. Moreover, risks relating to national and international inequity – which act as a 24 barrier to the transformation – are not yet reflected in decisions by the financial community. Stronger 25 steering by regulators and policy makers has the potential to close this gap. Despite the increasing 26 attention of investors to climate change, there is limited evidence that this attention has directly 27 impacted emission reductions. This leaves high uncertainty, both near-term (2021-30) and longer-term 28 (2021-50), on the feasibility of an alignment of financial flows with the Paris Agreement goals (high 29 confidence). {15.2, 15.6} 30
Progress on the alignment of financial flows with low GHG emissions pathways remains slow. 31 There is a climate financing gap which reflects a persistent misallocation of global capital (high 32 confidence) {15.2, 15.3}. Persistently high levels of both public and private fossil-fuel related financing 33 continue to be of major concern despite promising recent commitments. This reflects policy 34 misalignment, the current perceived risk-return profile of fossil fuel-related investments, and political 35 economy constraints (high confidence). Estimates of climate finance flows24exhibit highly divergent 36 patterns across regions and sectors and a slowing growth {15.3}. When the perceived risks are too high, 37 the misallocation of abundant savings persists and investors refrain from investing in infrastructure and 38 industry in search of safer financial assets, even earning low or negative real returns.
