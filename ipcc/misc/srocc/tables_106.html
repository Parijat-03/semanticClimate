These scenarios\nprovide useful thought experiments, but the feasibility of achieving the stylised assumptions is often unknown. Shock scenarios\nexplore the consequences of low probability, high-impact events such as pandemic diseases, cyber-attacks and failures in food supply\nchains (Challinor et al. 2018), often in food security studies. Because of the diversity of exploratory scenarios, attempts have been\nmade to categorise them into ‘archetypes’ based on the similarity between their assumptions in order to facilitate communication\n(IPBES 2018a).\nConditional probabilistic futures explore the consequences of model parameter uncertainty in which these uncertainties are conditional\non scenario assumptions (Neill 2004). Only a few studies have applied the conditional probabilistic approach to land-use futures\n(Brown et al. 2014; Engstrom et al. 2016; Henry et al. 2018). By accounting for uncertainties in key drivers these studies show large\nranges in land-use change, for example, global cropland areas of 893–2380 Mha by the end of the 21st century (Engstrom et al. 2016).\nThey also find that land-use targets may not be achieved, even across a wide range of scenario parameter settings, because of\ntrade-offs arising from the competition for land (Henry et al. 2018; Heck et al. 2018). Accounting for uncertainties across scenario\nassumptions can lead to convergent outcomes for land-use change, which implies that certain outcomes are more robust across\na wide range of uncertain scenario assumptions (Brown et al. 2014).\nIn addition to global scale scenario studies, sub-national studies demonstrate that regional climate change impacts on the land system\nare highly variable geographically because of differences in the spatial patterns of both climate and socio-economic change (Harrison\net al. 2014). Moreover, the capacity to adapt to these impacts is strongly dependent on the regional, socio-economic context and\ncoping capacity (Dunford et al. 2014); processes that are difficult to capture in global scale scenarios. Regional scenarios are often\nco-created with stakeholders through participatory approaches (Kok et al. 2014), which are powerful in reflecting diverse worldviews\nand stakeholder values.
