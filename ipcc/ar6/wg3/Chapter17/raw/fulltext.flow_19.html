There is an emerging modelling literature focusing on the synergies and trade-offs between low-carbon
11
development pathways and various aspects of sustainable development. The early literature, including
12
that on IAMs, and macroeconomic and sectoral models mainly focused on the co-benefits of mitigation
13
policies in terms of reduced air pollution, energy security and to some extent employment generation
14
security (IPCC 2014, 2018c; WGIII AR6 Chapter 6). Some models have been developed further with
15
assessments of a broader range of the joint benefits of mitigation, health, water, land-use and food
16
security (Clarke et al. 2014; IPCC 2014, 2018; Kolstad et al. 2014). According to WGIII AR6 Chapter
17
1, there is a need to incorporate issues and enablers further, including a wide range of non-climate risks,
18
varying forms of innovation, possibilities for behavioural and social change, feasible policies and equity
19
issues (Executive Summary in Chapter 1).

IAMs and macroeconomic models typically calculate mitigation costs based on the assumption that
22
markets internalise externalities like GHG emissions through carbon prices (Barker et al. 2016; IEA
23
2017, 2019). Yet, there are legitimate questions to be asked about whether carbon-pricing will be
24
efficient if markets are inefficient (World Bank 2019). However, market inefficiencies are difficult to
25
integrate into the models. How GHG emissions taxes would actually work is thus quite uncertain based
26
on the modelling studies (Barker et al. 2016; Fontana and Sawyer 2016; Meyer et al. 2018). Despite
27
these limitations, the use of GHG emission taxes as an effective instrument based on modelling results
28
in practice has implications for public policies and private-sector investments.

Despite the shortcomings of conventional economic thought and models, already pointed out, improved
31
models have demonstrated new perspectives on how mitigation costs can be assessed in macroeconomic
32
models. For instance, while a conventional perspective might suggest that climate-change mitigation
33
costs can limit investments in sustainability because they reduce the productivity of capital by
34
increasing energy prices and the products in which energies are embodied, another perspective is that
35
innovation can imply increases in efficiency and that the substitution of energy, material and labour can
36
lead to the accumulation of capital and productivity gains. This appears to occur with innovations in
37
end-use energy applications generating emissions reductions and delivering on other sustainable
38
development benefits (Wilson et al. 2019). Similarly, IAM models have been applied to model the
39
potential for low energy demand scenarios associated with demand-side innovations in the service
40
sector. Grubler et al. (2018) have developed a climate-friendly, low-energy demand (LED) scenario
41
which assumes information technology innovations such as the internet of things (IoT) and induced
42
social changes such as the sharing economy. Nonetheless there are still very important limits on the
43
degree to which highly aggregated IAM models and macroeconomic models can integrate ethics, equity
44
and several other key policy-relevant aspects of sustainable development (Easterlin et al. 2010; Koch
45
2020). A key limitation in this context is that, while all countries share the totality of the SDGs,
46
development priorities differ across countries and over time. Moreover, these priorities are strongly
47
linked to local contexts, and this can only be reflected directly in national models (Section 4.3.2 in
48
Chapter 4).
