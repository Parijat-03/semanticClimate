TS. 4 Mitigation and development pathways 1
While previous WG III assessments have explored mitigation pathways, since AR5 there has been an 2 increasing emphasis in the literature on development pathways, and in particular at the national scale. 3 Chapter 4 assesses near-term (2019-2030) to mid-term (2030- 2050) pathways, complementing Chapter 4 3 which focusses on long-term pathways (up to 2100). While there is considerable literature on country-5 level mitigation pathways, including but not limited to NDCs, the country distribution of this literature 6 is very unequal (high confidence). {4.2.1, Cross-Chapter Box 4 in Chapter 4} 7
TS. 4.1 Mitigation and development pathways in the near- to mid-term 8
An emissions gap persists, exacerbated by an implementation gap, despite mitigation efforts 9 including those in nationally determined contributions (NDCs). In this report the emissions gap is 10 understood as the difference between projected global emissions with national determined contributions 11 (NDCs) in 2030, and emissions in 2030 if mitigation pathways consistent with the Paris temperature 12 goals were achieved. The term implementation gap refers to the gap between NDC mitigation pledges, 13 and the expected outcome of existing policies. 14
Pathways consistent with the implementation and extrapolation of countries’ current11policies 15 see GHG emissions reaching 57 (52-60) GtCO2-eq yr-1by 2030 and to 46-67 GtCO2-eq yr-1by 16 2050, leading to a median global warming of 2.4°C to 3.5°C by 2100 (medium confidence). NDCs 17 with unconditional and conditional elements12lead to 53 (50-57) and 50 (47-55) GtCO2-eq, respectively 18 (medium confidence). {Table 4.1}. This leaves median estimated emissions gaps of 14-23 GtCO2-eq to 19 limit warming to 2°C and 25-34 GtCO2-eq to limit warming to 1.5°C relative to mitigation pathways. 20 (Figure TS.9) {Cross-Chapter Box 4 Figure 1 in Chapter 4} 21
