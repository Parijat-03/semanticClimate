2 FAQ 9.2 | How Much Will Sea Level Rise in the Next Few Decades?
3
4 As of 2018, global average sea level was about 15–25 cm higher than in 1900, and 7–15 cm higher than in 1971.\nSea level will continue to rise by an additional 10–25 cm by 2050. The major reasons for this ongoing rise in sea\nlevel are the thermal expansion of seawater as its temperature increases, and the melting of glaciers and ice\nsheets. Local sea level changes can be larger or smaller than the global average, with the smallest changes in
5 formerly glaciated areas, and the largest changes in low-lying river delta regions.\nAcross the globe, sea level is rising, and the rate of increase has accelerated. Sea level increased by about 4 mm
6 per year from 2006 to 2018, which was more than double the average rate over the 20th century. Rise during\nthe early 1900s was due to natural factors, such as glaciers catching up to warming that occurred in the Northern\nHemisphere during the 1800s. However, since at least 1970, human activities have been the dominant cause of
7 global average sea level rise, and they will continue to be for centuries into the future.\nSea level rises either through warming of ocean waters or the addition of water from melting ice and bodies of
8 water on land. Expansion due to warming caused about 50% of the rise observed from 1971 to 2018. Melting\nglaciers contributed about 22% over the same period. Melting of the two large ice sheets in Greenland and\nAntarctica has contributed about 13% and 7%, respectively, during 1971 to 2018, but melting has accelerated\nin the recent decades, increasing their contribution to 22% and 14% since 2016. Another source is changes in\nland-water storage: reservoirs and aquifers on land have reduced, which contributed about an 8% increase
9 in sea level.\nBy 2050, sea level is expected to rise an additional 10–25 cm whether or not greenhouse gas emissions are reduced
10 (FAQ 9.2, Figure 1). Beyond 2050, the amount by which sea level will rise is more uncertain. The accumulated\ntotal emissions of greenhouse gases over the upcoming decades will play a big role beyond 2050, especially in
11 determining where sea level rise and ice-sheet changes eventually level off.\nEven if net zero emissions are reached, sea level rise will continue because the deep ocean will continue to warm
12 and ice sheets will take time to catch up to the warming caused by past and present emissions: ocean and ice\nsheets are slow to respond to environmental changes (see FAQ 5.3). Some projections under low emissions show\nsea level rise continuing as net zero is approached at a rate comparable to today (3–8 mm per year by 2100 versus\n3–4 mm per year in 2015), while others show substantial acceleration to more than five times the present rate by\n2100, especially if emissions continue to be high and processes that accelerate retreat of the Antarctic Ice Sheet
13 occur widely (FAQ 9.1).\nSea level rise will increase the frequency and severity of extreme sea level events at coasts (see FAQ 8.2), such as
14 storm surges, wave inundation and tidal floods: risk can be increased by even small changes in global average\nsea level. Scientists project that, in some regions, extreme sea level events that were recently expected once in\n100 years will occur annually at 20–25% of locations by 2050 regardless of emissions, but by 2100 emissions choice
15 will matter: annually at 60% of locations for low emissions, and at 80% of locations under strong emissions.\nIn many places, local sea level change will be larger or smaller than the global average. From year to year and
16 place to place, changes in ocean circulation and wind can lead to local sea level change. In regions where large\nice sheets, such as the Fennoscandian in Eurasia and the Laurentide and Cordilleran in North America, covered\nthe land during the last ice age, the land is still slowly rising up now that the extra weight of the ice sheets is\ngone. This local recovery is compensating for global sea level rise in these regions and can even lead to local\ndecrease in sea level. In regions just beyond where the former ice sheets reached and the Earth bulged upwards,\nthe land is now falling and, as a result, local sea level rise is faster than the global rate. In many regions within\nlow-lying delta regions (such as New Orleans and the Ganges–Brahmaputra delta), the land is rapidly subsiding\n(sinking) because of human activities such as building dams or groundwater and fossil fuel extraction. Further,\nwhen an ice sheet melts, it has less gravitational pull on the ocean water nearby. This reduction in gravitational\nattraction causes sea level to fall close to the (now less-massive) ice sheet while causing sea level to rise farther\naway. Melt from a polar ice sheet therefore raises sea level most in the opposite hemisphere or in low latitudes –\namounting to tens of centimetres difference in rise between regions by 2100.
