National mitigation policies interact internationally with effects that both support and hinder 7 mitigation action (medium evidence, high agreement). Reductions in demand for fossil fuels tend to 8 negatively affect fossil fuel exporting countries. Creation of markets for emission reduction credits 9 tends to benefit countries able to supply credits. Policies to support technology development and 10 diffusion tend to have positive spill over effects. There is no consistent evidence of significant emissions 11 leakage or competitiveness effects between countries, including for emissions-intensive trade-exposed 12 industries covered by emission trading systems (medium confidence). {13.6} 13
Policy packages are better able to support socio-technical transitions and shifts in development 14 pathways toward low carbon futures than are individual policies (high confidence). For best effect, 15 they need to be harnessed to a clear vision for change and designed with attention to local governance 16 context. Comprehensiveness in coverage, coherence to ensure complementarity, and consistency of 17 policies with the overarching vision and its objectives are important design criteria. Integration across 18 objectives occurs when a policy package is informed by a clear problem framing and identification of 19 the full range of relevant policy sub-systems. The co-benefits and trade-offs of integrating adaptation and mitigation are most usefully 23 identified and assessed prior to policy making rather than being accidentally discovered (high 24 confidence). This requires strengthening relevant national institutions to reduce silos and overlaps, 25 increasing knowledge exchange at the country and regional levels, and supporting engagement with 26 bilateral and multilateral funding partners. Local governments are well placed to develop policies that 27 generate social and environmental co-benefits but to do so require legal backing and adequate capacity 28 and resources. {13.8} 29
Climate change mitigation is accelerated when attention is given to integrated policy and economy 30 wide approaches, and when enabling conditions (governance, institutions, behaviour and lifestyle, 31 innovation, policy, and finance), are present (robust evidence, medium agreement). Accelerating 32 climate mitigation includes simultaneously weakening high carbon systems and encouraging low 33 carbon systems; ensuring interaction between adjacent systems (e.g., energy and agriculture); 34 overcoming resistance to policies (e.g., from incumbents in high carbon emitting industries), including 35 by providing transitional support to the vulnerable and negatively affected by distributional impacts; 36 inducing changes in consumer practices and routines; providing transition support; and addressing 37 coordination challenges in policy and governance.
