Sub-national actors are important for mitigation because municipalities and regional 1 governments have jurisdiction over climate-relevant sectors such as land-use, waste and urban 2 policy. They are able to experiment with climate solutions and can forge partnerships with the 3 private sector and internationally to leverage enhanced climate action (high confidence). More 4 than 10,500 cities and nearly 250 regions representing more than 2 billion people have pledged largely 5 voluntary action to reduce emissions. Indirect gains include innovation, establishing norms and 6 developing capacity. However, sub-national actors often lack national support, funding, and capacity to 7 mobilize finance and human resources, and create new institutional competences. {13.5} 8
Climate litigation is growing and can affect the outcome and ambition of climate governance 9 (medium evidence, high agreement). Since 2015, at least 37 systemic cases have been initiated against 10 states that challenge the overall effort of a state to mitigate or adapt to climate change. If successful, 11 such cases can lead to an increase in a country’s overall ambition to tackle climate change. Climate 12 litigation has also successfully challenged governments’ authorisations of high-emitting projects setting 13 precedents in favour of climate action. Climate litigation against private sector and financial institutions 14 is also on the rise. {13.4} 15
The media shapes the public discourse about climate mitigation. This can usefully build public 16 support to accelerate mitigation action but may also be used to impede decarbonisation (medium 17 evidence, high agreement). Global media coverage (across a study of 59 countries) has been growing, 18 from about 47,000 articles in 2016-17 to about 87,000 in 2020-21. Generally, the media representation 19 of climate science has increased and become more accurate over time. On occasion, the propagation of 20 scientifically misleading information by organized counter-movements has fuelled polarisation, with 21 negative implications for climate policy. {13.4} 22
Explicit attention to equity and justice is salient to both social acceptance and fair and effective 23 policymaking for mitigation (high confidence). Distributional implications of alternative climate 24 policy choices can be usefully evaluated at city, local and national scales as an input to policymaking. 25 It is anticipated that institutions and governance frameworks that enable consideration of justice and 26 just transitions can build broader support for climate policymaking. {13.2, 13.6, 13.8, 13.9} 27
Carbon pricing is effective in promoting implementation of low-cost emissions reductions (high 28 confidence). While the coverage of emissions trading and carbon taxes has risen to over 20 percent of 29 global CO2 emissions, both coverage and price are lower than is needed for deep reductions. Market 30 mechanisms ideally are designed to be effective as well as efficient, balance distributional goals and 31 find social acceptance. Practical experience has driven progress in market mechanism design, especially 32 of emissions trading schemes. Carbon pricing is limited in its effect on adoption of higher-cost 33 mitigation options, and where decisions are often not sensitive to price incentives such as in energy 34 efficiency, urban planning, and infrastructure (robust evidence, medium agreement). Subsidies have 35 been used to improve energy efficiency, encourage the uptake of renewable energy and other sector-36 specific emissions saving options {13.6} 37
Carbon pricing is most effective if revenues are redistributed or used impartially (high 38 confidence). A carbon levy earmarked for green infrastructures or saliently returned to taxpayers 39 corresponding to widely accepted notions of fairness increases the political acceptability of carbon 40 pricing. {5.6, Box 5.11} 41
Removing fossil fuel subsidies could reduce emissions by 1-10% by 2030 while improving public 42 revenue and macroeconomic performance (robust evidence, medium agreement). {13.6} 43
Regulatory instruments play an important role in achieving specific mitigation outcomes in 44 sectoral applications (high confidence).
