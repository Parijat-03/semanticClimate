2 FAQ 3.3 | Are Climate Models Improving?
3
4 Yes, climate models have improved and continue to do so, becoming better at capturing complex and small-\nscale processes and at simulating present-day mean climate conditions. This improvement can be measured by\ncomparing climate simulations against historical observations. Both the current and previous generations of\nmodels show that increases in greenhouse gases cause global warming. While past warming is well simulated by\nthe new generation models as a group, some individual models simulate past warming that is either below or\nabove what is observed. The information about how well models simulate past warming, as well as other insights
5 from observations and theory, are used to refine this Report’s projections of global warming.\nClimate models are important tools for understanding past, present and future climate change. They are
6 sophisticated computer programs that are based on fundamental laws of physics of the atmosphere, ocean,\nice, and land. Climate models perform their calculations on a three-dimensional grid made of small bricks\nor ‘gridcells’ of about 100 km across. Processes that occur on scales smaller than the model grid cells (such\nas the transformation of cloud moisture into rain) are treated in a simplified way. This simplification is done\ndifferently in different models. Some models include more processes and complexity than others; some represent\nprocesses in finer detail (smaller grid cells) than others. Hence the simulated climate and climate change vary
7 between models.\nClimate modelling started in the 1950s and, over the years, models have become increasingly sophisticated as
8 computing power, observations and our understanding of the climate system have advanced. The models used\nin the IPCC First Assessment Report published in 1990 correctly reproduced many aspects of climate (FAQ 1.1).\nThe actual evolution of the climate since then has confirmed these early projections, when accounting for the\ndifferences between the simulated scenarios and actual emissions. Models continue to improve and get better\nand better at simulating the large variety of important processes that affect climate. For example, many models\nnow simulate complex interactions between different aspects of the Earth system, such as the uptake of carbon\ndioxide by vegetation on land and by the ocean, and the interaction between clouds and air pollutants. While\nsome models are becoming more comprehensive, others are striving to represent processes at higher resolution,\nfor example to better represent the vortices and swirls in currents responsible for much of the transport of heat
9 in the ocean.\nScientists evaluate the performance of climate models by comparing historical climate model simulations to
10 observations. This evaluation includes comparison of large-scale averages as well as more detailed regional and\nseasonal variations. There are two important aspects to consider: (i) how models perform individually and (ii)\nhow they perform as a group. The average of many models often compares better against observations than any\nindividual model, since errors in representing detailed processes tend to cancel each other out in multi-model
11 averages.\nAs an example, FAQ 3.3 Figure 1 compares simulations from the three most recent generations of models (available
12 around 2008, 2013 and 2021) with observations of three climate variables. It shows the correlation between\nsimulated and observed patterns, where a value of 1 represents perfect agreement. Many individual models of\nthe new generation perform significantly better, as indicated by values closer to 1. As a group, each generation\nout-performs the previous generation: the multi-model average (shown by the longer lines) is progressively closer\nto 1. The vertical extent of the colored bars indicates the range of model performance across each group. The\ntop of the bar moves up with each generation, indicating improved performance of the best performing models\nfrom one generation to the next. In the case of precipitation, the performance of the worst performing models

14 chemical and biological processes, as well as higher resolution, have improved the simulation of many aspects\nof the Earth system.
