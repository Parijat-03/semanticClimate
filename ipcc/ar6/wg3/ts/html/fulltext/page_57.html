Many countries, businesses, and individuals stand to lose wealth from stranded assets. Countries, 3 businesses, and individuals may therefore desire to keep assets in operation even if financial, social, or 4 environmental concerns call for retirement. This creates political economic risks, including actions by 5 asset owners to hinder climate policy reform {6.7; Box 6.11}. It will be easier to retire these assets 6 if the risks are communicated, if sustainability reporting is mandated and enforced, and if 7 corporations are protected with arrangements that shield them from short-term shareholder value 8 maximisation. 9
Without early retirements, or reductions in utilisation, the current fossil infrastructure will emit more 10 GHGs than is compatible with limiting warming to 1.5°C {2.7}. Including the pipeline of planned 11 investments would push these future emissions into the uncertainty range of 2°C carbon budgets {2.7}. 12 Continuing to build new coal-fired power plants and other fossil infrastructure will increase future 13 transition costs and may jeopardize efforts to likely limit warming to 2°C or 1.5°C with no or limited 14 overshoot. One study has estimated that USD11.8 trillion in current assets will need to be stranded by 15 2050 for 2°C world; further delaying action for another 10 years would result in an additional USD7.7 16 trillion in stranded assets by 2050. {15.5.2} 17

A low-carbon energy transition will shift investment patterns and create new economic 23 opportunities (high confidence). Total energy investment needs will rise, relative to today, over the 24 next decades, if it is to be likely that warming is limited to 2°C, or if warming is limited to 1.5°C with 25 no or limited overshoot. These increases will be far less pronounced, however, than the reallocations of 26 investment flows that are anticipated across sub-sectors, namely from fossil fuels (extraction, 27 conversion, and electricity generation) without CCS and toward renewables, nuclear power, CCS, 28 electricity networks and storage, and end-use energy efficiency. A significant and growing share of 29 investments between now and 2050 will be made in emerging economies, particularly in Asia. {6.7} 30
Climate change will affect many future local and national low-carbon energy systems. The 31 impacts, however, are uncertain, particularly at the regional scale (high confidence). Climate 32 change will alter hydropower production, bioenergy and agricultural yields, thermal power plant 33 efficiencies, and demands for heating and cooling, and it will directly impact power system 34 infrastructure. Climate change will not affect wind and solar resources to the extent that it would 35 compromise their ability to reduce emissions. {6.5} 36
Electricity systems powered predominantly by renewables will be increasingly viable over the 37 coming decades, but it will be challenging to supply the entire energy system with renewable 38 energy (high confidence). Large shares of variable solar PV and wind power can be incorporated in 39 electricity grids through batteries, hydrogen, and other forms of storage; transmission; flexible non-40 renewable generation; advanced controls; and greater demand-side responses. Because some 41 applications (e.g., aviation) are not currently amenable to electrification, it is anticipated that 100% 42 renewable energy systems will need to include alternative fuels such as hydrogen or biofuels. Economic, 43 regulatory, social, and operational challenges increase with higher shares of renewable electricity and 44 energy. The ability to overcome these challenges in practice is not fully understood. (Box TS.9) {6.6} 45
