0 Box SPM. 1 | Shared Socio-economic Pathways (SSPs)
1
2 In this report the implications of future socio-economic development on climate change mitigation, adaptation and land-use\nare explored using shared socio-economic pathways (SSPs). The SSPs span a range of challenges to climate change mitigation\nand adaptation.
3
4 • SSP1 includes a peak and decline in population (~7 billion in 2100), high income and reduced inequalities, effective land-\nuse regulation, less resource intensive consumption, including food produced in low-GHG emission systems and lower\nfood waste, free trade and environmentally-friendly technologies and lifestyles. Relative to other pathways, SSP1 has low\nchallenges to mitigation and low challenges to adaptation (i.e., high adaptive capacity)
5
6 • SSP2 includes medium population growth (~9 billion in 2100), medium income, technological progress, production and\nconsumption patterns are a continuation of past trends, and only a gradual reduction in inequality occurs. Relative to\nother pathways, SSP2 has medium challenges to mitigation and medium challenges to adaptation (i.e., medium adaptive\ncapacity).
7
8 • SSP3 includes high population growth (~13 billion in 2100), low income and continued inequalities, material-intensive\nconsumption and production, barriers to trade, and slow rates of technological change. Relative to other pathways, SSP3\nhas high challenges to mitigation and high challenges to adaptation (i.e., low adaptive capacity).
9
10 • SSP4 includes medium population growth (~9 billion in 2100), medium income, but significant inequality within and\nacross regions. Relative to other pathways, SSP4 has low challenges to mitigation, but high challenges to adaptation (i.e.,\nlow adaptive capacity).
11
12 • SSP5 includes a peak and decline in population (~7 billion in 2100), high income, reduced inequalities, and free trade. This\npathway includes resource-intensive production, consumption and lifestyles. Relative to other pathways, SSP5 has high\nchallenges to mitigation, but low challenges to adaptation (i.e., high adaptive capacity).
13
14 • The SSPs can be combined with Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs) which imply different levels of mitigation,\nwith implications for adaptation. Therefore, SSPs can be consistent with different levels of global mean surface\ntemperature rise as projected by different SSP-RCP combinations. However, some SSP-RCP combinations are not possible;\nfor instance RCP2.6 and lower levels of future global mean surface temperature rise (e.g., 1.5ºC) are not possible in SSP3\nin modelled pathways. {1.2.2, 6.1.4, Cross-Chapter Box 1 in Chapter 1, Cross-Chapter Box 9 in Chapter 6}
