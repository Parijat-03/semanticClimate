Box TS.14 Digitalisation 2
Digital technologies can promote large increases in energy efficiency through coordination and an 3 economic shift to services, but they can also greatly increase energy demand because of the energy used 4 in digital devices (high confidence). {Cross-Chapter Box 11 in Chapter 16, 16.2} 5
Digital devices, including servers, increase pressure on the environment due to the demand for rare 6 metals and end-of-life disposal. The absence of adequate governance in many countries can lead to 7 harsh working conditions and unregulated disposal of electronic waste. Digitalisation also affects firms' 8 competitiveness, the demand for skills, and the distribution of, and access to resources. The existing 9 digital divide, especially in developing countries, and the lack of appropriate governance of the digital 10 revolution can hamper the role that digitalisation could play in supporting the achievement of stringent 11 mitigation targets. At present, the understanding of both the direct and indirect impacts of digitalisation 12 on energy use, carbon emissions and potential mitigation is limited (medium confidence). 13
The digital transformation is a megatrend that is fundamentally changing all economies and societies, 14 albeit in very different ways depending on the level of development of a given country and on the nature 15 of its economic system. Digital technologies have significant potential to contribute to decarbonisation 16 due to their ability to increase energy and material efficiency, make transport and building systems less 17 wasteful, and improve the access to services for consumers and citizens. Yet, if left unmanaged, the 18 digital transformation will probably increase energy demand, exacerbate inequities and the 19 concentration of power, leaving developing economies with less access to digital technologies behind, 20 raise ethical issues, reduce labour demand and compromise citizens’ welfare. Appropriate governance 21 of the digital transformation can ensure that digitalisation works as an enabler, rather than as a barrier 22 and further strain in decarbonisation pathways. Governance can ensure that digitalisation not only 23 reduces GHG emissions intensity but also contributes to reducing absolute GHG emission, constraining 24 run-away consumption. {Cross-Chapter Box 11 in Chapter 16, 16.2} 25
Digital technologies have the potential to reduce energy demand in all end-use sectors through steep 26 improvements in energy efficiency. This includes material input savings and increased coordination as 27 they allow the use of fewer inputs to perform a given task. Smart appliances and energy management, 28 supported by choice architectures, economic incentives and social norms, effectively reduce energy 29 demand and associated GHG emissions by 5-10% while maintaining equal service levels. Data centres 30 can also play a role in energy system management, for example by waste heat utilisation where district 31 heat systems are close by; temporal and spatial scheduling of electricity demand can provide about 6% 32 of the total potential demand response. {5.5, Cross-Chapter Box 11 Table 1 in Chapter 16} 33
Digital technologies, analytics and connectivity consume large amounts of energy implying higher 34 direct energy demand and related carbon emissions. Global energy demand from digital appliances 35 reached 7.14 EJ in 2018. The demand for computing services increased by 550% between 2010 and 36 2018 and is now estimated at 1% of global electricity consumption. Due to efficiency improvements, 37 the associated energy demand increased only modestly, by about 6% from 2000 to 2018. {Box 9.5} 38
System-wide effects endanger energy and GHG emission savings. Rising demand can diminish energy 39 savings, and also produce run-away effects associated with additional consumption and GHG emissions 40 if left unregulated. Savings are varied in smart and shared mobility systems, as ride hailing increases 41 GHG emissions due to deadheading, whereas shared pooled mobility and shared cycling reduce GHG 42 emissions, as occupancy levels and/or weight per person kilometre transported improve. Systemic 43 effects have wider boundaries of analysis and are more difficult to quantify and investigate but are 44 nonetheless very relevant. Systemic effects tend to have negative impacts, but policies and adequate 45 infrastructures and choice architectures can help manage and contain these. {5.3, 5.4, 5.6} 46
