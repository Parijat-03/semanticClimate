Box TS.1: The COVID-19 pandemic: Impact on emissions and opportunities for mitigation 20
The COVID-19 pandemic triggered the deepest global economic contraction as well as CO2 emission 21 reductions since the Second World War {2.2.2}. While emissions and most economies rebounded in 22 2020, some impacts of the pandemic could last well beyond this. Owing to the very recent nature of this 23 event, it remains unclear what the exact short and long-term impacts on global emissions drivers, trends, 24 macroeconomics and finance will be. 25
Starting in the spring of 2020 a major break in global emissions trends was observed due to lockdown 26 policies implemented in response to the pandemic. Overall, global CO2-FFI emissions are estimated to 27 have declined by 5.8% (5.1%-6.3%) in 2020, or about 2.2 (1.9-2.4) GtCO2 in total. This exceeds any 28 previous global emissions decline since 1970 both in relative and absolute terms (Box TS.1 Figure 1). 29 During periods of economic lockdown, daily emissions, estimated based on activity and power-30 generation data, declined substantially compared to 2019, particularly in April 2020 –as shown in Box 31 TS.1 Figure 1 – but rebounded by the end of 2020. Impacts were differentiated by sector, with road 32 transport and aviation particularly affected. Different databases estimate the total power sector CO2 33 reduction from 2019 to 2020 at 3% (IEA7) and 4.5% (EDGAR8). Approaches that predict near real-time 34 estimates of the power sector reduction are more uncertain and estimates range more widely between 35 1.8%, 4.1% and 6.8%, the latter taking into account the over-proportional reduction of coal generation 36 due to low gas prices and merit order effects. 37
