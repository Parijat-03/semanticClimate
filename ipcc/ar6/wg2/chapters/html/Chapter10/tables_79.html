2 FAQ 10.3 | How are Indigenous knowledge and local knowledge being incorporated in the design and
3 implementation of adaptation projects and policies in Asia?\nIndigenous People, comprising about 6% of the global population, play a crucial role in managing climate change for two important reasons.
4 First, they have a physical and spiritual connection with land, water and associated ecosystems, thus making them most vulnerable to any
5 environmental and climatic changes. Second, their ecological and local knowledge are relevant to finding solutions to climate change.\nIndigenous knowledge and local knowledge (IKLK) play an important role in the formulation of adaptation
6 governance and related strategies (IPCC 2007), and best quality, locality-specific knowledge can help address the\nserious lack of education on climate change and uncertainties surrounding quality, salience, credibility and the
7 legitimacy of the available knowledge base.\nKey findings across Asia underline the importance of building, sustaining and augmenting local capacity through
8 addressing inadequacies in terms of resource base, climate-change awareness, government–community partnerships\nand vulnerability assessment. Furthermore, inclusion of as well as related practices will improve adaptation planning\nand decision-making processes concerning climate change.
