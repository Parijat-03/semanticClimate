2 FAQ 5.2 | Can Thawing Permafrost Substantially Increase Global Warming?
3
4 In the Arctic, large amounts of organic carbon are stored in permafrost – ground that remains frozen throughout\nthe year. If significant areas of permafrost thaw as the climate warms, some of that carbon may be released\ninto the atmosphere in the form of carbon dioxide or methane, resulting in additional warming. Projections\nfrom models of permafrost ecosystems suggest that future permafrost thaw will lead to some additional\nwarming – enough to be important, but not enough to lead to a ‘runaway warming’ situation, where permafrost
5 thaw leads to a dramatic, self-reinforcing acceleration of global warming.\nThe Arctic is the biggest climate-sensitive carbon pool on Earth, storing twice as much carbon in its frozen soils,
6 or permafrost, than is currently stored in the atmosphere. As the Arctic region warms faster than anywhere else\non Earth, there are concerns that this warming could release greenhouse gases to the atmosphere and therefore
7 significantly amplify climate change.\nThe carbon in the permafrost has built up over thousands of years, as dead plants have been buried and
8 accumulated within layers of frozen soil, where the cold prevents the organic material from decomposing. As\nthe Arctic warms and soils thaw, the organic matter in these soils begins to decompose rapidly and return to the\natmosphere as either carbon dioxide or methane, which are both important greenhouse gases. Permafrost can\nalso thaw abruptly in a given place, due to melting ice in the ground reshaping Arctic landscapes, lakes growing\nand draining, and fires burning away insulating surface soil layers. Thawing of permafrost carbon has already\nbeen observed in the Arctic, and climate models project that much of the shallow permafrost (<3 m depth)
9 throughout the Arctic would thaw under moderate to high amounts of global warming (2°C–4°C).\nWhile permafrost processes are complex, they are beginning to be included in models that represent the
10 interactions between the climate and the carbon cycle. The projections from these permafrost carbon models show\na wide range in the estimated strength of a carbon–climate vicious circle, from both carbon dioxide and methane,\nequivalent to 14–175 billion tonnes of carbon dioxide released per 1°C of global warming. By comparison, in\n2019, human activities have released about 40 billion tonnes of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. This has two\nimplications. First, the extra warming caused by permafrost thawing is strong enough that it must be considered\nwhen estimating the total amount of remaining emissions permitted to stabilize the climate at a given level of\nglobal warming (i.e., the remaining carbon budget, see FAQ 5.4). Second, the models do not identify any one\namount of warming at which permafrost thaw becomes a ‘tipping point’ or threshold in the climate system\nthat would lead to a runaway global warming. However, models do project that emissions would continuously
11 increase with warming, and that this trend could last for hundreds of years.\nPermafrost can also be found in other cold places (e.g., mountain ranges), but those places contain much less
12 carbon than in the Arctic. For instance, the Tibetan plateau contains about 3% as much carbon as is stored in\nthe Arctic. There is also concern about carbon frozen in shallow ocean sediments. These deposits are known as\nmethane hydrates or clathrates, which are methane molecules locked within a cage of ice molecules. They formed\nas frozen soils that were flooded when sea levels rose after the last ice age. If these hydrates thaw, they may\nrelease methane that can bubble up to the surface. The total amount of carbon in permafrost-associated\nmethane hydrates is much less than the carbon in permafrost soils. Global warming takes millennia to penetrate\ninto the sediments beneath the ocean, which is why these hydrates are still responding to the last deglaciation.\nAs a result, only a small fraction of the existing hydrates could be destabilised during the coming century. Even\nwhen methane is released from hydrates, most of it is expected to be consumed and oxidised into carbon dioxide\nin the ocean before reaching the atmosphere. The most complete modelling of these processes to date suggests
13 a release to the atmosphere at a rate of less than 2% of current human-induced methane emissions.\nOverall, thawing permafrost in the Arctic appears to be an important additional source of heat-trapping gases
14 to the atmosphere, more so than undersea hydrates. Climate and carbon cycle models are beginning to consider\npermafrost processes. While these models disagree on the exact amount of the heat-trapping gases that will be\nreleased into the atmosphere, they agree that: (i) the amount of such gases released from permafrost will increase\nwith the amount of global warming; and (ii) the warming effect of thawing permafrost is significant enough to\nbe considered in estimates of the remaining carbon budgets for limiting future warming.
