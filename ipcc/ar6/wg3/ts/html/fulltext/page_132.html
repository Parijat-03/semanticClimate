Experience and analyses show that technological change is inhibited if technological innovation 8 system functions are not adequately fulfilled; this inhibition occurs more often in developing 9 countries (high confidence). Examples of such functions are knowledge development, resource 10 mobilisation, and activities that shape the needs, requirements and expectations of actors within the 11 innovation system (guidance of the search). Capabilities play a key role in these functions, the build-up 12 of which can be enhanced by domestic measures, but also by international cooperation. For instance, 13 innovation cooperation on wind energy has contributed to the accelerated global spread of this 14 technology. As another example, the policy guidance by the Indian government, which also promoted 15 development of data, testing capabilities and knowledge within the private sector, has been a key 16 determinant of the success of an energy-efficiency programme for air conditioners and refrigerators in 17 India. {16.3, 16.5, 16.6, Cross-Chapter Box 12 in Chapter 16, Box 16.3} 18
Consistent with innovation system approaches, the sharing of knowledge and experiences 19 between developed and developing countries can contribute to addressing global climate and the 20 SDGs. The effectiveness of such international cooperation arrangements, however, depends on 21 the way they are developed and implemented (high confidence). The effectiveness and sustainable 22 development benefits of technology sharing under market conditions appears to be determined primarily 23 by the complexity of technologies, local capabilities and the policy regime. This suggests that the 24 development of planning and innovation capabilities remains necessary, especially in least-developed 25 countries and SIDS. International diffusion of low-emission technologies is also facilitated by 26 knowledge spill overs from regions engaged in clean R&D (medium confidence). {16.2} 27
