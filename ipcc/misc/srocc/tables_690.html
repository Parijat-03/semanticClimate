2 Climate change and sustainable development pathways
3 “Climate change poses a moderate threat to current sustainable development and a severe threat to future sustainable development”
4 (Denton et al. 2014; Fleurbaey et al. 2014).
5 Significant transformations may be required for climate-resilient pathways (Denton et al. 2014; Jones et al. 2014).
6 The design of climate policy is influenced by (i) differing ways that individuals and organisations perceive risks and uncertainties, and\n(ii) the consideration of a diverse array of risks and uncertainties – as well as human and social responses – which may be difficult\nto measure, are of low probability but which would have a significant impact if they occurred (Kunreuther et al. 2014; Fleurbaey et al.\n2014; Kolstad et al. 2014).
7 Building climate-resilient pathways requires iterative, continually evolving and complementary processes at all levels of government\n(Denton et al. 2014; Kunreuther et al. 2014; Kolstad et al. 2014; Somanthan et al. 2014; Lavell et al. 2012).
8 Important aspects of climate-resilient policies include local level institutions, decentralisation, participatory governance, iterative\nlearning, integration of local knowledge, and reduction of inequality (Dasgupta et al. 2014; Lavell et al. 2012; Cutter et al. 2012b;\nO’Brien et al. 2012; Roy et al. 2018).
9 Climate action and sustainable development are linked: adaptation has co-benefits for sustainable development, while “sustainable\ndevelopment supports, and often enables, the fundamental societal and systems transitions and transformations that help limit\nglobal warming” (IPCC 2018a). Redistributive policies that shield the poor and vulnerable can resolve trade-offs between mitigation\nobjectives and the hunger, poverty and energy access SDGs.

11 Land and rural livelihoods
12 Policies and institutions relating to land, including land tenure, can contribute to the vulnerability of rural people, and constrain
13 adaptation. Climate policies, such as encouraging cultivation of biofuels, or payments under REDD+, will have significant secondary\nimpacts, both positive and negative, in some rural areas (Dasgupta et al. 2014).
14 “Sustainable land management is an effective disaster risk reduction tool” (Cutter et al. 2012a).
15
16 Risk and risk management
17 A variety of emergent risks not previously assessed or recognised, can be identified by taking into account: (i) the “interactions
18 of climate change impacts on one sector with changes in exposure and vulnerability, as well as adaptation and mitigation actions”,\nand (ii) “indirect, trans-boundary, and long-distance impacts of climate change” including price spikes, migration, conflict and the\nunforeseen impacts of mitigation measures (Oppenheimer et al. 2014).
19 “Under any plausible scenario for mitigation and adaptation, some degree of risk from residual damages is unavoidable” (Oppenheimer\net al. 2014).

21 Decision-making
22 “Risk management provides a useful framework for most climate change decision-making. Iterative risk management is most suitable
23 in situations characterised by large uncertainties, long time frames, the potential for learning over time, and the influence of both\nclimate as well as other socio-economic and biophysical changes” (Jones et al. 2014).
24 “Decision support is situated at the intersection of data provision, expert knowledge, and human decision making at a range of scales\nfrom the individual to the organisation and institution” (Jones et al. 2014).
25 “Scenarios are a key tool for addressing uncertainty”, either through problem exploration or solution exploration (Jones et al. 2014).
