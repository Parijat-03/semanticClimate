17.4.4
Institutional capacities and multi-level governance 25
Capable institutions and multi-level governance often support the inter-agency coordination and
26
stakeholder coalitions that drive sustainable transitions. Such institutions and governance arrangements
27
are frequently required to formulate and implement the multi-sectoral policies that spur the adoption
28
and scaling of innovative solutions to climate change and other sustainable development challenges.
29
For example, such institutional and governance conditions have helped support the industrial policies
30
that will be needed to spread renewables through the creation of domestic supply chains (Zenghelis
31
2020) or to pilot CDR methods (Quarton and Samsatli 2020).
32
However, government agencies with climate and other remits do not always work well together: the
33
absence of coordination and consensus building mechanisms can further deepen inter-agency conflicts
34
that stall a transition. These challenges appear not only within but also between levels of decision-
35
making. Studies of developing megacities, for instance, have found the lack of mechanisms promoting
36
vertical cross-level integration to be a sizable constraint on decarbonisation (Canitez 2019). Differences
37
in perspectives across non-state actors can similarly frustrate transitions in areas such as green buildings
38
(Song et al. 2020).
39
40
Here coordination complicates matters: coalition-building may require mutually reinforcing changes to
41
institutions and policies. For example, decentralized renewable energy has made progress in Argentina,
42
but consumer electricity subsidies give agencies and firms supporting conventional energy an advantage
43
over those promoting renewable energy. Similarly, the lack of concrete guidance in green finance
44
policies can deprive government agencies and other stakeholders of the information needed to balance
45
ecological and financial goals (Wang and Zhi 2016). Many of these challenges can be particularly
46
formidable in developing countries, where agencies lack sufficient financial and other capacities. A lack
47
of government funds to cover ongoing maintenance costs along with resource shortages in rural
48
locations can pose constraints on sustainable energy (Schaube et al. 2018).
