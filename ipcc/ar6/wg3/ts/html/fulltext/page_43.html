Each of the IMPs comprises: a storyline and a quantitative illustration. The storyline describes the key 1 characteristics of the pathway qualitatively; the quantitative illustration is selected from the literature 2 on long-term scenarios to effectively represent the IMP numerically. The five Illustrative Mitigation 3 Pathways (IMPs) each emphasise a different scenario element as its defining feature, and are named 4 accordingly: heavy reliance on renewables (IMP-Ren), strong emphasis on low demand for energy 5 (IMP-LD), extensive use of Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR) in the energy and the industry sectors to 6 achieve net negative emissions (IMP-Neg), mitigation in the context of broader sustainable 7 development and shifting development pathways (IMP-SP), and the implications of a less rapid and 8 gradual strengthening of near-term mitigation actions (IMP-GS). In some cases, sectoral chapters may 9 use different quantifications that follow the same storyline narrative but contain data that better 10 exemplify the chapter’s assessment. Some IMP variants are also used to explore the sensitivity around 11 alternative temperature goals. {3.2, 3.3} 12
The two additional pathways illustrative of higher emissions are current policies (CurPol) and moderate 13 action (ModAct). 14

23 The Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) 24
First published in 2017, the Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) are alternative projections of 25 socio-economic developments that may influence future GHG emissions. 26
The initial set of SSP narratives described worlds with different challenges to mitigation and adaptation: 27 SSP1 (sustainability), SSP2 (middle of the road), SSP3 (regional rivalry), SSP4 (inequality) and SSP5 28 (rapid growth).
