TS. 6.2 International cooperation 1
International cooperation is having positive and measurable results (high confidence). The Kyoto 2 Protocol led to measurable and substantial avoided emissions, including in 20 countries with Kyoto first 3 commitment period targets that have experienced a decade of declining absolute emissions. It also built 4 national capacity for GHG accounting, catalysed the creation of GHG markets, and increased 5 investments in low-carbon technologies. Other international agreements and institutions have led to 6 avoided CO2 emissions from land-use practices, as well as avoided emissions of some non-CO2 7 greenhouse gases. (medium confidence) {14.3, 14.5, 14.6} 8
New forms of international cooperation have emerged since AR5 in line with an evolving 9 understanding of effective mitigation policies, processes, and institutions. Both new and pre-10 existing forms of co-operation are vital for achieving climate mitigation goals in the context of 11 sustainable development (high confidence). While previous IPCC assessments have noted important 12 synergies between the outcomes of climate mitigation and achieving sustainable development 13 objectives, there now appear to be synergies between the two processes themselves (medium 14 confidence). Since AR5, international cooperation has shifted towards facilitating national level 15 mitigation action through numerous channels, including though processes established under the 16 UNFCCC regime and through regional and sectoral agreements and organisations. {14.2, 14.3, 14.5, 17 14.6} 18
Participation in international agreements and transboundary networks is associated with the 19 adoption of climate policies at the national and sub-national levels, as well as by non-state actors 20 (high confidence). International cooperation helps countries achieve long-term mitigation targets when 21 it supports development and diffusion of low-carbon technologies, often at the level of individual 22 sectors, which can simultaneously lead to significant benefits in the areas of sustainable development 23 and equity (medium confidence). {14.2, 14.3, 14.5, 14.6} 24
International cooperation under the UN climate regime took an important new direction with the 25 entry into force of the 2015 Paris Agreement, which strengthened the objective of the UN climate 26 regime, including its long-term temperature goal, while adopting a different architecture to that 27 of the Kyoto Protocol (high confidence). The core national commitments under the Kyoto Protocol 28 were legally binding quantified emission targets for developed countries tied to well-defined 29 mechanisms for monitoring and enforcement. By contrast, the commitments under the Paris Agreement 30 are primarily procedural, extend to all parties, and are designed to trigger domestic policies and 31 measures, enhance transparency, and stimulate climate investments, particularly in developing 32 countries, and to lead iteratively to rising levels of ambition across all countries. Issues of equity remain 33 of central importance in the UN climate regime, notwithstanding shifts in the operationalisation of 34 ‘common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities’ from Kyoto to Paris. {14.3} 35
There are conflicting views on whether the Paris Agreement’s commitments and mechanisms will 36 lead to the attainment of its stated goals (medium confidence). Arguments in support of the Paris 37 Agreement are that the processes it initiates and supports will in multiple ways lead, and indeed have 38 already led, to rising levels of ambition over time. The recent proliferation of national mid-century net 39 zero GHG targets can be attributed in part to the Paris Agreement. Moreover, its processes and 40 commitments will enhance countries’ abilities to achieve their stated level of ambition, particularly 41 among developing countries. Arguments against the Paris Agreement are that it lacks a mechanism to 42 review the adequacy of individual Parties’ nationally determined contributions (NDCs), that 43 collectively current NDCs are inconsistent in their level of ambition with achieving the Paris 44 Agreement’s long-term temperature goal, that its processes will not lead to sufficiently rising levels of 45 ambition in the NDCs, and that NDCs will not be achieved because the targets, policies and measures 46 they contain are not legally binding at the international level.
