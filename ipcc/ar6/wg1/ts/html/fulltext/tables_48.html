3 Based on multiple lines of evidence using interhemispheric gradients of CO concentrations, isotopes, and inventory data, it is\n2\nunequivocal that the growth in CO in the atmosphere since 1750 (see Section TS.2.2) is due to the direct emissions from human\n2\nactivities. The combustion of fossil fuels and land-use change for the period 1750–2019 resulted in the release of 700 ± 75 PgC (likely\nrange, 1 PgC = 1015 g of carbon) to the atmosphere, of which about 41% ± 11% remains in the atmosphere today (high confidence).\nOf the total anthropogenic CO emissions, the combustion of fossil fuels was responsible for about 64% ± 15%, growing to an 86% ±\n2\n14% contribution over the past 10 years. The remainder resulted from land-use change. During the last decade (2010–2019), average\nannual anthropogenic CO emissions reached the highest levels in human history at 10.9 ± 0.9 PgC yr–1 (high confidence). Of these\n2\nemissions, 46% accumulated in the atmosphere (5.1 ± 0.02 PgC yr–1), 23% (2.5 ± 0.6 PgC yr–1) was taken up by the ocean and 31%\n(3.4 ± 0.9 PgC yr–1) was removed by terrestrial ecosystems (high confidence). {5.2.1, 5.2.2, 5.2.3}

5 The ocean (high confidence) and land (medium confidence) sinks of CO have increased with anthropogenic emissions over the past\n2\nsix decades (Box TS.5, Figure 1). This coherence between emissions and the growth in ocean and land sinks has resulted in the airborne\nfraction of anthropogenic CO remaining at 44 ± 10% over the past 60 years (high confidence). Interannual and decadal variability of\n2\nthe ocean and land sinks indicate that they are sensitive to changes in the growth rate of emissions as well as climate variability and\nare therefore also sensitive to climate change (high confidence). {5.2.1}

7 The land CO sink is driven by carbon uptake by vegetation, with large interannual variability, for example, linked to the El Niño–\n2\nSouthern Oscillation (ENSO). Since the 1980s, carbon fertilization from rising atmospheric CO has increased the strength of the net\n2\nland CO sink (medium confidence). During the historical period, the growth of the ocean sink has been primarily determined by\n2\nthe growth rate of atmospheric CO. However, there is medium confidence that changes to physical and chemical processes in the\n2\nocean and in the land biosphere, which govern carbon feedbacks, are already modifying the characteristics of variability, particularly\nthe seasonal cycle of CO, in both the ocean and land. However, changes to the multi-decadal trends in the sinks have not yet been\n2\nobserved. {2.3.4, 3.6.1, 5.2.1}
