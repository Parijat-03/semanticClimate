Box TS.7 The Long-term Economic Benefits of Mitigation from Avoided Climate Change 2 Impacts 3
Integrated studies use either a cost-effectiveness analysis (CEA) approach (minimising the total 4 mitigation costs of achieving a given policy goal) or a cost-benefit analysis (CBA) approach 5 (balancing the cost and benefits of climate action). In the majority of studies that have produced the 6 body of work on the cost of mitigation assessed in this report, a CEA approach is adopted, and the 7 feedbacks of climate change impacts on the economic development pathways are not accounted for. 8 This omission of climate impacts leads to overly optimistic economic projections in the reference 9 scenarios, in particular in reference scenarios with no or limited mitigation action where the extent of 10 global warming is the greatest. Mitigation cost estimates computed against no or limited policy 11 reference scenarios therefore omit economic benefits brought by avoided climate change impact along 12 mitigation pathways. {1.7, 3.6.1} 13
The difference in aggregate economic impacts from climate change between two given temperature 14 levels represents the aggregate economic benefits arising from avoided climate change impacts due to 15 mitigation action. Estimates of these benefits vary widely, depending on the methodology used and 16 impacts included, as well as on assumed socioeconomic development conditions, which shape exposure 17 and vulnerability. The aggregate economic benefits of avoiding climate impacts increase with the 18 stringency of the mitigation. Global economic impact studies with regional estimates find large 19 differences across regions, with developing and transitional economies typically more vulnerable. 20 Furthermore, avoided impacts for poorer households and poorer countries represent a smaller share in 21 aggregate quantifications expressed in GDP terms or monetary terms, compared to their influence on 22 well-being and welfare (high confidence). {3.6.2, Cross-Working Group Box 1 in Chapter 3} 23
CBA analysis and CBA integrated assessment models remain limited in their ability to represent all 24 damages from climate change, including non-monetary damages, and capture the uncertain and 25 heterogeneous nature of damages and the risk of catastrophic damages, such that other lines of evidence 26 should be considered in decision-making. However, emerging evidence suggests that, even without 27 accounting for co-benefits of mitigation on other sustainable development dimensions, the global 28 benefits of pathways likely to limit warming to 2°C outweigh global mitigation costs over the 21st 29 century (medium confidence). Depending on the study, the reason for this result lies in assumptions of 30 economic damages from climate change in the higher end of available estimates, in the consideration 31 of risks of tipping-points or damages to natural capital and non-market goods, or in the combination of 32 updated representations of carbon cycle and climate modules, updated damage estimates and updated 33 representations of economic and mitigation dynamics. In the studies that perform a sensitivity analysis, 34 this result is found to be robust to a wide range of assumptions on social preferences (in particular on 35 inequality aversion and pure rate of time preference), and holds except if assumptions of economic 36 damages from climate change are in the lower end of available estimates and the pure rate of time 37 preference is in the higher range of values usually considered (typically above 1.5%). However, 38 although such pathways bring overall net benefits over time (in terms of aggregate discounted present 39 value), they involve distributional consequences between and within generations. {3.6.2} 40
