1 Sustainable sourcing ND ND Mitigation: No studies available to assess the global impact (Table 6.19). Adaptation: Moderate benefits by\ndiversifying and increasing flexibility in the food system to climate stressors and shocks while simultaneously creating\neconomic alternatives for the poor (thereby strengthening adaptive capacity) and lowering expenditures of food\nprocessors and retailers by reducing losses (Muller et al. 2017) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.27). Desertification: No studies\navailable to assess the global impact (Table 6.35 and Table 6.43). Land degradation: Potentially large benefits, as\nover 4 Mkm2 is currently certified for sustainable forest production, which could increase in future (Table 6.44). Food\nsecurity: Moderate benefits by diversifying markets and developing value-added products in the food supply system, by\nincreasing its economic performance and revenues to local farmers (Reidsma et al. 2010), by strengthening the capacity\nof food production chains to adapt to future markets and to improve income of smallholder farmers (Murthy and Madhava\nNaidu 2012) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.51). It may also provide more direct links between producers and consumers.
2 Management of\nsupply chains ND ND ND Mitigation: There are no studies assessing the mitigation potential globally (Table 6.19). Adaptation: Large benefits by\nimproving resilience to price increases or reducing volatility of production (Fafchamps et al. 1998; Haggblade et al. 2017)\n(Table 6.27). Desertification and land degradation: No studies assessing global potential (Tables 6.35 and 6.43). Food\nsecurity: Moderate benefits through helping to manage food price increases and volatility (Vellakkal et al. 2015; Arndt\net al. 2016) (Table 6.51).
3 Enhanced urban\nfood systems ND ND ND ND There are no studies that assess the global potential to contribute to mitigation, adaptation, desertification\nor land degradation (Tables 6.19, 6.27, 6.35 and 6.43). Food security: Large benefits by increasing food access\nto urban dwellers and shortening of supply chains (Chappell et al. 2016) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.51).
4 Improved food\nprocessing and\nretailing ND ND Mitigation: Moderate benefits through reduced energy consumption, climate-friendly foods and reduced GHG emis-\nsions from transportation (Avetisyan et al. 2014), waste (Porter et al. 2016), and energy use (Mohammadi et al. 2014;\nSong et al. 2017) (Table 6.19). Adaptation: Large benefits among poor farmers through reduced costs and improved\nresilience (Table 6.27). Desertification and land degradation: There are no studies assessing global potential\n(Tables 6.35 and Table 6.43). Food security: Large benefits by supporting healthier diets and reducing food loss\nand waste (Garnett 2011) (Chapter 5 and Table 6.51).
5 Improved energy\nuse in food systems ND ND Mitigation: Moderate benefits by reducing GHG emissions through decreasing use of fossil fuels and energy-intensive\nproducts, though the emission reduction is not accounted for in the agriculture, forestry and other land-use (AFOLU)\nsector (Smith et al. 2014; IPCC AR5 WG3 Chapter 11) (Table 6.19). Adaptation: Large benefits for small farmers by\nreducing costs and increasing their resilience to climate change (Table 6.27). Desertification and land degradation:\nThere are no studies assessing global potential (Tables 6.35 and 6.43). Food security: Large benefits, largely by\nimproving efficiency for 2.5 million people still using traditional biomass for energy (Chapter 5 and Table 6.51).
