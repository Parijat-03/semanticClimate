2 FAQ 11.1 | How Do Changes In Climate Extremes Compare With Changes In\nClimate Averages?
3
4 Human-caused climate change alters the frequency and intensity of climate variables (e.g., surface temperature)\nand phenomena (e.g., tropical cyclones) in a variety of ways. We now know that the ways in which average and\nextreme conditions have changed (and will continue to change) depend on the variable and the phenomenon\nbeing considered. Changes in local surface temperature extremes closely follow the corresponding changes in\nlocal average surface temperatures. On the contrary, changes in precipitation extremes (heavy precipitation)\ngenerally do not follow those in average precipitation, and can even move in the opposite direction (e.g., with
5 average precipitation decreasing but extreme precipitation increasing).\nClimate change will manifest very differently depending on which region, season and variable we are interested
6 in. For example, over some parts of the Arctic, temperatures will warm at rates about three to four times higher\nduring winter compared to summer months. And in summer, most of northern Europe will experience larger\ntemperatures increases than most places in south-east South America and Australasia, with differences that can\nbe larger than 1°C, depending on the level of global warming. In general, differences across regions and seasons
7 arise because the underlying physical processes differ drastically across regions and seasons.\nClimate change will also manifest differently for different weather regimes and can lead to contrasting changes
8 in average and extreme conditions. Observations of the recent past and climate model projections show that,\nin most places, changes in daily temperatures are dominated by a general warming where the climatological\naverage and extreme values are shifted towards higher temperatures, making warm extremes more frequent and\ncold extremes less frequent. The top panels in FAQ 11.1, Figure 1 show projected changes in surface temperature\nfor long-term average conditions (left) and for extreme hot days (right) during the warm season (summer in mid-\nto high latitudes). Projected increases in long-term average temperature differ substantially between different\nplaces, varying from less than 3°C in some places in central South Asia and southern South America to over 7°C\nin some places in North America, North Africa and the Middle East. Changes in extreme hot days follow changes\nin average conditions quite closely, although, in some places, the warming rates for extremes can be intensified\n(e.g., southern Europe and the Amazon basin) or weakened (e.g., northern Asia and Greenland) compared to
9 average values.\nRecent observations and global and regional climate model projections point to changes in precipitation
10 extremes (including both rainfall and snowfall extremes) differing drastically from those in average precipitation.\nThe bottom panels in FAQ 11.1, Figure 1 show projected changes in the long-term average precipitation (left)\nand in heavy precipitation (right). Averaged precipitation changes show striking regional differences, with\nsubstantial drying in places such as southern Europe and northern South America and wetting in places such as\nthe Middle East and southern South America. Changes in extreme precipitation are much more uniform, with\nsystematic increases over nearly all land regions. The physical reasons behind the different responses of averaged\nand extreme precipitation are now well understood. The intensification of extreme precipitation is driven by\nthe increase in atmospheric water vapour (about 7% per 1°C of warming near the surface), although this is\nmodulated by various dynamical changes. In contrast, changes in average precipitation are driven not only by\nmoisture increases but also by slower processes that constrain future changes over the globe to only 2–3% per
11 1°C of warming near the surface.\nIn summary, the specific relationship between changes in average and extreme conditions strongly depends on
12 the variable or phenomenon being considered. At the local scale, average and extreme surface temperature\nchanges are strongly related, while average and extreme precipitation changes are often weakly related. For\nboth variables, the changes in average and extreme conditions vary strongly across different places due to the\neffect of local and regional processes.
