2 FAQ 7.3 | What Is Equilibrium Climate Sensitivity and How Does It Relate to Future Warming?

4 For a given future scenario, climate models project a range of changes in global surface temperature. This range\nis closely related to equilibrium climate sensitivity, or ECS, which measures how climate models respond to\na doubling of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. Models with high climate sensitivity project stronger future\nwarming. Some climate models of the new generation are more sensitive than the range assessed in the IPCC\nSixth Assessment Report. This leads to end-of-century global warming in some simulations of up to 2°C–3°C\nabove the current IPCC best estimate. Although these higher warming levels are not expected to occur, high-ECS
5 models are useful for exploring low-likelihood, high-impact futures.\nThe equilibrium climate sensitivity (ECS) is defined as the long-term global warming caused by a doubling of
6 carbon dioxide above its pre-industrial concentration. For a given emissions scenario, much of the uncertainty in\nprojections of future warming can be explained by the uncertainty in ECS (FAQ 7.3, Figure 1). The significance\nof equilibrium climate sensitivity has long been recognized, and the first estimate was presented by Swedish
7 scientist Svante Arrhenius in 1896.\nThis Sixth Assessment Report concludes that there is a 90% or more chance (very likely) that the ECS is between
8 2°C and 5°C. This represents a significant reduction in uncertainty compared to the Fifth Assessment Report,\nwhich gave a 66% chance (likely) of ECS being between 1.5°C and 4.5°C. This reduction in uncertainty has been\npossible not through a single breakthrough or discovery but instead by combining evidence from many different
9 sources and by better understanding their strengths and weaknesses.\nThere are four main lines of evidence for ECS.
10 • The self-reinforcing processes, called feedback loops, that amplify or dampen the warming in response to
11 increasing carbon dioxide are now better understood. For example, warming in the Arctic melts sea ice,\nresulting in more open ocean area, which is darker and therefore absorbs more sunlight, further intensifying\nthe initial warming. It remains challenging to represent realistically all the processes involved in these\nfeedback loops, particularly those related to clouds (see FAQ 7.2). Such identified model errors are now taken\ninto account, and other known, but generally weak, feedback loops that are typically not included in models
12 are now included in the assessment of ECS.\n• Historical warming since early industrialisation provides strong evidence that climate sensitivity is not small.
13 Since 1850, the concentrations of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases have increased, and as a result\nthe Earth has warmed by about 1.1°C. However, relying on this industrial-era warming to estimate ECS is\nchallenging, partly because some of the warming from greenhouse gases was offset by cooling from aerosol
14 particles and partly because the ocean is still responding to past increases in carbon dioxide.\n• Evidence from ancient climates that had reached equilibrium with greenhouse gas concentrations, such as the
15 coldest period of the last ice age around 20,000 years ago, or warmer periods further back in time, provide
16 useful data on the ECS of the climate system (see FAQ 1.3).\n• Statistical approaches linking model ECS values with observed changes, such as global warming since the
17
18 1970s, provide complementary evidence.\nAll four lines of evidence rely, to some extent, on climate models, and interpreting the evidence often benefits
19 from model diversity and spread in modelled climate sensitivity. Furthermore, high-sensitivity models can provide\nimportant insights into futures that have a low likelihood of occurring but that could result in large impacts.\nBut, unlike in previous assessments, climate models are not considered a line of evidence in their own right in the
20 IPCC Sixth Assessment Report.\nThe ECS of the latest climate models is, on average, higher than that of the previous generation of models
21 and also higher than this Report’s best estimate of 3.0°C. Furthermore, the ECS values in some of the new\nmodels are both above and below the 2°C to 5°C very likely range, and although such models cannot be ruled\nout as implausible solely based on their ECS, some simulations display climate change that is inconsistent with\nthe observed changes when tested with ancient climates. A slight mismatch between models and this Report’s\nassessment is only natural because this Report’s assessment is largely based on observations and an improved\nunderstanding of the climate system.
