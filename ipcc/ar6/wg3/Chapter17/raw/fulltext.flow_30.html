The ‘Just Transition’ concept has evolved over the years (Sweeney and Treat 2018) and is still
13
undergoing further evolution. It emphasizes the key principles of respect and dignity for vulnerable
14
groups, the creation of decent jobs, social protection, employment rights, fairness in energy access and
15
use, and social dialogue and democratic consultation with relevant stakeholders, whilst coping with the
16
effects of asset-stranding or the transition to green and clean economies. The concept has come under
17
increased scrutiny, with its protagonists emphasizing the need to focus on the equality of the transition,
18
not simply on its speed (Forsyth 2014). The emphasis on justice is also gaining in momentum, with a
19
growing recognition that the sustainability transition is about justice in the transition and not simply
20
about economics (Newell and Mulvaney 2013; Swilling, M. Annecke 2010; Williams and Doyon 2020).
21
Scholars are increasingly of the view that a transition involving low-carbon development should not
22
replace old forms of injustice with new ones (Setyowati 2021).

The economic implications of the transition will be felt by developing countries with high degrees of
25
dependence on hydrocarbon products as a revenue stream, as they are exposed to reduced fiscal
26
incomes, given the low demand for oil and low oil prices and the associated economic fallout of the
27
pandemic. This link with stranded assets is important, but it may be overlooked, as countries whose
28
assets are becoming stranded may not have the relevant resources, knowledge, autonomy or agency to
29
design a fresh orientation or decide on the transition. In addition, some developing countries are
30
dependent not only on fossil-fuel revenues, but also on foreign exchange earnings from exports. This
31
dependence comes into sharp focus when one considers that 30% of the Malaysian government’s
32
revenues are linked to petroleum products, and that Mozambique, by exploiting its newly discovered
33
natural-gas reserves, can earn seven times the country’s current GDP over a period of 25 years (Cronin
34
et al. 2021). Thus, any attempt to accelerate the transition to low-carbon development must take into
35
account foreign exchange, domestic revenue and employment generation, which are precisely what
36
ensure the attractiveness of fossil-fuel industries (Addison and Roe 2018).

Energy use and its deployment are sovereign matters. State responsibilities over the control and use of
38
natural resources concern both current and future generations (Carney 2016). Climate-change impacts
39
will disable the food, water and energy systems of the most vulnerable. Therefore, the resources
40
required to enable a just transition are predicated on good leadership and governance institutions that
41
will support quality and justice-based transitions. Beyond energy systems, changes to land systems can
42
benefit from sustainable land management in ways that will reduce the pressure on land for food and at
43
the same time support carbon storage. With land coming under increased pressure, land and forest
44
management are critical for carbon sequestration, as well as other ecosystem benefits. Extractive
45
processes have impacts on land, and often there are few if any redistributive benefits for communities
46
in regions where extraction takes place. In addition, extraction of strategic minerals such as cobalt,
47
copper and lithium have been linked to violence, human rights abuses and conflict (Cronin et al. 2021).
