4 iii. Costs of action and inaction
5 Evidence suggests that the cost of inaction in mitigation and adaptation, and land use, exceeds the cost of interventions in
6 both individual countries, regions, and worldwide (Nkonya et al. 2016). Continued inaction reduces the future policy option space,\ndampens economic growth and increases the challenges of mitigation as well as adaptation (Moore and Diaz 2015; Luderer et al.\n2013). The cost of reducing emissions is estimated to be considerably less than the costs of the damages at all levels (Kainuma\net al. 2013; Moran 2011; Sánchez and Maseda 2016).
7 The costs of adapting to climate impacts are also projected to be substantial, although evidence is limited (summarised in Chambwera\net al. 2014a). Estimates range from 9 to 166 billion USD per year at various scales and types of adaptation, from capacity building\nto specific projects (Fankhauser 2017). There is insufficient literature about the costs of adaptation in the agriculture or land-based\nsectors (Wreford and Renwick 2012) due to lack of baselines, uncertainty around biological relationships and inherent uncertainty about\nanticipated avoided damage estimates, but economic appraisal of actions to maintain the functions of the natural environment and\nland sector generate positive net present values (Adaptation Sub-committee 2013).
8 Preventing land degradation from occurring is considered more cost-effective in the long term compared to the magnitude of resources\nrequired to restore already degraded land (Cowie et al. 2018a) (Section 3.6.1). Evidence from drylands shows that each US dollar\ninvested in land restoration provides between 3 and 6 USD in social returns over a 30-year period, using a discount rate between 2.5\nand 10% (Nkonya et al. 2016). SLM practices reverse or minimise economic losses of land degradation, estimated at between 6.3 and\n10.6 trillion USD annually, (ELD Initiative 2015) more than five times the entire value of agriculture in the market economy (Costanza\net al. 2014; Fischer et al. 2017; Sandifer et al. 2015; Dasgupta et al. 2013) (Section 3.7.5).
9 Across other areas such as food security, disaster mitigation and risk reduction, humanitarian response, and healthy diet\n(to address malnutrition as well as disease), early action generates economic benefits greater than costs (high evidence, high\nagreement) (Fankhauser 2017; Wilkinson et al. 2018; Venton 2018; Venton et al. 2012; Clarvis et al. 2015; Nugent et al. 2018; Watts et\nal. 2018; Bertram et al. 2018) (Sections 6.3 and 6.4).

11 iv. Decision-making under uncertainty
12 Given that significant uncertainty exists regarding the future impacts of climate change, effective decisions must be made under
13 unavoidable uncertainty (Jones et al., 2014).
14 Approaches that allow for decision-making under uncertainty are continually evolving (Section 7.5). An emerging trend is towards\nnew frameworks that will enable multiple decision-makers with multiple objectives to explore the trade-offs between potentially\nconflicting preferences to identify strategies that are robust to deep uncertainties (Singh et al. 2015; Driscoll et al. 2016; Araujo Enciso\net al. 2016; Herman et al. 2014; Pérez et al. 2016; Girard et al. 2015; Haasnoot et al. 2018; Roelich and Giesekam 2019).

16 Valuation of benefits and damages and costing interventions: Measurement issues
17 Cost appraisal tools for climate adaptation are many and their suitability depends on the context (Section 7.5.2.2). Cost-benefit
18 analysis (CBA) and cost-effectiveness analysis (CEA) are commonly applied, especially for current climate variability situations.\nHowever, these tools are not without criticism and their limitations have been observed in the literature (see Rogelj et al. 2018). In\ngeneral, measuring costs and providing valuations are influenced by four conditions: measurement and valuation; the time dimension;\nexternalities; and aggregate versus marginal costs.

20 Measurement and value issues
21 ES not traded in the market fall outside the formal or market-based valuation and so their value is either not accounted for
22 or underestimated in both private and public decisions (Atkinson et al. 2018). Environmental valuation literature uses a range of\ntechniques to assign monetary values to environmental outcomes where no market exists (Atkinson et al. 2018; Dallimer et al. 2018),\nbut some values remain inestimable. For some indigenous cultures and peoples, land is not considered something that can be sold and\nbought, so economic valuations are not meaningful even as proxy approaches (Boillat and Berkes 2013; Kumpula et al. 2011; Pert et\nal. 2015; Xu et al. 2005).
