4 Time dimension (short versus long term) and the issue of discount rates
5 Economics uses a mechanism to convert future values to present day values known as discounting, or the pure rate of time preference.
6 Discount rates are increasingly being chosen to reflect concerns about intergenerational equity, and some countries (e.g., the UK and\nFrance) apply a declining discount rate for long-term public projects. The choice of discount rate has important implications for policy\nevaluation (Anthoff, Tol, and Yohe, 2010; Arrow et al., 2014; Baral, Keenan, Sharma, Stork, and Kasel, 2014; Dasgupta et al., 2013;\nLontzek, Cai, Judd, and Lenton, 2015; Sorokin et al., 2015; van den Bergh and Botzen, 2014) (high evidence, high agreement). Stern\n(2007), for example, used a much lower discount rate (giving almost equal weight to future generations) than the mainstream authors\n(e.g., Nordhaus (1941) and obtained much higher estimates of the damage of climate change).

8 Positive and negative externalities (consequences and impacts not accounted for in market economy),
9 All land use generates externalities (unaccounted for side effects of an activity). Examples include loss of ES (e.g., reduced pollinators;
10 soil erosion, increased water pollution, nitrification, etc.). Positive externalities include sequestration of carbon dioxide (CO ) and\n2\nimproved soil water filtration from afforestation. Externalities can also be social (e.g., displacement and migration) and economic\n(e.g., loss of productive land). In the context of climate change and land, the major externality is the agriculture, forestry and other\nland-use (AFOLU) sourced greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. Examples of mechanisms to internalise externalities are discussed in 7.5.

12 Aggregate versus marginal costs
13 Costs of climate change are often referred to through the marginal measure of the social cost of carbon (SCC), which evaluate the
14 total net damages of an extra metric tonne of CO emissions due to the associated climate change (Nordhaus 2014). The SCC can be\n2\nused to determine a carbon price, but SCC depends on discount rate assumptions and may neglect processes, including large losses of\nbiodiversity, political instability, violent conflicts, large-scale migration flows, and the effects of climate change on the development\nof economies (Stern 2013; Pezzey 2019).
15 At the sectoral level, marginal abatement cost (MAC) curves are widely used for the assessment of costs related to CO or GHG\n2\nemissions reduction. MAC measures the cost of reducing one more GHG unit and MAC curves are either expert-based or model-\nderived and offer a range of approaches and assumptions on discount rates or available abatement technologies (Moran 2011).
