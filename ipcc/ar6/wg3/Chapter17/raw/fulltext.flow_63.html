17.5
Conclusions
22
This chapter has been concerned to assess the opportunities and challenges for acceleration in the
23
context of sustainable development. As such, many of the claims reviewed involve not only increasing
24
the speed of the transition but also ensuring that it is just, equitable and delivers a wider range of
25
environmental and social benefits. A sustainability transition requires removing the underlying drivers
26
of vulnerability and high emissions (quality and depth) while aligning the interests of different
27
communities, regions, sectors, stakeholders and cultures (scale and breadth).
28
29
Interest in a sustainability transition has grown steadily over the history of the IPCC and of climate and
30
related policy processes. That interest hit a high point in 2015 with the Paris Agreement and the 2030
31
Agenda for Sustainable Development and its 17 SDGs. It has continued to remain high as countries
32
have issued NDCs on climate change, VNRs on the SDGs and, in some instances, integrated climate
33
and SDG plans (or similarly themed integrated actions, e.g. circular economy plans). Interest has also
34
gained momentum as local governments, businesses and other stakeholders have followed suit with
35
climate change- or SDG-related plans.
36
37
Implementing many of the recent pledges, however, has proved challenging. Part of the challenge is a
38
need to address everything from public policies and prevailing technologies to individual lifestyles and
39
social norms to governance arrangements and institutions with associated political economy
40
implications. These factors can lock in development pathways and prevent transitions from gathering
41
the momentum needed for large-scale transformations of socioeconomic systems. Another
42
consideration is that transition pathways are likely to vary across and within countries due to different
43
development levels, starting points, differential vulnerabilities, capacities, agencies, geographies, power
44
dynamics, political economies, ecosystems and other contextual factors.
45
46

47
have reflected on interventions that can enable transitions. Because these disciplines often focus on
48
different levels of analysis and draw upon diverse analytical methods and empirical evidence, the
49
recommended interventions also tend to vary. For instance, economic arguments often point to the need
50
for targeted regulation or investments, institutional claims centre on multilevel governance reforms, and
51
psychology encourages participation to change mind sets and social norms. Systems-level perspectives
52
