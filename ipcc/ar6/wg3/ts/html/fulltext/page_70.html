TS. 5.3 Transport 1
Meeting climate mitigation goals would require transformative changes in the transport sector. 2 In 2019, direct GHG emissions from the transport sector were 8.7 GtCO2-eq (up from 5.0 GtCO2-eq in 3 1990) and accounted for 23% of global energy-related CO2 emissions. 70% of direct transport emissions 4 came from road vehicles, while 1%, 11%, and 12% came from rail, shipping, and aviation, respectively. 5 Emissions from shipping and aviation continue to grow rapidly. Transport-related emissions in 6 developing regions of the world have increased more rapidly than in Europe or North America, a trend 7 that is expected to continue in coming decades. (high confidence) {10.1, 10.5, 10.6} 8
Since AR5 there has been a growing awareness of the need for demand management solutions 9 combined with new technologies, such as the rapidly growing use of electromobility for land 10 transport and the emerging options in advanced biofuels and hydrogen-based fuels for shipping 11 and aviation and in other specific land-based contexts (high confidence). There is a growing need 12 for systemic infrastructure changes that enable behavioural modifications and reductions in demand for 13 transport services that can in turn reduce energy demand. The response to the COVID-19 pandemic has 14 also shown that behavioural interventions can reduce transport-related GHG emissions. For example, 15 COVID-19-based lockdowns have confirmed the transformative value of telecommuting replacing 16 significant numbers of work and personal journeys as well as promoting local active transport. There 17 are growing opportunities to implement strategies that drive behavioural change and support the 18 adoption of new transport technology options. {Chapter 5, 10.2, 10.3, 10.4, 10.8} 19
Changes in urban form, behaviour programs, the circular economy, the shared economy, and 20 digitalisation trends can support systemic changes that lead to reductions in demand for transport 21 services or expands the use of more efficient transport modes (high confidence). Cities can reduce 22 their transport-related fuel consumption by around 25% through combinations of more compact land 23 use and the provision of less car-dependent transport infrastructure. Appropriate infrastructure, 24 including protected pedestrian and bike pathways, can also support much greater localised active 25 travel.17Transport demand management incentives are expected to be necessary to support these 26 systemic changes. There is mixed evidence of the effect of circular economy initiatives, shared economy 27 initiatives, and digitalisation on demand for transport services (Box TS.14). For example, while 28 dematerialisation can reduce the amount of material that needs to be transported to manufacturing 29 facilities, an increase in online shopping with priority delivery can increase demand for freight transport. 30 Similarly, while teleworking could reduce travel demand, increased ridesharing could increase vehicle-31 km travelled. {Chapter 1, Chapter 5, 10.2, 10.8} 32
Battery-electric vehicles (BEVs) have lower life cycle greenhouse gas emissions than internal 33 combustion engine vehicles (ICEVs) when BEVs are charged with low carbon electricity (high 34 confidence). Electromobility is being rapidly implemented in micro-mobility (e-autorickshaws, e-35 scooters, e-bikes), in transit systems, especially buses, and to a lesser degree, in personal vehicles. BEVs 36 could also have the added benefit of supporting grid operations. The commercial availability of mature 37 lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) has underpinned this growth in electromobility. As global battery 38 production increases, unit costs are declining. Further efforts to reduce the GHG footprint of battery 39 production, however, are essential for maximising the mitigation potential of BEVs. The continued 40 growth of electromobility for land transport would entail investments in electric charging and related 41 grid infrastructure. Electromobility powered by low-carbon electricity has the potential to rapidly 42 reduce transport GHG and can be applied with multiple co-benefits, especially in the developing 43 countries. {10.3, 10.4, 10.8} 44
FOOTNOTE 17‘Active travel’ is travel that requires physical effort, for example journeys made by walking or cycling.
