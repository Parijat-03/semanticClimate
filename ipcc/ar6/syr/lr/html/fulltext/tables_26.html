2 Limiting human-caused global warming to a specific level requires limiting cumulative CO emissions,\n2
3 reaching net zero or net negative CO emissions, along with strong reductions in other GHG emissions\n2
4 (see 3.3.2). Future additional warming will depend on future emissions, with total warming dominated by past
5 and future cumulative CO₂ emissions {WGI SPM D.1.1, WGI Figure SPM.4; SR1.5 SPM A.2.2}.
6
7 Reaching net zero CO emissions is different from reaching net zero GHG emissions. The timing of net\n2
8 zero for a basket of GHGs depends on the emissions metric, such as global warming potential over a 100-year
9 period, chosen to convert non-CO emissions into CO -equivalent (high confidence). However, for a given\n2 2
10 emissions pathway, the physical climate response is independent of the metric chosen (high confidence) {WGI
11 SPM D.1.8; WGIII Box TS.6, WGIII Cross-chapter box 2}.
12
13 Achieving global net zero GHG emissions requires all remaining CO and metric-weighted41 non-CO\n2 2
14 GHG emissions to be counterbalanced by durably stored CO removals (high confidence). Some non-\n2
15 CO emissions, such as CH and N O from agriculture, cannot be fully eliminated using existing and\n2 4 2
16 anticipated technical measures {WGIII SPM C.2.4, WGIII SPM C.11.4, Cross-Chapter Box 3}.
17
18 Global net zero CO or GHG emissions can be achieved even if some sectors and regions are net emitters,\n2
19 provided that others reach net negative emissions (see Figure 4.1). The potential and cost of achieving net
20 zero or even net negative emissions vary by sector and region. If and when net zero emissions for a given
21 sector or region are reached depends on multiple factors, including the potential to reduce GHG emissions and
22 undertake carbon dioxide removal, the associated costs, and the availability of policy mechanisms to balance
23 emissions and removals between sectors and countries. (high confidence) {WGIII Box TS.6, WGIII Cross-
24 Chapter Box 3}.
25
26 The adoption and implementation of net-zero emission targets by countries and regions also depend on
27 equity and capacity considerations (high confidence). The formulation of net zero pathways by countries
28 will benefit from clarity on scope, plans-of-action, and fairness. Achieving net zero emission targets relies on
29 policies, institutions, and milestones against which to track progress. Least-cost global modelled pathways
30 have been shown to distribute the mitigation effort unevenly, and the incorporation of equity principles could
31 change the country-level timing of net zero (high confidence). The Paris Agreement also recognizes that
32 peaking of emissions will occur later in developing countries than developed countries (Article 4.1) {WGIII
33 Box TS.6, WGIII Cross-Chapter Box 3, WGIII 14.3}.
