Carbon Capture and Utilisation (CCU) applied to fossil CO2 do not count as removal technologies. CCS 1 and CCU can only be part of CDR methods if the CO2 is biogenic or directly captured from ambient 2 air, and stored durably in geological reservoirs or products. {12.3} 3
There is a great variety of CDR methods and respective implementation options {Cross-Chapter Box 8 4 Figure 1 in Chapter 12}. Some of these methods (like afforestation and soil-carbon sequestration) have 5 been practiced for decades to millennia, although not necessarily with the intention to remove carbon 6 from the atmosphere. Conversely, for methods such as DACCS and BECCS, experience is growing but 7 still limited in scales. A categorisation of CDR methods can be based on several criteria, depending on 8 the highlighted characteristics. In this report, the categorisation is focused on the role of CDR methods 9 in the carbon cycle, i.e. on the removal process (land-based biological; ocean-based biological; 10 geochemical; chemical) and on the timescale of storage (decades to centuries; centuries to millennia; 11 10 thousand years or longer), the latter being closely linked to different carbon storage media. Within 12 one category (e.g., ocean-based biological CDR) options often differ with respect to other dynamic or 13 context-specific dimensions such as mitigation potential, cost, potential for co-benefits and adverse 14 side-effects, and technology readiness level. It is useful to distinguish between CO2 removal from the atmosphere as the outcome of deliberate 16 activities implementing CDR options, and the net emissions outcome achieved with the help of CDR 17 deployment (i.e., gross emissions minus gross removals). As part of ambitious mitigation strategies at 18 global or national levels, gross CDR can fulfil three different roles in complementing emissions 19 abatement: (1) lowering net CO2 or GHG emissions in the near-term; (2) counterbalancing ‘hard-to-20 abate’ residual emissions like CO2 from industrial activities and long-distance transport, or CH4 and 21 nitrous oxide from agriculture, in order to help reach net zero CO2 or GHG emissions in the mid-term; 22 (3) achieving net negative CO2 or GHG emissions in the long-term if deployed at levels exceeding 23 annual residual emissions {2.7, 3.3, 3.4, 3.5}. These roles of CDR are not mutually exclusive: for 24 example, achieving net zero CO2 or GHG emissions globally might involve individual developed 25 countries attaining net negative CO2 emissions at the time of global net zero, thereby allowing 26 developing countries a smoother transition. {Cross-Chapter Box 8 Figure 2 in Chapter 12} 27
