6 Country Reporting of GHG Inventories (GHGIs): All parties to the UNFCCC are required to report national GHGIs of anthropogenic\nemissions and removals. Reporting requirements are differentiated between developed and developing countries. Because of the\ndifficulty of separating direct anthropogenic fluxes from indirect or natural fluxes, the IPCC (2003) adopted the ‘managed land’\nconcept as a proxy to facilitate GHGI reporting. All GHG fluxes on ‘managed land’ are defined as anthropogenic, with each country\napplying their own definition of ‘managed land’ (i.e., ‘where human interventions and practices have been applied to perform\nproduction, ecological or social functions’ (IPCC 2006)). Fluxes may be determined on the basis of changes in carbon stocks (e.g.,\nfrom forest inventories) or by activity data (e.g., area of land cover change management activity multiplied by emission factors or with\nmodelled fluxes). Depending on the specific methods used, GHGIs include all direct anthropogenic effects and may include the indirect\nanthropogenic effects of environmental change (generally sinks) and natural effects (Section 2.3.1.2). GHG fluxes from ‘unmanaged\nland’ are not reported in GHGIs because they are assumed to be non-anthropogenic. The reported estimates may then be filtered\nthrough agreed ‘accounting rules’ (i.e., what countries actually count towards their mitigation targets (Cowie et al. 2007; Lee and Sanz\n2017). The accounting aims to better quantify the additional mitigation actions by, for example, factoring out the impact of natural\ndisturbances and forest age-related dynamics (Canadell et al. 2007; Grassi et al. 2018).
