Many of the potential trade-offs between mitigation and other sustainable development outcomes 9 depend on policy design and can be compensated or avoided with additional policies and 10 investments, or through policies that integrate mitigation with other SDGs (high confidence). 11 Targeted SDG policies and investments, for example in the areas of healthy nutrition, sustainable 12 consumption and production, and international collaboration, can support climate change mitigation 13 policies and resolve or alleviate trade-offs. Trade-offs can also be addressed by complementary policies 14 and investments, as well as through the design of cross-sectoral policies integrating mitigation with the 15 SDGs, and in particular: good health and well-being (SDG 3), zero hunger and nutrition (SDG 2), 16 responsible consumption and production (SDG 12), reduced inequalities (SDG 10) and life on land 17 (SDG 15). Decent living standards, which encompasses many SDG dimensions, are achievable at lower 19 energy use than previously thought (high confidence). Mitigation strategies that focus on lowering 20 demand for energy and land-based resources exhibit reduced trade-offs and negative consequences for 21 sustainable development relative to pathways involving either high emissions and climate impacts or 22 pathways with high consumption and emissions that are ultimately compensated by large quantities of 23 BECCS. The timing of mitigation actions and their effectiveness will have significant consequences for 27 broader sustainable development outcomes in the longer term (high confidence). Ambitious 28 mitigation can be considered a precondition for achieving the SDGs. {3.7} 29
Adopting coordinated cross-sectoral approaches to climate mitigation can target synergies and 30 minimise trade-offs, both between sectors and between sustainable development objectives (high 31 confidence). This requires integrated planning using multiple-objective-multiple-impact policy 32 frameworks. Strong inter-dependencies and cross-sectoral linkages create both opportunities for 33 synergies and need to address trade-offs related to mitigation options and technologies. This can only 34 be done if coordinated sectoral approaches to climate change mitigation policies are adopted that 35 mainstream these interactions and ensure local people are involved in the development of new products, 36 as well as production and consumption practices. For instance, there can be many synergies in urban 37 areas between mitigation policies and the SDGs but capturing these depends on the overall planning of 38 urban structures and on local integrated policies such as combining affordable housing and spatial 39 planning with walkable urban areas, green electrification and clean renewable energy (medium 40 confidence). Integrated planning and cross-sectoral alignment of climate change policies are also 41 particularly evident in developing countries’ NDCs under the Paris Agreement, where key priority 42 sectors such as agriculture and energy are closely aligned with the proposed mitigation and adaptation 43 actions and the SDGs. {12.6.2, Supplementary Material Table 17.1, 17.3.3} 44
