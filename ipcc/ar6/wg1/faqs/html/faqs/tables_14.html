3 FAQ 2.2 | What Is the Evidence for Climate Change?
4
5 The evidence for climate change rests on more than just increasing surface temperatures. A broad range of\nindicators collectively leads to the inescapable conclusion that we are witnessing rapid changes to many aspects\nof our global climate. We are seeing changes in the atmosphere, ocean, cryosphere, and biosphere. Our scientific
6 understanding depicts a coherent picture of a warming world.\nWe have long observed our changing climate. From the earliest scientists taking meteorological observations
7 in the 16th and 17th centuries to the present, we have seen a revolution in our ability to observe and diagnose\nour changing climate. Today we can observe diverse aspects of our climate system from space, from aircraft\nand weather balloons, using a range of ground-based observing technologies, and using instruments that can
8 measure to great depths in the ocean.\nObserved changes in key indicators point to warming over land areas. Global surface temperature over land has
9 increased since the late 19th century, and changes are apparent in a variety of societally relevant temperature\nextremes. Since the mid-1950s the troposphere (i.e., the lowest few km of the atmosphere) has warmed, and\nprecipitation over land has increased. Near-surface specific humidity (i.e., water vapour) over land has increased\nsince at least the 1970s. Aspects of atmospheric circulation have also evolved since the mid-20th century, including
10 a poleward shift of mid-latitude storm tracks.\nChanges in the global ocean point to warming as well. Global average sea surface temperature has increased
11 since the late 19th century. The heat content of the global ocean has increased since the 19th century, with more\nthan 90% of the excess energy accumulated in the climate system being stored in the ocean. This ocean warming\nhas caused ocean waters to expand, which has contributed to the increase in global sea level in the past century.\nThe relative acidity of the ocean has also increased since the early 20th century, caused by the uptake of carbon
12 dioxide from the atmosphere, and oxygen loss is evident in the upper ocean since the 1970s.\nSignificant changes are also evident over the cryosphere – the portion of the Earth where water is seasonally or
13 continuously frozen as snow or ice. There have been decreases in Arctic sea ice area and thickness and changes\nin Antarctic sea ice extent since the mid-1970s. Spring snow cover in the Northern Hemisphere has decreased\nsince the late-1970s, along with an observed warming and thawing of permafrost (perennially frozen ground).\nThe Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets are shrinking, as are the vast majority of glaciers worldwide, contributing
14 strongly to the observed sea level rise.\nMany aspects of the biosphere are also changing. Over the last century, long-term ecological surveys show that
15 many land species have generally moved poleward and to higher elevations. There have been increases in green\nleaf area and/or mass (i.e., global greenness) since the early 1980s, and the length of the growing season has\nincreased over much of the extratropical Northern Hemisphere since at least the mid-20th century. There is also\nstrong evidence that various phenological metrics (such as the timing of fish migrations) for many marine species
16 have changed in the last half century.\nChange is apparent across many components of the climate system. It has been observed using a very broad range
17 of techniques and analysed independently by numerous groups around the world. The changes are consistent in\npointing to a climate system that has undergone rapid warming since the industrial revolution.
