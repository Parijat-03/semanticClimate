The impact of climate change in limiting wellbeing is most acutely felt by the world’s poorest people, 19
communities, and nations, who have the smallest carbon footprint, constrained capacity to respond and 20
limited voice in important decision-making circles (Okereke and Ehresman 2015; Tosam and Mbih 21
2015; Mugambiwa and Tirivangasi 2017). The wide variation in the contribution to, and impact of 22
climate change within and across countries makes equity, inequality, justice, and poverty eradication, 23
inescapable aspects of the relationship between sustainable development and climate change (Reckien 24

eradication (Burton et al. 2001; Smit et al. 2001; Klinsky and Winkler 2014; Tschakert and Olsson 28
2005; IPCC 2014a, 2018b). 29
1.6.2
Concepts and frameworks for integrating climate mitigation and development:
30
At one level, sustainable development can be seen as a meta framework for integrating climate action 31
with other global sustainability goals (Antal and Van Den Bergh 2016; Casadio Tarabusi and Guarini 32
2013). Fundamentally, the concept of sustainable development underscores the interlinkages and 33
interdependence of human and natural systems and the need to balance economic, social, and 34
environmental (including climate pollution) aspects in development planning and processes (Nunan 35
2017; Gomez-Echeverri 2018b; Zhenmin and Espinosa 2019). 36
Despite the appeal of the concept, tensions remain over the interpretation and practical application, with 37
acute disagreements regarding what the balancing entails in real life, how to measure wellbeing, which 38
goals to set, and the means through which such goals might be pursued (Michelsen et al. 2016; Shang 39
et al. 2019; Okereke and Massaquoi 2017; UNEP 2018b; Arrow et al. 2011; Dasgupta et al. 2015; 40
Sugiawan et al. 2019; Haberl et al. 2019). 41
Moreover, countries differ enormously in their respective situation regarding their development path – 42
