TS. 4.2 Long-term mitigation pathways 1
The characteristics of a wide range of long-term mitigation pathways, their common elements 2 and differences are assessed in Chapter 3. Differences between pathways typically represent 3 choices that can steer the system in alternative directions through the selection of different 4 combinations of response options (high confidence). More than 2000 quantitative emissions 5 pathways were submitted to the AR6 scenarios database, of which more than 1200 pathways included 6 sufficient information for the associated warming to be assessed (consistent with AR6 WG I methods). 7 (Box TS.5) {3.2, 3.3} 8
Many pathways in the literature show how to likely limit global warming to 2°C with no overshoot 9 or to limit warming to 1.5°C with limited overshoot compared to 1850-1900. The likelihood of 10 limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot has dropped in AR6 WG III compared 11 to AR6 SR1.5 because global GHG emissions have risen since 2017, leading to higher near-term 12 emissions (2030) and higher cumulative CO2 emissions until the time of net zero (medium 13 confidence). Only a small number of published pathways limit global warming to 1.5°C without 14 overshoot over the course of the 21stcentury. {3.3, Annex III.II.3} 15
Mitigation pathways limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot reach 50% CO2 16 reductions in the 2030s, relative to 2019, then reduce emissions further to reach net zero CO2 17 emissions in the 2050s. Pathways likely limiting warming to 2°C reach 50% reductions in the 18 2040s and net zero CO2 by 2070s (medium confidence). (Figure TS.10, Box TS.6) {3.3} 19
Cost-effective mitigation pathways assuming immediate action to likely limit warming to 2°C are 20 associated with net global GHG emissions of 30-49 GtCO2-eq yr-1by 2030 and 13-26 GtCO2-eq 21 yr-1by 2050 (medium confidence). This corresponds to reductions, relative to 2019 levels, of 13-45% 22 by 2030 and 52-76% by 2050. Pathways that limit global warming to below 1.5°C with no or limited 23 overshoot require a further acceleration in the pace of transformation, with net GHG emissions typically 24 around 21-36 GtCO2-eq yr-1by 2030 and 1-15 GtCO2-eq yr-1by 2050; this corresponds to reductions of 25 34–60% by 2030 and 73-98% by 2050 relative to 2019 levels. {3.3} 26

Box TS.5: Illustrative Mitigation Pathways (IMPs), and Shared Socio-economic Pathways 29 (SSPs) 30
The Illustrative Mitigation Pathways (IMPs) 31
The over 2500 model-based pathways submitted to the AR6 scenarios database pathways explore 32 different possible evolutions of future energy and land use (with and without climate policy) and the 33 consequences for greenhouse gas emissions. 34
From the full range of pathways, five archetype scenarios – referred to in this report as Illustrative 35 Mitigation Pathways (IMPs) – were selected to illustrate key mitigation-strategy themes that flow 36 through several chapters in this report. A further two pathways illustrative of high emissions assuming 37 continuation of current policies or moderately increased action were selected to show the consequences 38 of current policies and pledges. Together these pathways provide illustrations of potential future 39 developments that can be shaped by human choices, including: Where are current policies and pledges 40 leading us? What is needed to reach specific temperature goals? What are the consequences of using 41 different strategies to meet these goals? What are the consequences of delay? How can we shift 42 development from current practices to give higher priority to sustainability and the SDGs? 43
