2 FAQ 7.2 | What Is the Role of Clouds in a Warming Climate?
3
4 One of the biggest challenges in climate science has been to predict how clouds will change in a warming world\nand whether those changes will amplify or partially offset the warming caused by increasing concentrations of\ngreenhouse gases and other human activities. Scientists have made significant progress over the past decade and
5 are now more confident that changes in clouds will amplify, rather than offset, global warming in the future.\nClouds cover roughly two-thirds of the Earth’s surface. They consist of small droplets and/or ice crystals, which
6 form when water vapour condenses or deposits around tiny particles called aerosols (such as salt, dust, or smoke).\nClouds play a critical role in the Earth’s energy budget at the top of our atmosphere and therefore influence\nEarth’s surface temperature (see FAQ 7.1). The interactions between clouds and the climate are complex and\nvaried. Clouds at low altitudes tend to reflect incoming solar energy back to space, creating a cooling effect by\npreventing this energy from reaching and warming the Earth. On the other hand, higher clouds tend to trap\n(i.e., absorb and then emit at a lower temperature) some of the energy leaving the Earth, leading to a warming\neffect. On average, clouds reflect back more incoming energy than the amount of outgoing energy they trap,\nresulting in an overall net cooling effect on the present climate. Human activities since the pre-industrial era\nhave altered this climate effect of clouds in two different ways: by changing the abundance of the aerosol\nparticles in the atmosphere and by warming the Earth’s surface, primarily as a result of increases in greenhouse
7 gas emissions.\nThe concentration of aerosols in the atmosphere has markedly increased since the pre-industrial era, and this
8 has had two important effects on clouds. First, clouds now reflect more incoming energy because cloud droplets\nhave become more numerous and smaller. Second, smaller droplets may delay rain formation, thereby making\nthe clouds last longer, although this effect remains uncertain. Hence, aerosols released by human activities have\nhad a cooling effect, counteracting a considerable portion of the warming caused by increases in greenhouse\ngases over the last century (see FAQ 3.1). Nevertheless, this cooling effect is expected to diminish in the future, as
9 air pollution policies progress worldwide, reducing the amount of aerosols released into the atmosphere.\nSince the pre-industrial period, the Earth’s surface and atmosphere have warmed, altering the properties of
10 clouds, such as their altitude, amount and composition (water or ice), thereby affecting the Earth’s energy budget\nand, in turn, changing temperature. This cascading effect of clouds, known as the cloud feedback, could either\namplify or offset some of the future warming and has long been the biggest source of uncertainty in climate\nprojections. The problem stems from the fact that clouds can change in many ways and that their processes occur\non much smaller scales than global climate models can explicitly represent. As a result, global climate models\nhave disagreed on how clouds, particularly over the subtropical ocean, will change in the future and whether the
11 change will amplify or suppress the global warming.\nSince the last IPCC Report in 2013 (the Fifth Assessment Report, or AR5), understanding of cloud processes has
12 advanced with better observations, new analysis approaches and explicit high-resolution numerical simulation of\nclouds. Also, current global climate models simulate cloud behaviour better than previous models, due both to\nadvances in computational capabilities and process understanding. Altogether, this has helped to build a more\ncomplete picture of how clouds will change as the climate warms (FAQ 7.2, Figure 1). For example, the amount\nof low-clouds will reduce over the subtropical ocean, leading to less reflection of incoming solar energy, and\nthe altitude of high-clouds will rise, making them more prone to trapping outgoing energy; both processes\nhave a warming effect. In contrast, clouds in high latitudes will be increasingly made of water droplets rather\nthan ice crystals. This shift from fewer, larger ice crystals to smaller but more numerous water droplets will\nresult in more of the incoming solar energy being reflected back to space and produce a cooling effect. Better\nunderstanding of how clouds respond to warming has led to more confidence than before that future changes\nin clouds will, overall, cause additional warming (i.e., by weakening the current cooling effect of clouds). This is
13 called a positive net cloud feedback.\nIn summary, clouds will amplify rather than suppress the warming of the climate system in the future, as more
14 greenhouse gases and fewer aerosols are released to the atmosphere by human activities.
