0 Box TS.7 | Climate and Air Quality Responses to Short-lived Climate Forcers in Shared\nSocio-economic Pathways
1
2 Future changes in emissions of short-lived climate forcers (SLCFs) are expected to cause an additional global mean\nwarming, with a large diversity in the end-of-century response across the WGI core set of Shared Socio-economic\nPathways (SSPs), depending upon the level of climate change and air pollution mitigation (Box TS.7, Figure 1). This\nadditional warming is either due to reductions in cooling aerosols for air pollution regulation or due to increases in\nmethane (CH), ozone and hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs). This additional warming is stable after 2040 in SSPs associated\n4\nwith lower global air pollution as long as CH emissions are also mitigated, but the overall warming induced by SLCF\n4\nchanges is higher in scenarios in which air quality continues to deteriorate (induced by growing fossil fuel use and\nlimited air pollution control) (high confidence).\nSustained CH mitigation reduces global surface ozone, contributing to air quality improvements, and also reduces\n4\nsurface temperature in the longer term, but only sustained CO emissions reductions allow long-term climate\n2\nstabilization (high confidence). Future changes in air quality (near-surface ozone and particulate matter, or PM) at\nglobal and local scales are predominantly driven by changes in ozone and aerosol precursor emissions rather than\nclimate (high confidence). Air quality improvements driven by rapid decarbonization strategies, as in SSP1-1.9 and\nSSP1-2.6, are not sufficient in the near term to achieve air quality guidelines set by the World Health Organization in\nsome highly polluted regions (high confidence). Additional policies (e.g., access to clean energy, waste management)\nenvisaged to attain United Nations Sustainable Development Goals bring complementary SLCF reduction. {4.4.4, 6.6.3,\n6.7.3, Box 6.2}
3
4 The net effect of SLCF emissions changes on temperature will depend on how emissions of warming and cooling SLCFs will evolve\nin the future. The magnitude of the cooling effect of aerosols remains the largest uncertainty in the effect of SLCFs in future climate\nprojections. Since the SLCFs have undergone large changes over the past two decades, the temperature and air pollution responses\nare estimated relative to the year 2019 instead of 1995–2014.
5 Temperature Response
6 In the next two decades, it is very likely that SLCF emissions changes will cause a warming relative to 2019, across the WGI core set of
7 SSPs (see Section TS.1.3.1), in addition to the warming from long-lived GHGs. The net effect of SLCF and HFC changes in global surface\ntemperature across the SSPs is a likely warming of 0.06°C–0.35°C in 2040 relative to 2019. This near-term global mean warming\nlinked to SLCFs is quite similar in magnitude across the SSPs due to competing effects of warming (CH, ozone) and cooling (aerosols)\n4\nforcers (Box TS.7, Figure 1). There is greater diversity in the end-of-century response among the scenarios. SLCF changes in scenarios\nwith no climate change mitigation (SSP3-7.0 and SSP5-8.5) will cause a warming in the likely range of 0.4°C–0.9°C in 2100 relative to\n2019 due to increases in CH, tropospheric ozone and HFC levels. For the stringent climate change and pollution mitigation scenarios\n4\n(SSP1-1.9 and SSP1-2.6), the cooling from reductions in CH, ozone and HFCs partially balances the warming from reduced aerosols,\n4\nprimarily sulphate, and the overall SLCF effect is a likely increase in global surface temperature of 0.0°C–0.3°C in 2100, relative to\n2019. With intermediate climate change and air pollution mitigations, SLCFs in SSP2-4.5 add a likely warming of 0.2°C–0.5°C to global\nsurface temperature change in 2100, with the largest warming resulting from reductions in aerosols. {4.4.4, 6.7.3}

9 Assuming implementation and efficient enforcement of both the Kigali Amendment to the Montreal Protocol on Substances that\nDeplete the Ozone Layer and current national plans result in limiting emissions (as in SSP1-2.6), the effects of HFCs on global\nsurface temperature, relative to 2019, would remain below +0. 02°C from 2050 onwards versus about +0.04°C–0.08°C in 2050 and\n+0.1°C–0.3°C in 2100 considering only national HFC regulations decided prior to the Kigali Amendment (as in SSP5-8.5) (medium\nconfidence). {6.6.3, 6.7.3}
10 Air Quality Responses
11 Air pollution projections range from strong reductions in global surface ozone and PM (e.g., SSP1-2.6, with stringent mitigation of both
12 air pollution and climate change) to no improvement and even degradation (e.g., SSP3-7.0 without climate change mitigation and\nwith only weak air pollution control) (high confidence). Under the SSP3-7.0 scenario, PM levels are projected to increase until 2050\nover large parts of Asia, and surface ozone pollution is projected to worsen over all continental areas through 2100 (high confidence).\nIn SSP5-8.5, a scenario without climate change mitigation but with stringent air pollution control, PM levels decline through 2100, but\nhigh CH levels hamper the decline in global surface ozone at least until 2080 (high confidence). {6.7.1}\n4
