Bioenergy is the most land-intensive energy option, but total land occupation of other renewable 1 energy options can also become significant in high deployment scenarios. While not as closely 2 connected to the AFOLU sector as bioenergy, other renewable energy options can influence 3 AFOLU activities in both synergistic and detrimental ways (high confidence). The character of land 4 occupation, and associated impacts, vary considerably among mitigation options and also for the same 5 option depending on geographic location, scale, system design and deployment strategy. Land 6 occupation can be large uniform areas, e.g., reservoir hydropower dams and tree plantations, and more 7 distributed occupation that is integrated with other land uses, e.g., wind turbines and agroforestry in 8 agriculture landscapes. Deployment can be partly decoupled from additional land use, e.g., use of 9 organic waste and residues and integration of solar PV into buildings and other infrastructure (high 10 confidence). Wind and solar power can coexist with agriculture in beneficial ways (medium confidence). 11 Indirect land occupation includes new agriculture areas following displacement of food production with 12 bioenergy plantations and expansion of mining activities providing minerals required for manufacture 13 of EV batteries, PV, and wind power. {7.4, 12.5} 14
The deployment of land-based mitigation measures can provide co-benefits, but there are also 15 risks and trade-offs from inappropriate land management (high confidence). Such risks can best 16 be managed if AFOLU mitigation is pursued in response to the needs and perspectives of multiple 17 stakeholders to achieve outcomes that maximise synergies while limiting trade-offs (medium 18 confidence). The results of implementing AFOLU measures are often variable and highly context 19 specific. Depending on local conditions (e.g., ecosystem, climate, food system, land ownership) and 20 management strategies (e.g., scale, method), mitigation measures can positively or negatively affect 21 biodiversity, ecosystem functioning, air quality, water availability and quality, soil productivity, rights 22 infringements, food security, and human well-being. The agriculture and forestry sectors can devise 23 management approaches that enable biomass production and use for energy in conjunction with the 24 production of food and timber, thereby reducing the conversion pressure on natural ecosystems (medium 25 confidence). Mitigation measures addressing GHGs may also affect other climate forcers such as albedo 26 and evapotranspiration. Integrated responses that contribute to mitigation, adaptation, and other land 27 challenges will have greater likelihood of being successful (high confidence); measures which provide 28 additional benefits to biodiversity and human well-being are sometimes described as ‘Nature-based 29 Solutions’. {7.1, 7.4, 7.6, 12.4, 12.5} 30
AFOLU mitigation measures have been well understood for decades but deployment remains 31 slow, and emissions trends indicate unsatisfactory progress despite beneficial contributions to 32 global emissions reduction from forest-related options (high confidence). Globally, the AFOLU 33 sector has so far contributed modestly to net mitigation, as past policies have delivered about 0.65 34 GtCO2 yr-1of mitigation during 2010–2019 or 1.4% of global gross emissions. The majority (>80%) of 35 emission reduction resulted from forestry measures. Although the mitigation potential of AFOLU 36 measures is large from a biophysical and ecological perspective, its feasibility is hampered by lack of 37 institutional support, uncertainty over long-term additionality and trade-offs, weak governance, 38 fragmented land ownership, and uncertain permanence effects. Despite these impediments to change, 39 AFOLU mitigation options are demonstrably effective and with appropriate support can enable rapid 40 emission reductions in most countries. {7.4, 7.6} 41
Concerted, rapid and sustained effort by all stakeholders, from policy makers and investors to 42 land-owners and managers is a pre-requisite for achieving high levels of mitigation in the AFOLU 43 sector (high confidence). To date USD0.7 billion yr-1is estimated to have been spent on AFOLU 44 mitigation. This is well short of the more than USD400 billion yr-1that is estimated to be necessary to 45 deliver the up to 30% of global mitigation effort envisaged in deep mitigation scenarios (medium 46 confidence).
