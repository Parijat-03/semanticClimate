1 Improved food\nprocessing and\nretailing Improved food processing and retailing involves\nseveral practices related to a) greening supply chains\n(e.g., utilising products and services with a reduced impact\non the environment and human health), b) adoption\nof specific sustainability instruments among agri-food\ncompanies (e.g., eco-innovation practices), c) adopting\nemission accounting tools (e.g., carbon and water\nfoot-printing), d) implementing ‘demand forecasting’\nstrategies (e.g., changes in consumer preference for\n‘green’ products) and, e) supporting polycentric supply-\nchain governance processes. Improved food processing and retailing can provide benefits\nfor climate mitigation since GHG-friendly foods can reduce\nagri-food GHG emissions from transportation, waste and\nenergy use. In cases where climate extremes and natural\ndisasters disrupt supply chain networks, improved food\nprocessing and retailing can benefit climate adaptation by\nbuffering the impacts of changing temperature and rainfall\npatterns on upstream agricultural production. It can provide\nbenefits for food security by supporting healthier diets and\nreducing food loss and waste. Successful implementation\nis dependent on organisational capacity, the agility and\nflexibility of business strategies, the strengthening of public-\nprivate policies and effectiveness of supply-chain governance. 2 Improved energy\nuse in food systems Agriculture’s energy efficiency can be improved to\nreduce the dependency on non-renewable energy\nsources. This can be realised either by decreased\nenergy inputs, or through increased outputs per unit\nof input. In some countries, managerial inefficiency\n(rather than a technology gap) is the main source for\nenergy-efficiency loss. Heterogenous patterns of energy\nefficiency exist at the national scale and promoting\nenergy-efficient technologies along with managerial\ncapacity development can reduce the gap and provide\nlarge benefits for climate adaptation. Improvements in\ncarbon monitoring and calculation techniques such as\nthe foot-printing of agricultural products can enhance\nenergy-efficiency transition management and uptake\nin agricultural enterprises. Transformation to low-carbon technologies such as\nrenewable energy and energy efficiency can offer\nopportunities for significant climate change mitigation,\nfor example, by providing a substitute to transport fuel\nthat could benefit marginal agricultural resources, while\nsimultaneously contributing to long-term economic growth.\nIn poorer nations, increased energy efficiency in agricultural\nvalue-added production, in particular, can provide large\nmitigation benefits. Under certain scenarios, the efficiency of\nagricultural systems can stagnate and could exert pressure\non grasslands and rangelands, thereby impacting on land\ndegradation and desertification. Rebound effects can also\noccur, with adverse impacts on emissions. 1 Management of\nurban sprawl Unplanned urbanisation leading to sprawl and\nextensification of cities along the rural-urban fringe has\nbeen identified as a driver of forest and agricultural land\nloss and a threat to food production around cities. It\nhas been estimated that urban expansion will result in\na 1.8–2.4% loss of global croplands by 2030. This rapid\nurban expansion is especially strong in new emerging\ntowns and cities in Asia and Africa. Policies to prevent such\nurbanisation have included integrated land-use planning,\nagricultural zoning ordinances and agricultural districts,\nurban redevelopment, arable land reclamation, and\ntransfer/purchase of development rights or easements. The prevention of uncontrolled urban sprawl may provide\nadaptation co-benefits, but adverse side effects for\nadaptation might arise due to restricted ability of people\nto move in response to climate change. Barbero-Sierra et al. 2013; Bren\nd’Amour et al. 2016; Cai et al.\n2013; Chen 2007; Francis et al.\n2012; Gibson et al. 2015; Lee et al.\n2015; Qian et al. 2015; Shen et al.\n2017; Tan et al. 2009
2 Livelihood\ndiversification When households’ livelihoods depend on a small number\nof sources of income without much diversification, and\nwhen those income sources are in fields that are highly\nclimate dependent, like agriculture and fishing, this\ndependence can put food security and livelihoods at risk.\nLivelihood diversification (drawing from a portfolio of\ndissimilar sources of livelihood as a tool to spread risk)\nhas been identified as one option to increase incomes\nand reduce poverty, increase food security, and promote\nclimate resilience and risk reduction. Livelihood diversification offers benefits for desertification\nand land degradation, particularly through non-traditional\ncrops or trees in agroforestry systems which improve soil.\nLivelihood diversification may increase on-farm biodiversity\ndue to these investments in more ecosystem-mimicking\nproduction systems, like agroforestry and polycultures.\nDiversification into non-agricultural fields, such as wage\nlabour or trading, is increasingly favoured by farmers as\na low-cost strategy, particularly to respond to increasing\nclimate risks. 3 Use of local seeds Using local seeds (also called seed sovereignty) refers\nto use of non-improved, non-commercial seeds varieties.\nThese can be used and stored by local farmers as low-cost\ninputs and can often help contribute to the conservation of\nlocal varieties and landraces, increasing local biodiversity.\nMany local seeds also require no pesticide or fertiliser use,\nleading to less land degradation in their use. Use of local seeds is important in the many parts of\nthe developing world that do not rely on commercial\nseed inputs. Promotion of local seed-saving initiatives\ncan include seed networks, banks and exchanges, and\nnon-commercial open source plant breeding. These locally\ndeveloped seeds can help protect local agrobiodiversity and\ncan often be more climate resilient than generic commercial\nvarieties, although the impacts on food security and overall\nland degradation are inconclusive.
