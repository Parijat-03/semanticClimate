2 FAQ 3.1 | How Do We Know Humans Are Responsible for Climate Change?
3
4 The dominant role of humans in driving recent climate change is clear. This conclusion is based on a synthesis of\ninformation from multiple lines of evidence, including direct observations of recent changes in Earth’s climate;\nanalyses of tree rings, ice cores, and other long-term records documenting how the climate has changed in the
5 past; and computer simulations based on the fundamental physics that governs the climate system.\nClimate is influenced by a range of factors. There are two main natural drivers of variations in climate on time
6 scales of decades to centuries. The first is variations in the sun’s activity, which alter the amount of incoming\nenergy from the sun. The second is large volcanic eruptions, which increase the number of small particles (aerosols)\nin the upper atmosphere that reflect sunlight and cool the surface–an effect that can last for several years\n(see also FAQ 3.2). The main human drivers of climate change are increases in the atmospheric concentrations\nof greenhouse gases and of aerosols from burning fossil fuels, land use and other sources. The greenhouse\ngases trap infrared radiation near the surface, warming the climate. Aerosols, like those produced naturally\nby volcanoes, on average cool the climate by increasing the reflection of sunlight. Multiple lines of evidence
7 demonstrate that human drivers are the main cause of recent climate change.\nThe current rates of increase of the concentration of the major greenhouse gases (carbon dioxide, methane
8 and nitrous oxide) are unprecedented over at least the last 800,000 years. Several lines of evidence clearly show\nthat these increases are the results of human activities. The basic physics underlying the warming effect of\ngreenhouse gases on the climate has been understood for more than a century, and our current understanding\nhas been used to develop the latest generation climate models (see FAQ 3.3). Like weather forecasting models,\nclimate models represent the state of the atmosphere on a grid and simulate its evolution over time based on\nphysical principles. They include a representation of the ocean, sea ice and the main processes important in
9 driving climate and climate change.\nResults consistently show that such climate models can only reproduce the observed warming (black line in
10 FAQ 3.1, Figure 1) when including the effects of human activities (grey band in FAQ 3.1, Figure 1), in particular\nthe increasing concentrations of greenhouse gases. These climate models show a dominant warming effect of\ngreenhouse gas increases (red band, which shows the warming effects of greenhouse gases by themselves),\nwhich has been partly offset by the cooling effect of increases in atmospheric aerosols (blue band). By contrast,\nsimulations that include only natural processes, including internal variability related to El Niño and other similar\nvariations, as well as variations in the activity of the sun and emissions from large volcanoes (green band in\nFAQ 3.1, Figure 1), are not able to reproduce the observed warming. The fact that simulations including only\nnatural processes show much smaller temperature increases indicates that natural processes alone cannot explain\nthe strong rate of warming observed. The observed rate can only be reproduced when human influence is added
11 to the simulations.\nMoreover, the dominant effect of human activities is apparent not only in the warming of global surface
12 temperature, but also in the pattern of warming in the lower atmosphere and cooling in the stratosphere,\nwarming of the ocean, melting of sea ice, and many other observed changes. An additional line of evidence for\nthe role of humans in driving climate change comes from comparing the rate of warming observed over recent\ndecades with that which occurred prior to human influence on climate. Evidence from tree rings and other\npaleoclimate records shows that the rate of increase of global surface temperature observed over the past fifty
13 years exceeded that which occurred in any previous 50-year period over the past 2000 years (see FAQ 2.1).\nTaken together, this evidence shows that humans are the dominant cause of observed global warming over
14 recent decades.
