4 System Level Explanations 31
Systems explanations help explain the dynamics of transitions toward sustainable development while
32
explicitly uncovering links between the human and natural worlds, the socio-cultural embeddedness of
33
technology, and the inertia behind high-carbon development pathways. This line of thinking often
34
envisages transitions emerging from complex systems in which many different elements interact at
35
small scales and spontaneously self-organize to produce behaviour that is unexpected, unmanaged and
36
fundamentally different from the sum of the system’s constituent parts.
37
38
Social-ecological systems theory describes the processes of exchange and interaction between human
39
and ecological systems, investigating in particular non-linear feedback occurring across different scales
40
(Folke 2006; Holling 2001). This approach has informed subsequent theoretical and empirical
41
developments, including the ‘planetary boundaries’ approach (Rockström et al. 2009),
42
conceptualizations of vulnerability and adaptive capacity (Hinkel 2011; Pelling 2010) and more recent
43
explorations of urban resilience (Romero-Lankao et al. 2016) and regenerative sustainability (Clayton
44
and Radcliffe 2018; Robinson and Cole 2015). Employing a systems lens to address the ‘root causes’
45
of unsustainable development pathways (such as dysfunctional social or economic arrangements) rather
46
than the ‘symptoms’ (dwelling quality, vehicle efficiency, etc.) can trigger the non-linear change needed
47
for a transformation to take place (Pelling et al. 2015). Exploring synergies between climate-change
48
adaptation, mitigation and other sustainability priorities (such as biodiversity and social equity, for
49
instance) (Beg 2002; Burch et al. 2014; Shaw et al. 2014) may help to yield these transformative
50
outcomes, though data regarding the specific nature of these synergies is still emerging.
