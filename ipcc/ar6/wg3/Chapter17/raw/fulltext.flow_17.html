17.3.2 Short-term and long-term transitions 15
It is increasingly being recognised that sustainable-development policy goals and meeting short- and
16
long-terms climate policy goals are closely linked (IPCC 2018). It is also being realised that, under the
17
Paris Agreement, climate change policies should be integrated into sustainable development agendas,
18
while the UN 2030 Agenda as well includes SDG 13 on climate actions. In this way, both UN
19
agreements provide joint opportunities for systematic transitions in support of both climate change and
20
sustainable development. Achievement of the Paris Agreement’s goals will require a rapid and deep
21
worldwide transition in all GHG emissions sectors, including land-use, energy, industry, buildings,
22
transport and cities, as well as in consumption and behaviour (United Nations Environment Programme
23
2019). Meeting the goals of such a transformation requires that the long-term targets and pathways to
24
fulfil the stabilization scenarios play an important role in guiding the direction and pathways of short-
25
term transitions. There is therefore a need for long- and short-term policies and investment decisions to
26
be closely coordinated.
27
28
In the context of the Paris Agreement, countries have submitted their initial plans for the
29
decarbonization of their economies to the UNFCCC in the form of their so-called national determined
30
contributions (NDCs). The ambitions of the NDCs are closely related to the ongoing UNFCCC
31
negotiations over the financial measures and forms of compensation. Although the Paris Agreement
32
emphasizes the links between climate policies and sustainable development, the UN’s 2030 Agenda
33
and the SDGs are not very well represented at present in the NDCs, according to Fuso Nerini et al.
34
(2019). Very few of the NDCs include any reference to the SDGs, which Fuso Nerini et al. (2019)
35
highlight as a barrier to the successful implementation of the Paris Agreement, which induces them to
36
call for a more holistic policy approach. Campagnolo and Davide (2019) have assessed the impacts of
37
the submitted NDCs on poverty eradication and inequalities of income based on empirical research and
38
a global CGE model. One conclusion is that the NDCs of less developed countries would tend to reduce
39
poverty alleviation, but this can be offset if international financial support is provided for the mitigation
40
actions.
41
42
The alignment of climate-policy targets in the NDCs with sustainable development has been assessed
43
by means of integrated assessment models (IAMs), macroeconomic and sectoral modelling. Iyer et al.
44
(2018) based on IAM-based studies, the implications of framing NDCs being placed more narrowly on
45
mitigation targets rather than on a framing in which the impacts on sustainable development were
46
explicitly taken into consideration. It was thus concluded that some SDGs would be directly supported
47
as a side benefit of the climate policy targets included in the NDCs, while other SDGs needed a special
48
policy design going beyond narrow climate policy objectives. Iyer et al. (2018) also assessed the
49
regional distribution of efforts in terms of domestic mitigation costs and SDG impacts and concluded
50
that the geographical distribution of mitigation costs and SDG benefits were not similar, so a special
51
effort would be needed to match climate policies and policies to meet the SDGs. Accordingly, a national
52
decision-making perspective suggests that SDGs should be integrated into national climate policies.
