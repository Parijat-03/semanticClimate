Land-based, long-range, heavy-duty trucks can be decarbonised through battery-electric haulage 1 (including the use of electric road systems), complemented by hydrogen- and biofuel-based fuels 2 in some contexts. These same technologies and expanded use of available electric rail systems can 3 support rail decarbonisation (medium confidence). Initial deployments of battery-electric, hydrogen- 4 and bio-based haulage are underway, and commercial operations of some of these technologies are 5 considered feasible by 2030 (medium confidence). Decarbonisation options for shipping and aviation still require R&D, though advanced biofuels, 12 ammonia, and synthetic fuels are emerging as viable options (medium confidence). Increased 13 efficiency has been insufficient to limit the emissions from shipping and aviation, and natural gas-based 14 fuels are expected to be inadequate to meet stringent decarbonisation goals for these segments (high 15 confidence). High energy density, low carbon fuels are required, but they have not yet reached 16 commercial scale. Advanced biofuels could provide low carbon jet fuel (medium confidence). The 17 production of synthetic fuels using low-carbon hydrogen with CO2 captured through DACCS/BECCS 18 could provide jet and marine fuels but these options still require demonstration at scale (low confidence). 19 Ammonia produced with low-carbon hydrogen could also serve as a marine fuel (medium confidence). 20 Deployment of these fuels requires reductions in production costs. Scenarios from bottom-up and top-down models indicate that, without intervention, CO2 23 emissions from transport could grow in the range of 16% and 50% by 2050 (medium confidence). 24 The scenarios literature projects continued growth in demand for freight and passenger services, 25 particularly in developing countries in Africa and Asia (high confidence). This growth is projected to 26 take place across all transport modes. Increases in demand notwithstanding, scenarios that limit 27 warming to 1.5°C degree with no or limited overshoot suggest that a 59% reduction (42-68% 28 interquartile range) in transport-related CO2 emissions by 2050, compared to modelled 2020 levels is 29 required. While many global scenarios place greater reliance on emissions reduction in sectors other 30 than transport, a quarter of the 1.5°C scenarios describe transport-related CO2 emissions reductions in 31 excess of 68% (relative to modelled 2020 levels) (medium confidence). Illustrative Mitigation Pathways 32 IMP-ren and IMP-LD (TS 4.2) describe emission reductions of 80% and 90% in the transport sector, 33 respectively, by 2050. Transport-related emission reductions, however, may not happen uniformly 34 across regions. For example, transport emissions from the Developed Countries, and Eastern Europe 35 and West-Central Asia countries decrease from 2020 levels by 2050 across all scenarios limiting global 36 warming to 1.5°C by 2100, but could increase in Africa, Asia and developing Pacific (APC), Latin 37 America and Caribbean, and the Middle East in some of these scenarios. {10.7} 38
The scenarios literature indicates that fuel and technology shifts are crucial in reducing carbon 39 emissions to meet temperature goals (high confidence).
