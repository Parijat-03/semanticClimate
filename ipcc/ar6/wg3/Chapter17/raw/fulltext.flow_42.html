17.3.3.4 Cities, Infrastructure and Transportation
22
With 80% of the global population expected to be urban by 2050, cities will shape development paths
23
for the foreseeable future (United Nation 2018). The challenge for many policymakers is to construct
24
development paths that make cities clean, prosperous and liveable while mitigating climate change and
25
building resilience to heatwaves, flooding and other climate risks. The IPCC 1.5 report sees achieving
26
these objectives as feasible: cities could potentially realize significant climate and sustainable-
27
development benefits from shifting development paths (Wiktorowicz et al. 2018). The section assesses
28
the synergies and trade-offs between meeting the SDGs and climate-change mitigation, as well as
29
providing a general overview of mitigation options in cities and of enabling factors, including city
30
networks and plans for jointly addressing the SDGs and climate-change mitigation.
31
32
Chapter 8 concludes that urban areas potentially offer several joint benefits between mitigation and the
33
SDGs, and that since AR5, evidence of the co-benefits of urban mitigation continues to grow. In
34
developing countries, a co-benefits approach that frames climate objectives alongside other
35
development benefits arise increasingly being seen as an important concept justifying and driving
36
climate-change actions in developing countries (Sethi and Puppum De Oliveria 2018; Seto et al. 2016).
37
38
Evidence of the co-benefits of urban mitigation measures on human health has increased significantly
39
since the IPCC AR5, especially through the use of health-impact assessments in cities like Geneva,
40
where energy savings and cleaner energy-supply structures based on measures for urban planning,
41
heating and transport have reduced CO
2
, NOx and PM10 emissions and increased the opportunities for
42
physical activity for the prevention of cardiovascular diseases (Diallo et al. 2016).
43
There is increasing evidence that climate-mitigation measures can lower health risks that are related to
44
energy poverty, especially in vulnerable groups, such as the elderly (Monforti-Ferrario et al. 2019).
45
Moreover, the use of urban forestry and green infrastructure as both a climate mitigation and an
46
adaptation measure can reduce heat stress (Kim and Coseo 2019; Privitera and La Rosa 2017) while
47
removing air pollutants to improve air quality (Scholz et al. 2018; De la Sota et al. 2019) and enhancing
48
well-being, including contributions to local development and possible reductions of inequalities (Lwasa
49
et al. 2015). Other studies evidence the potential to reduce premature mortality by up to 7,000 in 53
50
towns and cities and to create 93,000 net new jobs and lower global climate costs, as well as reduce
51
personal energy costs based on road maps for renewable energy transformations (Jacobson et al. 2018).
