3
4 Human-driven climate change can alter the regional characteristics of a climate hazard by changing the magnitude\nor intensity of the climate hazard, the frequency with which it occurs, the duration that hazardous conditions\npersist, the timing when the hazard occurs, or the spatial extent threatened by the hazard. By examining each\nof these aspects of a hazard’s profile change, climate services may provide climate risk information that allows
5 decision makers to better tailor adaptation, mitigation and risk management strategies.\nA climate hazard is a climate condition with the potential to harm natural systems or society. Examples include
6 heatwaves, droughts, heavy snowfall events and sea level rise. Climate scientists look for patterns in climatic\nimpact-drivers to detect the signature of changing hazards that may influence stakeholder planning (FAQ 12.1).\nClimate service providers work with stakeholders and impacts experts to identify key system responses and\ntolerance thresholds (FAQ 12.2) and then examine historical observations and future climate projections to\nidentify associated changes to the characteristics of a regional hazard’s profile. 10 For example, increases to the number of heavy snowfall events, tornadoes, or floods experienced in a year or in
11 a decade.\nDuration is the length of time over which hazardous conditions persist beyond a threshold, such as an increase
12 in the number of consecutive days where maximum air temperature exceeds 35°C, the number of consecutive
13 months of drought conditions, or the number of days that a tropical cyclone affects a location.\nTiming captures the occurrence of a hazardous event in relation to the course of a day, season, year, or other
14 period in which sectoral elements are evolving or co-dependent (such as the time of year when migrating animals\nexpect to find a seasonal food supply). Examples include a shift towards an earlier day of the year when the last\nspring frost occurs or a delay in the typical arrival date for the first seasonal rains, the length of the winter period\nwhen the ground is typically covered by snow, or a reduction in the typical time needed for soil moisture to move
15 from normal to drought conditions.\nSpatial extent is the region in which a hazardous condition is expected, such as the area currently threatened by
16 tropical cyclones, geographical areas where the coldest day of the year restricts a particular pest or pathogen,\nterrain where permafrost is present, the area that would flood following a common storm, zones where climate
17 conditions are conducive to outdoor labour, or the size of a marine heatwave.\nHazard profile changes are often intertwined or stem from related physical changes to the climate system. For
18 example, changes in the frequency and magnitude of extreme events are often directly related to each other\nas a result of atmospheric dynamics and chemical processes. In many cases, one aspect of hazard change is more\napparent than others, which may provide a first emergent signal indicating a larger set of changes to come
19 (FAQ 1.2).\nInformation about how a hazard has changed or will change helps stakeholders prioritize more robust adaptation,
20 mitigation and risk management strategies. For example, allocation of limited disaster relief resources may be\ndesigned to recognize that tropical cyclones are projected to become more intense even as the frequency of\nthose storms may not change. Planning may also factor in the fact that even heatwaves that are not record-\nbreaking in their intensity can still be problematic for vulnerable populations when they persist over a long\nperiod. Likewise, firefighters recognize new logistical challenges in the lengthening of the fire weather season\nand an expansion of fire conditions into parts of the world where fires were not previously a great concern.\nStrong engagement between climate scientists and stakeholders therefore helps climate services tailor and\ncommunicate clear information about the types of changing climate hazards to be addressed in resilience efforts.
