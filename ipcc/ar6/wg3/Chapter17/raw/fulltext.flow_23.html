17.3.2.2 Renewable energy penetration and fossil-fuel phase-out
4
As pointed out in Chapter 6, the achievement of long-term temperature goals in line with the Paris
5
Agreement requires the rapid penetration of renewable energy and a timely phasing out of fossil fuels,
6
especially coal, from the global energy system. Limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot
7
means that global CO
2
emissions must reach “net zero” in 2050/2060 (IPCC 2018). Net zero emissions
8
imply that fossil fuel use is minimised and replaced by renewables and other low-carbon primary forms
9
of energy, or that the residual emissions from fossil fuels are offset by carbon dioxide removal. The
10
1.5°C scenario requires a 2-3% annual improvement rate in carbon intensities till 2050. The historical
11
record only shows a slight improvement in the carbon intensity rate of global energy supplies, far from
12
what is required to limit likely global warming to 2°C, or limit warming to 1.5°C with no or limited
13
overshoot.
14
15
The role of coal in the global energy system is changing fast. Given the global temperature goals of the
16
Paris Agreement, the global coal sector needs a transition to near zero by 2050 – earlier in some regions
17
(Bauer et al. 2018; IEA 2017; IPCC 2018). Other global trends, including air quality, water shortages,
18
the improved cost efficiencies of renewables, the technical availability of energy storage and the
19
economic rebalancing of emerging countries, are also driving global coal consumption t to a plateau
20
followed by a reverse (Sator 2018; Spencer et al. 2018). The world should be prepared for a managed
21
transition away from coal and should identify appropriate transition options for the future of coal, which
22
can include both the penetration of renewable energy and improvements in energy efficiency (Shah et
23
al. 2015).
24
25
Phasing out fossil fuels from energy systems is technically possible and is estimated to be relatively
26
low in cost (Chapter 6). The cost of low-carbon alternatives, including onshore and offshore wind, solar
27
PV and electric vehicles, has been reduced substantially in recent years and has become competitive
28
with fossil fuels (Shen et al. 2020). However, studies show that replacing fossil fuels with renewables
29
can have major synergies and trade-offs with a broader agenda of sustainable development (Swain and
30
Karimu 2020), including land use and food security (McCollum et al. 2018), decent jobs and economic
31
growth (Swain and Karimu 2020). Clarke et al. (2014:Table 6.7) provides detailed mapping of the
32
sectoral co-benefits and adverse side-impacts of and links to transformation pathways. In Section
33
17.3.3.7 in this chapter, this is supplemented with a mapping of the synergies and trade-offs between
34
the deployment of renewable energy and the SDGs.
35
36
The general conclusion is that the potential co-benefits of renewable energy end-use measures outweigh
37
the adverse impacts in most sectors and in relation to the SDGs, though this is not the case for the
38
AFOLU (Agriculture, Forestry and Other Land Uses) sectors. Some locally negative economic impacts
39
can result in increased energy costs and competition over land areas and water resources. Some sectors
40
may also experience increasing unemployment as a consequence of the transition process. Although the
41
deployment of renewable energy will generate a new industry and associated jobs and benefits in some
42
areas and economies, these impacts will often not directly replace or offset activities in areas that have
43
been heavily dependent on the fossil-fuel industry.

46

47

48

49

51
