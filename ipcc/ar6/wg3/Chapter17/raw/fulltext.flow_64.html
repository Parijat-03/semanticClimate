National and sub-national, sectoral and cross-sectoral, short- and long-term transition studies have
6
assessed the links between sustainable development and mitigation policies and synergies and the trade-

8
trade-offs, despite the actual impacts of policy implementation depending on scale, context and the
9

From a cross-sectoral perspective, it can be concluded that the AFOLU sector offers many low-cost
12
mitigation options with synergetic SDG impacts, which, however, can also create trade-offs between
13
land-use for food, energy, forest and biodiversity. Some options can help to mitigate such trade-offs,
14
like agricultural practices, forest conservation and soil carbon sequestration. Lifestyle changes,
15
including dietary changes and reduced food waste, could jointly support the SDGs and mitigation.

Industry also offers several mitigation options with SDG synergies, for example, related to energy
17
efficiency and the circular economy. Some of the renewable-energy options in industry could indicate
18
some trade-offs in relation to land use, with implications for food- and water security and costs. Cities
19
provide a promising basis for implementing mitigation with SDG synergies, particularly if urban
20
planning, transportation, infrastructure and settlements are coordinated jointly. Similarly, studies of the
21
building sector have identified many synergies between the SDGs and mitigation, but there are issues
22
related to the costs of new technologies. Also, in relation to households and buildings, important equity
23
issues emerge due to the ability of low-income groups to afford the introduction of new technologies.
24
Altogether these cross-sectoral conclusions create a need for policies to address both synergies and
25
trade-offs, as well as for coordination between different sectoral domains. Context-specific assessments
26
of synergies and trade-offs are here important, as is sharing the benefits and costs associated with
27
mitigation policies.

Several opportunities for creating SDG synergies and avoiding trade-offs have also been identified in
30
relation to integrated adaptation and mitigation policies. The AFOLU sector has a large potential for
31
integrating adaptation and mitigation policies related to agriculture, bioenergy crops, forestry and water
32
use. As was concluded for mitigation options, integrated adaptation and mitigation policies also entail
33
the risks of creating trade-offs in relation to food, water, energy access and biodiversity. There are
34
several potentially strong links between climate-change adaptation in industry and climate-change
35
adaptation more generally. Various supply chains can be affected by climate change, and mitigation
36
options related to energy and water supply can be disrupted by climate events, implying that great
37
benefits may come from integrating adaptation in industrial planning efforts. Adaptation options in
38
industry can imply increasing the demand for packaging materials such as plastics and for access to
39
refrigeration, which are also major sources of GHG emissions, which then would require further
40
mitigation options. Mitigation and the co-benefits of adaptation in urban areas in relation to air quality,
41
health, green jobs and equality issues can in most cases to be synergetic and can also support the SDGs.
42
One exception are compact cities, with their trade-offs between mitigation and adaptation because
43
decreasing urban sprawl can increase the risks of flooding and heat stress. Detailed mapping of
44
mitigation and adaptation in urban areas shows that there are many, very close interactions between the
45
two policy domains and that coordinated governance across sectors is therefore called for.
