Holistic planning and the nexus approach 1
Poor sectoral coordination and institutional fragmentation have triggered a wide range of unsustainable
2
uses of resources and threatened the long-term sustainability of food, water and energy security (Rasul
3
2016). Greater policy coherence among the three sectors is critical to moving to a sustainable and
4
efficient use of resources (United Nations 2019), given that political ambition, values, the energy mix,
5
infrastructure and innovation capacities collectively shape transition outcomes (Neofytou et al. 2020).
6
Capacity- and coalition-building, particularly among sub-national and non-state actors (e.g. non-
7
governmental organizations) is a particularly important enabler of greater coherence (Bernstein and
8
Hoffmann 2018). The nexus approach, a systems-based methodology that focuses attention on the many
9
ways in which natural resources are deeply interwoven and mutually interdependent, can strengthen
10
coordination and help to avoid maladaptive pathways (Cremades et al. 2016).
11
12
A major shift is required in the decision-making process in the direction of taking a holistic view,
13
developing institutional mechanisms to coordinate the actions of diverse actors and strengthening
14
complementarities and synergies (Nikas et al. 2020; Rasul 2016). Currently, nexus approaches have
15
moved from purely conceptual arguments to application and implementation. (Liu et al. 2018) suggest
16
the need for a systematic procedure and provide perspectives on future directions. These include
17
expanding nexus frameworks that take into account interaction linkages with the SDGs, incorporating
18
overlooked drivers and regions, diversifying nexus toolboxes and making these strategies central to
19
policy-making and governance in integrating and implementing the SDGs.
20
21
In respect of processes, (Seyfang and Haxeltine 2012) found a lack of realistic and achievable
22
expectations among both members (internally) and the wider public (externally), which hampers the
23
acceleration of transitions. This movement could concentrate strategically on developing and promoting
24
short-term steps towards shared long-term visions, including clearly identifiable goals and end-points.
25
Sustainability science must link research on problem structures with a solutions-oriented approach that
26
seeks to understand, conceptualize and foster experiments in how socio-technical innovations for
27
sustainability develop, are diffused and are scaled up (Miller et al. 2014).
28
29
Various strategies and processes have been explored that might facilitate the translation of barriers into
30
enablers, thus accelerating transitions to sustainable development. Common themes include frequent
31
monitoring and system evaluation to reveal the barriers in the first place, the collaborative co-creation
32
and envisioning of pathways toward sustainable development, ambitious goal-setting, the strategic
33
tackling of sources of path dependence or inertia, iterative evaluations of progress and risk management,
34
adaptive management and building in opportunities for agile course-correction at multiple levels of
35
governance (Burch et al. 2014; Halbe et al. 2015). Given the political infeasibility of stable, long-term
36
climate policies, the better choice may be to embrace uncertainty in specific policies but entrench the
37
low-carbon transition as the overarching goal. Framing climate policy too narrowly, rather than taking
38
a more holistic, sustainable development-oriented approach, may tie success to single policies, rather
39
than allowing for system-wide change.
40
41
Decarbonisation may be encouraged by embedding the transition in a broader socio-economic agenda,
42
focusing on constructing social legitimacy to justify the transformation, encouraging municipalities
43
with a material interest in the transition and reforming institutions to support the long-term transition
44
goals (Rosenbloom et al. 2019). In jurisdictions where climate and energy policy have been integrated
45
and harmonized, such as the UK, progress has been made in transitioning to sustainable energy (Warren
46
et al. 2016).

50
low-carbon energy generation and not least more efficient resource-planning (UN- UNDRR 2019).
