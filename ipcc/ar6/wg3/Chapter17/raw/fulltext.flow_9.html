One way a link is made between climate and the SDGs is through Voluntary National Reviews (VNRs).
6
Paralleling the bottom-up orientation of the Paris Agreement and the NDCs, every year approximately
7
forty countries voluntarily share their VNRs with the international community at the High-Level
8
Political Forum (HLPF). Even more flexible than the NDCs, the VNRs can include content such as a
9
summary of key policies and measures that are intended to achieve the SDGs, a list of the means of
10
implementation that support the SDGs, and related challenges and needs. The VNRs also often cover
11
SDG 13 (on climate change) as well as many other issues connected with climate change. Even with
12
these links, implementation of the SDGs should be mentioned as part of national development processes
13
reflecting different countries’ different priorities, visions and plans (Hanson and Puplampu 2018;
14
Marcotullio et al. 2018; OECD 2016; Puplampu et al. 2017; Srikanth 2018).
15
16
Yet another way that the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development underlines the importance of
17
capturing synergies is its calls for policy coherence (goals 17 and 14). Policy coherence and integration
18
between sectors are two of the most critical factors in breaking down the silo mode of working of
19
different sectors. Working across climate and other sustainability agendas is essential to coherence.

21
A final way that the sustainability and climate agendas have been linked is through vertical integration.
22
Following a similar trend that appeared with Agenda 21, for which many cities adopted local plans, a
23
growing number of cities have introduced Voluntary Local Reviews. The VLRs resemble the VNRs,
24
but place the emphasis on local actions and needs regarding the SDGs (and some links to climate
25
change) (Ortíz-Moya et al. 2021). The 2019 SDG Report shows that 150 countries have developed
26
national urban plans, almost half of them also being in the implementation phase (United Nations
27
General Assembly 2019).

17.1.3 Integrating Climate Change and Sustainable Development in Other Policymaking 30
Processes 31
Other non-UN-led initiatives involving international organizations or clusters of countries have also
32
helped to raise the issue of sustainable development as a framework for mitigation. The OECD, for
33
instance, assesses different types of investments and economic activities with reference to their
34
significance for environmental sustainability (OECD 2020), while G20 countries have drawn up action
35
agendas with sustainable development at the (UToronto 2016). Meanwhile, the Petersberg Climate
36
Dialogue, a political movement convened by major country-group representatives launched in 2010 by
37
the German government, has also called for sustainability to be an intrinsic part of the transition
38
(UNFCCO 2020; BMU 2018).
39
Due in part to the shifting orientation of these international processes, there is growing evidence of
40
action on climate change and sustainable development at other levels of decision-making. National
41
policies often aim to implement climate change policies in the context of sustainable development
42
(Chimhowu et al. 2019; Chirambo 2018; ECLAC 2017; Fuseini and Kemp 2015; Galli et al. 2018;
43
Haywood et al. 2019; Ministry of Environment of Jordan 2016; McKenzie and Abdulkadri 2018;
44
UNDESA 2016, 2017, 2018; UN Women 2017). Some countries are adjusting their existing policies to
45
build on themes familiar to sustainable development (Lucas et al. 2016), including renewable energy
46
and energy efficiency (Fastenrath and Braun 2018; Kousksou et al. 2015), urban planning (Gorissen et
47
al. 2018; Loorbach et al. 2016; Mendizabal et al. 2018), health systems (Pencheon 2018; Roschnik et
48
al. 2017) and agricultural systems (Lipper and Zilberman 2018; Shaw and Roberts 2017). Cross-cutting
49
and integrated approaches, such as the circular economy, have also been gaining traction in some
50
European countries (EESC 2015) and G20 countries (Noura et al. 2020). Many of these efforts have
51
also extended up to the regional and down to the local level (Gorissen et al. 2018; Hess 2014; Shaw and
52
Roberts 2017).
