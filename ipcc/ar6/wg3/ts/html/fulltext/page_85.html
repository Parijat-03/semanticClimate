While several technological options exist for decarbonizing the main industrial feedstock 3 chemicals and their derivatives, the costs vary widely (high confidence). Fossil fuel-based 4 feedstocks are inexpensive and still without carbon pricing, and their biomass- and electricity-based 5 replacements are expected to be more expensive. The chemical industry consumes large amounts of 6 hydrogen, ammonia, methanol, carbon monoxide, ethylene, propylene, benzene, toluene, and mixed 7 xylenes & aromatics from fossil feedstock, and from these basic chemicals produces tens of thousands 8 of derivative end-use chemicals. Hydrogen, biogenic or air-capture carbon, and collected plastic waste 9 for the primary feedstocks can greatly reduce total emissions. Biogenic carbon feedstock is expected to 10 be limited due to competing land-uses. {11.4.1} 11
Light industry and manufacturing can be largely decarbonized through switching to low GHG 12 fuels (e.g., biofuels and hydrogen) and electricity (e.g., for electrothermal heating and heat 13 pumps) (high confidence). Most of these technologies are already mature, for example for low 14 temperature heat, but a major challenge is the current low cost of fossil CH4 and coal relative to low and 15 zero GHG electricity, hydrogen, and biofuels. {11.4.1} 16
The pulp and paper industry has significant biogenic carbon emissions but relatively small fossil 17 carbon emissions. Pulp mills have access to biomass residues and by-products and in paper mills 18 the use of process heat at low to medium temperatures allows for electrification (high confidence). 19 Competition for feedstock will increase if wood substitutes for building materials and petrochemicals 20 feedstock. The pulp and paper industry can also be a source of biogenic carbon dioxide, carbon for 21 organic chemicals feedstock, and for CDR using CCS. {11.4.1} 22
The geographical distribution of renewable resources has implications for industry (medium 23 confidence). The potential for zero emission electricity and low-cost hydrogen from electrolysis 24 powered by solar and wind, or hydrogen from other very low emission sources, may reshape where 25 currently energy and emissions intensive basic materials production is located, how value chains are 26 organized, trade patterns, and what gets transported in international shipping. Regions with bountiful 27 solar and wind resources, or low fugitive CH4 co-located with CCS geology, may become exporters of 28 hydrogen or hydrogen carriers such as methanol and ammonia, or home to the production of iron and 29 steel, organic platform chemicals, and other energy intensive basic materials. {11.2, 11.4, Box 11.1} 30
The level of policy maturity and experience varies widely across the mitigation options (high 31 confidence). Energy efficiency is a well-established policy field with decades of experience from 32 voluntary and negotiated agreements, regulations, energy auditing and demand side-management 33 (DSM) programs. In contrast, materials demand management and efficiency are not well understood 34 and addressed from a policy perspective. Barriers to recycling that policy could address are often 35 specific to the different material loops (e.g., copper contamination for steel and lack of technologies or 36 poor economics for plastics) or waste management systems. For electrification and fuel switching the 37 focus has so far been mainly on innovation and developing technical supply-side solutions rather than 38 creating market demand. {11.5.2, 11.6} 39
