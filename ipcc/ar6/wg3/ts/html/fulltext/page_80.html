TS. 5.5 Industry 1
The industry chapter focuses on new developments since AR5 and emphasises the role of the energy-2 intensive and emissions-intensive basic materials industries in strategies for reaching net zero 3 emissions. The Paris Agreement, the SDGs and the COVID-19 pandemic provide a new context for the 4 evolution of industry and mitigation of industry greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions (high confidence). 5 {11.1.1} 6
Net zero CO2 industrial sector emissions are possible but challenging (high confidence). Energy 7 efficiency will continue to be important. Reduced materials demand, material efficiency, and circular 8 economy solutions can reduce the need for primary production. Primary production options include 9 switching to new processes that use low to zero GHG energy carriers and feedstocks (e.g., electricity, 10 hydrogen, biofuels, and carbon dioxide capture and utilisation (CCU) to provide carbon feedstocks). 11 Carbon capture and storage (CCS) will be required to mitigate remaining CO2 emissions {11.3}. These 12 options require substantial scaling up of electricity, hydrogen, recycling, CO2, and other infrastructure, 13 as well as phase-out or conversion of existing industrial plants. While improvements in the GHG 14 intensities of major basic materials have nearly stagnated over the last 30 years, analysis of historical 15 technology shifts and newly available technologies indicate these intensities can be significantly 16 reduced by mid-century. {11.2, 11.3, 11.4} 17
Industry sector emissions have been growing faster since 2000 than emissions in any other sector, 18 driven by increased basic materials extraction and production (high confidence). GHG emissions 19 attributed to the industrial sector originate from fuel combustion, process emissions, product use and 20 waste, which jointly accounted for 14.1 GtCO2-eq or 24% of all direct anthropogenic emissions in 2019, 21 second behind the energy supply sector. Industry is a leading GHG emitter - 20 GtCO2-eq or 34% of 22 global emissions in 2019 - if indirect emissions from power and heat generation are included. The share 23 of emissions originating from direct fuel combustion is decreasing and was 7 GtCO2-eq, 50% of direct 24 industrial emissions in 2019. {11.2.2} 25
Global material intensity – the in-use stock of manufactured capital in tonnes per unit of GDP– 26 is increasing (high confidence). In-use stock of manufactured capital per capita has been growing 27 faster than GDP per capita since 2000. Total global in-use stock of manufactured capital grew by 3.4% 28 yr-1in 2000–2019. At the same time, per capita material stocks in several developed countries have 29 stopped growing, showing a decoupling from GDP per capita. {11.2.1, 11.3.1} 30
The demand for plastic has been growing most strongly since 1970 (high confidence). The current 31 >99% reliance on fossil feedstock, very low recycling, and high emissions from petrochemical 32 processes is a challenge for reaching net zero emissions. At the same time, plastics are important for 33 reducing emissions elsewhere, for example, light-weighting vehicles. There are as yet no shared visions 34 for fossil-free plastics, but several possibilities. {11.4.1.3} 35
Scenario analyses show that significant reductions in global GHG emissions and even close to net 36 zero emissions from GHG intensive industry (e.g., steel, plastics, ammonia, and cement) can be 37 achieved by 2050 by deploying multiple available and emerging options (medium confidence). 38 Significant reductions in industry emissions require a reorientation from the historic focus on important 39 but incremental improvements (e.g., energy efficiency) to transformational changes in energy and 40 feedstock sourcing, materials efficiency, and more circular material flows. {11.3, 11.4}41
