The deployment of low-carbon aviation and shipping fuels that support decarbonisation of the 6 transport sector could require changes to national and international governance structures 7 (medium confidence). The UNFCCC does not specifically cover emissions from international shipping 8 and aviation. Reporting emissions from international transport is at the discretion of each country. While 9 the International Civil Aviation Organisation (ICAO) and International Maritime Organisation (IMO) 10 have established emissions reductions targets, only strategies to improve fuel efficiency and demand 11 reductions have been pursued, and there has been minimal commitment to new technologies. {10.5, 12 10.6, 10.7} 13
There are growing concerns about resource availability, labour rights, non-climate 14 environmental impacts, and costs of critical minerals needed for lithium-ion batteries (medium 15 confidence). Emerging national strategies on critical minerals and the requirements from major vehicle 16 manufacturers are leading to new, more geographically diverse mines. The standardisation of battery 17 modules and packaging within and across vehicle platforms, as well as increased focus on design for 18 recyclability are important. Given the high degree of potential recyclability of lithium-ion batteries, a 19 nearly closed-loop system in the future could mitigate concerns about critical mineral issues (medium 20 confidence). {10.3, 10.8} 21
Legislated climate strategies are emerging at all levels of government, and together with pledges 22 for personal choices, could spur the deployment of demand and supply-side transport mitigation 23 strategies (medium confidence). At the local level, legislation can support local transport plans that 24 include commitments or pledges from local institutions to encourage behaviour change by adopting an 25 organisational culture that motivates sustainable behaviour with inputs from the creative arts. Such 26 institution-led mechanisms could include bike-to-work campaigns, free transport passes, parking 27 charges, or eliminating car benefits. Community-based solutions like solar sharing, community 28 charging, and mobility as a service can generate new opportunities to facilitate low-carbon transport 29 futures. At the regional and national levels, legislation can include vehicle and fuel efficiency standards, 30 R&D support, and large-scale investments in low-carbon transport infrastructure.
