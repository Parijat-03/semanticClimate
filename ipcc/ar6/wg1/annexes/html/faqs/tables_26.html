2 FAQ 4.3 | At a Given Level of Global Warming, What Are the Spatial Patterns of Climate Change?
3
4 As the planet warms, climate change does not unfold uniformly across the globe, but some patterns of regional\nchange show clear, direct and consistent relationships to increases in global surface temperature. The Arctic\nwarms more than other regions, land areas warm more than the ocean surface, and the Northern Hemisphere\nmore than the Southern Hemisphere. Precipitation increases over high latitudes, tropics and large parts of\nthe monsoon regions, but decreases over the subtropics. For cases like these, we can infer the direction and\nmagnitude of some regional changes – particularly temperature and precipitation changes – for any given level
5 of global warming.\nThe intensity of climate change will depend on the level of global warming. It is possible to identify certain
6 patterns of regional climate change that occur consistently, but increase in amplitude, across increasing levels of\nglobal warming. Such robust spatial patterns of climate change are largely independent of the specific scenario\n(and pathway in time) that results in a given level of global warming. That is, as long as different scenarios result\nin the same global warming level, irrespective of the time when this level is attained in each scenario, we can\ninfer the patterns of regional change that would result from this warming. When patterns of changes are robust,\nregional consequences can be assessed for all levels of global warming, for all future time periods, and for all
7 scenarios. Temperature and precipitation show such robust patterns of changes that are particularly striking.\nThe high latitudes of the Northern Hemisphere are projected to warm the most, by two to four times the level
8 of global warming – a phenomenon referred to as Arctic amplification (FAQ 4.3, Figure 1, left). Several processes\ncontribute to this high rate of warming, including increases in the absorption of solar radiation due to the loss\nof reflective sea ice and snow in a warmer world. In the Southern Hemisphere, Antarctica is projected to warm\nfaster than the mid-latitude Southern Ocean, but the Southern Hemisphere high latitudes are projected to\nwarm at a reduced amplitude compared to the level of global warming (FAQ 4.3, Figure 1, left). An important\nreason for the relatively slower warming of the Southern Hemisphere high latitudes is the upwelling of Antarctic
9 deep waters that drives a large surface heat uptake in the Southern Ocean.\nThe warming is generally stronger over land than over the ocean, and in the Northern Hemisphere compared to
10 the Southern Hemisphere, and with less warming over the central subpolar North Atlantic and the southernmost\nPacific. The differences are the result of several factors, including differences in how land and ocean areas\nabsorb and retain heat, the fact that there is more land area in the Northern Hemisphere than in the Southern\nHemisphere, and the influence of ocean circulation. In the Southern Hemisphere, robust patterns of relatively\nhigh warming are projected for subtropical South America, southern Africa, and Australia. The relatively strong\nwarming in subtropical southern Africa arises from strong interactions between soil moisture and temperature
11 and from increased solar radiation as a consequence of enhanced subsidence.\nPrecipitation changes are also proportional to the level of global warming (FAQ 4.3, Figure 1, right), although
12 uncertainties are larger than for the temperature change. In the high latitudes of both the Southern and Northern\nHemispheres, increases in precipitation are expected as the planet continues to warm, with larger changes\nexpected at higher levels of global warming (FAQ 4.3, Figure 1, right). The same holds true for the projected\nprecipitation increases over the tropics and large parts of the monsoon regions. General drying is expected\nover the subtropical regions, particularly over the Mediterranean, southern Africa and parts of Australia, South\nAmerica, and south-west North America, as well as over the subtropical Atlantic and parts of the subtropical\nIndian and Pacific Oceans. Increases in precipitation over the tropics and decreases over the subtropics amplify
13 with higher levels of global warming.\nSome regions that are already dry and warm, such as southern Africa and the Mediterranean, are expected to
14
15 become progressively drier and drastically warmer at higher levels of global warming.\nIn summary, climate change will not affect all the parts of the globe evenly. Rather, distinct regional patterns of
16 temperature and precipitation change can be identified, and these changes are projected to amplify as the level\nof global warming increases.
