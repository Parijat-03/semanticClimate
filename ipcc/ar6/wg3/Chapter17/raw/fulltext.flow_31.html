The determination of a just transition is complex and not simply dependent on the allocation of
18
perceived risks or solutions, but rather on how risks and solutions are defined (Forsyth 2014). Acting
19
urgently to achieve environmental solutions or meet transition imperatives has certain risks given the
20
need to go beyond commonplace definitions of the just transition by emphasizing the distributive or
21
procedural aspects. The framing of policies to align with fast and low-cost mitigation without paying
22
sufficient attention to social and economic resilience creates its own potential risks and can enhance
23
social vulnerability rather than address it. The need to distribute climate-change solutions must not
24
delegitimise appropriate economic growth strategies, nor indeed create the additional risks of policy
25
imposition. Perceptions of justice with regard to environmental problems and solutions matter equally.
26
Hence, the types of transition pathway that are chosen may have equality implications. Mitigation at all
27
costs, if done “cheaply and crudely”, can create additional problems for social justice and inclusive
28
development (Forsyth 2014).

The assumption that the benefits of mitigation are enough to offset trade-offs with other policy
31
objectives can be questioned. If one accepts the argument that not all adaptation addresses vulnerability
32
concerns (Kjellén 2006), and that some adaptation strategies can heighten vulnerabilities if there are
33
flaws in their design and implementation, then the same logic applies, namely that not all mitigation is
34
necessarily beneficial. Hence the emphasis on the transition resulting from mitigation should be placed
35
not only on speed or cost effectiveness, but also on legitimacy of the actions, and whether the transition
36
is well designed or not. In short, justice is not always a shorthand for acting ethically, but rather a point
37
of reasoning on what is considered legitimate. Planning for the transition often discounts human rights
38
and social inclusivity that can occur as the result of a rapid transition. The emphasis should be placed
39
on the management of the transition rather than the speed – for instance, if in the rush to build new
40
hydropower energy sources implies that populations are displaced, then this constitutes a human rights
41
violation (Castro et al. 2016; Piggot et al. 2019).

Ambitious climate goals can increase the urgency of mitigation and accelerate the speed at which carbon
44
neutrality is achieved. However, if the transition is done with speed, then this will leave diversification
45
efforts stymied, particularly in developing countries that are highly dependent on fossil-fuel revenue
46
streams (UNEP/SEI 2020). Transition decisions and policies may also have far-reaching gendered
47
implications, as the closure of mines is often linked to several ancillary businesses impacts where men
48
are laid off and women may have to take on multiple jobs to compensate for the reduction in the
49
household‘s income (Piggot et al. 2019; UNU-INRA 2020).
