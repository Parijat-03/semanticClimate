Electrification is expected to be the dominant strategy in buildings as electricity is increasingly used for 1 heating and for cooking. Electricity will help to integrate renewable energy into buildings and will also 2 lead to more flexible demand for heating, cooling, and electricity. District heating and cooling offers 3 potential for demand flexibility through energy storage and supply flexibility through cogeneration. 4 Heat pumps are increasingly used in buildings and industry for heating and cooling {9.3.3, Box 9.3}. 5 The ease of switching to electricity means that hydrogen is not expected to be a dominant pathway for 6 buildings {Box 9.6}. Using electricity directly for heating, cooling and other building energy demand 7 is more efficient than using hydrogen as a fuel, for example, in boilers or fuel cells. In addition, 8 electricity distribution is already well developed in many regions compared to essentially non-existent 9 hydrogen infrastructure, except for a few chemicals industry pipelines. At the same time, hydrogen 10 could potentially be used for on-site storage should technology advance sufficiently. 11
Electrification is already occurring in several modes of personal and light freight transport, and vehicle-12 to-grid solutions for flexibility have been extensively explored in the literature and small-scale pilots. 13 The role of hydrogen in transport depends on how far technology develops. Batteries are currently a 14 more attractive option than hydrogen and fuel-cells for light-duty vehicles. Hydrogen and hydrogen-15 derived synthetic fuels, such as ammonia and methanol, may have a more important role in heavy 16 vehicles, shipping, and aviation {10.3}. Current transport of fossil fuels may be replaced by future 17 transport of hydrogen and hydrogen carriers such as ammonia and methanol, or energy intensive basic 18 materials processed with hydrogen (e.g. reduced iron) in regions with bountiful renewable resources. 19 {Box 11.1} 20
Both light and heavy industry are potentially large and flexible users of electricity for both final energy 21 use (e.g., directly and using heat pumps in light industry) and for feedstocks (e.g., hydrogen for steel 22 making and chemicals). For example, industrial process heat demand, ranging from below 100°C to 23 above 1000 °C, can be met through a wide range of electrically powered technologies instead of using 24 fuels. Future demand for hydrogen (e.g., for nitrogen fertiliser or as reduction agent in steel production) 25 also offers electricity demand flexibility for electrolysis through hydrogen storage and flexible 26 production cycles {11.3.5}. The main use of hydrogen and hydrogen carriers in industry is expected to 27 be as feedstock (e.g., for ammonia and organic chemicals) rather than for energy as industrial 28 electrification increases. 29
