TS. 7 Mitigation in the context of sustainable development 2
Accelerating climate mitigation in the context of sustainable development involves not only expediting 3 the pace of change but also addressing the underlying drivers of vulnerability and emissions. 4 Addressing these drivers can enable diverse communities, sectors, stakeholders, regions and cultures to 5 participate in just, equitable and inclusive processes that improve the health and well-being of people 6 and the planet. Looking at climate change from a justice perspective also means placing the emphasis 7 on: i) the protection of vulnerable populations from the impacts of climate change, ii) mitigating 8 the effects of low-carbon transformations, and iii) ensuring an equitable decarbonised world (high 9 confidence). {17.1} 10
The SDG framework25can serve as a template to evaluate the long-term implications of mitigation 11 on sustainable development and vice versa (high confidence). Understanding the co-benefits and 12 trade-offs associated with mitigation is key to understanding how societies prioritise among the 13 various sectoral policy options (medium confidence). Areas with anticipated trade-offs include food 14 and biodiversity, energy affordability/access, and mineral resource extraction. Areas with anticipated 15 co-benefits include health, especially regarding air pollution, clean energy access and water availability. 16 The possible implementation of the different sectoral mitigation options therefore depends on 17 how societies prioritise mitigation versus other products and services: not least, how societies prioritise 18 food, material well-being, nature conservation and biodiversity protection, as well as considerations 19 such as their future dependence on CDR. The beneficial and adverse impacts of deploying climate-change mitigation and adaptation 23 responses are highly context-specific and scale-dependent. There are synergies and trade-offs 24 between adaptation and mitigation as well as synergies and trade-offs with sustainable 25 development (high confidence). Strong links also exists between sustainable development, 26 vulnerability and climate risks, as limited economic, social and institutional resources often result in 27 low adaptive capacities and high vulnerability, especially in developing countries. Resource limitations 28 in these countries can similarly weaken the capacity for climate mitigation and adaptation. The move 29 towards climate-resilient societies requires transformational or deep systemic change. This has 30 important implications for countries’ sustainable development pathways (medium evidence, high 31 agreement). (Box TS.3, Figure TS.29) {4.5, Figure 4.9, 17.3.3} 32
