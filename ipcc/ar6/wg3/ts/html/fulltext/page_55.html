TS. 5 Mitigation responses in sectors and systems 1
Chapters 5-12 assess recent advances in knowledge in individual sectors and systems. These chapters 2 – Energy (Chapter 6), Urban and other settlements (Chapter 8), Transport (Chapter 10), Buildings 3 Chapter 9), Industry (Chapter 11), and Agriculture, forestry and other land use (AFOLU) (Chapter 7) – 4 correspond broadly to the IPCC National Greenhouse Gas Inventory reporting categories and build on 5 similar chapters in previous WG III reports. Chapters 5 and 12 tie together the cross-sectoral aspects of 6 this group of chapters including the assessment of costs and potentials, demand side aspects of 7 mitigation, and carbon dioxide removal (CDR). 8
TS. 5.1 Energy 9
A broad-based approach to deploying energy sector mitigation options can reduce emissions over 10 the next ten years and set the stage for still deeper reductions beyond 2030 (high confidence). 11 There are substantial, cost-effective opportunities to reduce emissions rapidly, including in electricity 12 generation, but near-term reductions will not be sufficient to likely limit warming to 2°C or limit 13 warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot. {6.4, 6.6, 6.7} 14
Warming cannot be limited to 2°C or 1.5°C without rapid and deep reductions in energy system 15 CO2 and GHG emissions (high confidence). In scenarios likely limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or 16 limited overshoot (likely below 2°C), net energy system CO2 emissions (interquartile range) fall by 87% 17 to 97%% (60% to 79%) in 2050. In 2030, in scenarios limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited 18 overshoot, net CO2 and GHG emissions fall by 35-51% and 38-52% respectively. In scenarios limiting 19 warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot (likely below 2°C), net electricity sector CO2 emissions 20 reach zero globally between 2045 and 2055 (2050 and 2080) (high confidence). {6.7} 21
Limiting warming to 2°C or 1.5°C will require substantial energy system changes over the next 22 30 years. This includes reduced fossil fuel consumption, increased production from low- and zero-23 carbon energy sources, and increased use of electricity and alternative energy carriers (high 24 confidence). Coal consumption without CCS falls by 67% to 82% (interquartile range) in 2030 in 25 scenarios limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot. Oil and gas consumption fall more 26 slowly. Low-carbon sources produce 93% to 97% of global electricity by 2050 in scenarios that likely 27 limit warming to 2°C or below. In scenarios limiting warming to 1.5°C with no or limited overshoot 28 (likely below 2°C), electricity supplies 48% to 58% (36% to 47%) of final energy in 2050, up from 20% 29 in 2019. {6.7} 30
Net zero energy systems will share common characteristics, but the approach in every country 31 will depend on national circumstances (high confidence). Common characteristics of net-zero energy 32 systems will include: (1) electricity systems that produce no net CO2 or remove CO2 from the 33 atmosphere; (2) widespread electrification of end uses, including light-duty transport, space heating, 34 and cooking; (3) substantially lower use of fossil fuels than today; (4) use of alternative energy carriers 35 such as hydrogen, bioenergy, and ammonia to substitute for fossil fuels in sectors less amenable to 36 electrification; (5) more efficient use of energy than today; (6) greater energy system integration across 37 regions and across components of the energy system; and (7) use of CO2 removal including DACCS 38 and BECCS to offset residual emissions. {6.6} 39
Energy demands and energy sector emissions have continued to rise (high confidence). From 2015 40 to 2019, global final energy consumption grew by 6.6%, CO2 emissions from the global energy system 41 grew by 4.6%, and total GHG emissions from energy supply rose by 2.7%. Fugitive CH4 emissions 42 from oil, gas, and coal, accounted for 5.8% of GHG emissions in 2019. Coal electricity capacity grew 43 by 7.6% between 2015 and 2019, as new builds in some countries offset declines in others. Total 44 consumption of oil and oil products increased by 5%, and natural gas consumption grew by 15%. 45
