The impacts of climate-change mitigation and adaptation responses, are highly context-specific
12
and scale-dependent. There are synergies and trade-offs between adaptation and mitigation as
13
well as synergies and trade-offs with sustainable development(robust evidence, high agreement).

A strong link exists between sustainable development, vulnerability and climate risks, as limited
15
economic, social and institutional resources often result in low adaptive capacities and high
16
vulnerability, especially in developing countries. Resource limitations in these countries can similarly
17
weaken the capacity for climate mitigation and adaptation. The move towards climate-resilient societies
18
requires transformational or deep systemic change. This has important implications countries’
19
sustainable development pathways (medium evidence, high agreement) {17.3.3.6}.

Sectoral mitigation options present synergies with the SDGs, but there are also trade-offs, which
22
can become barriers to implementation. Such trade-offs are particularly identified in relation to
23
the use of land for bioenergy crops, water and food access, and competition for land between
24
forest or food production (robust evidence, high agreement). Many industrial mitigation options, like
25
efficiency improvements, waste management and the circular economy, have synergies with the SDGs
26
relating to access to food, water and energy (robust evidence, high agreement). The promotion of
27
renewable energy in some industrial sectors, can imply stranded energy supply investments, which need
28
to be taken into consideration (medium evidence, medium agreement). The Agriculture, Forestry, and
29
Other Land Uses (AFOLU) sector offers many low-cost mitigation options, but actions aimed at
30
producing bioenergy, extending food access and protecting biodiversity can also create trade-offs
31
between different land-uses (robust evidence, high agreement). Some options can help to minimize
32
these trade-offs, for example, integrated land management, cross-sectoral policies and efficiency
33
improvements. Lifestyle changes, including dietary changes and reduced food waste, have several
34
synergies with climate mitigation and the SDGs (medium evidence, medium agreement). Cross-sectoral
35
policies are important in avoiding trade-offs, to ensure that synergies between mitigation and SDGs are
36
captured, and to ensure local people are involved in the development of new products, as well as
37
production and consumption practices. There can be many synergies in urban areas between mitigation
38
policies and the SDGs, but capturing these depends on the overall planning of urban structures and on
39
local integrated policies, where, for example, affordable housing and spatial planning as a climate
40
mitigation measure are combined with walkable urban areas, green electrification and clean renewable
41
energy. Such integrated options can also reduce the pressures on agricultural land by reducing urban
42
growth, thus improving food security. Access to green electricity can also support quality education
43
(medium evidence, medium agreement). {17.3.3, 17.3.3.1, 17.3.3.3}.
