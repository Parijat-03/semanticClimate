TS. 5.2 Urban and other settlements 1
Although urbanisation is a global trend often associated with increased incomes and higher 2 consumption, the growing concentration of people and activities is an opportunity to increase 3 resource efficiency and decarbonise at scale (very high confidence). The same urbanisation level can 4 have large variations in per capita urban carbon emissions. For most regions, per capita urban emissions 5 are lower than per capita national emissions (very high confidence). {8.1.4, 8.3.3, 8.4, Box 8.1} 6
Most future urban population growth will occur in developing countries, where per capita 7 emissions are currently low but are expected to increase with the construction and use of new 8 infrastructure and the built environment, and changes in incomes and lifestyles (very high 9 confidence). The drivers of urban GHG emissions are complex and include an interplay of population 10 size, income, state of urbanisation, and how cities are laid out. How new cities and towns are designed, 11 constructed, managed, and powered will lock-in behaviour, lifestyles, and future urban GHG emissions. 12 Urban strategies can improve well-being while minimising impact on GHG emissions. However, 13 urbanisation can result in increased global GHG emissions through emissions outside the city’s 14 boundaries (very high confidence). {8.1.4, 8.3, Box 8.1, 8.4, 8.6} 15
The urban share of combined global (CO2 and CH4 emissions is substantial and continues to 16 increase (high confidence). Urban areas generated between 67–72% (~28 GtCO2-eq) of combined 17 global CO2 and CH4 emissions in 2020 through the production and consumption of goods and services. 18 These emissions are projected to rise to 34–65 GtCO2-eq by 2050 with moderate to no mitigation efforts, 19 driven by a growing population, infrastructure, and service demands in urban areas. About 100 of the 20 highest emitting urban areas account for approximately 18% of the global carbon footprint (high 21 confidence). {8.1.6, 8.3.3} 22
The urban share of regional GHG emissions increased between 2000 and 2015, with much inter-23 regional variation in the magnitude of the increase (high confidence). Globally, the urban share of 24 national emissions increased six percentage points, from 56% in 2000 to 62% in 2015. For 2000 to 25 2015, the urban emissions share increased from 28% to 38% in Africa, from 46% to 54% in Asia and 26 Developing Pacific, from 62% to 72% in Developed Countries, from 57% to 62% in Eastern Europe 27 and West-Central Asia, from 55% to 66% in Latin America and Caribbean, and from 68% to 69% in 28 the Middle East (high confidence). {8.1.6, 8.3.3} 29
Per capita urban GHG emissions increased between 2000 and 2015, with cities in developed 30 countries accounting for nearly seven times more per capita than the lowest emitting region 31 (medium confidence). From 2000 to 2015, global urban GHG emissions per capita increased from 5.5 32 to 6.2 tCO2-eq per person (an increase of 11.8%). Emissions in Africa increased from 1.3 to 1.5 tCO2-33 eq per person (22.6%); in Asia and Developing Pacific from 3.0 to 5.1 tCO2-eq per person (71.7%); in 34 Eastern Europe and West-Central Asia from 6.9 to 9.8 tCO2-eq per person (40.9%); in Latin America 35 and the Caribbean from 2.7 to 3.7 tCO2-eq per person (40.4%); and in the Middle East from 7.4 to 9.6 36 tCO2-eq per person (30.1%).
