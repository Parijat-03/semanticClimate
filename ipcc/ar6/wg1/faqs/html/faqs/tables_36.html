2 FAQ 6.1 | What Are Short-lived Climate Forcers and How Do They Affect the Climate?
3
4 Short-lived climate forcers (SLCFs) are compounds such as methane and sulphate aerosols that warm or cool the\nEarth’s climate over shorter time scales – from days to years – than greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, whose\nclimatic effect lasts for decades, centuries or more. Because SLCFs do not remain in the atmosphere for very\nlong, their effects on the climate are different from one region to another and can change rapidly in response\nto changes in SLCF emissions. As some SLCFs also negatively affect air quality, measures to improve air quality\nhave resulted in sharp reductions in emissions and concentrations of those SLCFs in many regions over the few
5 last decades.\nThe SLCFs include gases as well as tiny particles called aerosols, and they can have a warming or cooling effect
6 on the climate (FAQ 6.1, Figure 1). Warming SLCFs are either greenhouse gases (e.g., ozone or methane) or\nparticles like black carbon (also known as soot), which warm the climate by absorbing energy and are sometimes\nreferred to as short-lived climate pollutants. Cooling SLCFs, on the other hand, are mostly made of aerosol\nparticles (e.g., sulphate, nitrate and organic aerosols) that cool down the climate by reflecting away more
7 incoming sunlight.\nSome SLCFs do not directly affect the climate but produce climate-active compounds and are referred to as
8 precursors. SLCFs are emitted both naturally and as a result of human activities, such as agriculture or extraction\nof fossil fuels. Many of the human sources, particularly those involving combustion, produce SLCFs at the\nsame time as carbon dioxide and other long-lived greenhouse gases. Emissions have increased since the start\nof industrialization, and humans are now the dominant source for several SLCFs and SLCF precursors, such as\nsulphur dioxide (which produces sulphate aerosols) and nitrogen oxides (which produce nitrate aerosols and
9 ozone), despite strong reductions over the last few decades in some regions due to efforts to improve air quality.\nThe climatic effect of a chemical compound in the atmosphere depends on two things: (i) how effective it
10 is at cooling or warming the climate (its radiative efficiency) and (ii) how long it remains in the atmosphere\n(its lifetime). Because they have high radiative efficiencies, SLCFs can have a strong effect on the climate even\nthough they have relatively short lifetimes of up to about two decades after emission. Today, there is a balance
11 between warming and cooling from SLCFs, but this can change in the future.\nThe short lifetime of SLCFs constrains their effects in both space and time. First, of all the SLCFs, methane and
12 the short-lived halocarbons persist the longest in the atmosphere: up to two decades (FAQ 6.1, Figure 1). This is\nlong enough to mix in the atmosphere and to spread globally. Most other SLCFs only remain in the atmosphere\nfor a few days to weeks, which is generally too short for mixing in the atmosphere, sometimes even regionally.\nAs a result, the SLCFs are unevenly distributed and their effects on the climate are more regional than those of
13 longer-lived gases. Second, rapid (but sustained) changes in emissions of SLCFs can result in rapid climatic effects.\nIn addition to the direct warming and cooling effects, SLCFs have many other consequences for the climate system
14 and for air quality (see FAQ 6.2). For instance, deposition of black carbon on snow darkens its surface, which\nsubsequently absorbs more solar energy, leading to more melting and more warming. Aerosols also modify the\nproperties of clouds, which has indirect cooling effects on the climate and causes changes in local rainfall (see\nFAQ 7.2). Climate models indicate that SLCFs have altered atmospheric circulation on local and even hemispheric\nscales (e.g., monsoons) as well as regional precipitation. For instance, recent observations show that regional\nweather is influenced by strong regional contrasts in the evolution of aerosol concentrations, particularly over
15 South and East Asia.\nAlthough policies to limit climate change and discussions of the so-called remaining carbon budgets primarily
16 focus on carbon dioxide (see FAQ 5.4), SLCFs can significantly affect temperature changes. It is therefore important\nto understand how SLCFs work and to quantify their effects. Because reducing some of the SLCF emissions, such as\nmethane, can simultaneously reduce warming effects and adverse effects on air quality as well as help attaining\nSustainable Development Goals, mitigation of SLCFs is often viewed as a favourable ‘win-win’ policy option.
