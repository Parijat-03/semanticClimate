The transition to a low-carbon development is wired in issues of justice and equity: how do you align
12
carbon reductions to meet the needs of humanity? Distributive justice calls for a fairer sharing of the
13
benefits and burdens of the transition process, while procedural justice is essentially about ensuring that
14
the demands of vulnerable groups are not ignored in the pull to the transition. The impacts of climate
15
change and the mitigation burdens are experienced differently by different social actors, with
16
indigenous communities facing multiple threats and being subjected to unequal power dynamics
17
(Sovacool 2021).
18
19
Nonetheless, the production of fossil fuels is central to many economies with numerous development
20
implications related to rents associated with export revenues, energy security and poverty alleviation
21
(Lazarus and van Asselt 2018). The central question is who decides which types of carbon should be
22
burnable or non-burnable. Hence, social equality is at the heart of the transition process, but it falls short
23
of a response on how to chart a new road map towards carbon neutrality, especially given that fossil-
24
fuel producers and investors tend to belong to large, powerful companies and wield a great deal of
25
influence and power, especially when their entrenched interests are at stake (Lazarus and van Asselt
26
2018). The question of whether developing countries should be compensated for foregoing their
27
resources in light of their current development needs has not yielded many results and had only limited
28
success in mobilising international finance, as demonstrated by the case of Yasuni-ITT in Ecuador
29
(Sovacool and Scarpaci 2016). According to Sovacool et al. (2021), affected communities and their
30
views may be discounted and excluded from planning, which can neglect important matters such as
31
rights, recognition and representation (Sovacool 2021).
32
33
Fossil fuel-dependent countries are doubly exposed to the vulnerability related to climate-change
34
impacts and are being targeted in the global effort to address the problem (Peszko et al. 2020). Countries
35
that are heavily reliant on oil, coal and gas are also those most at risk from a low-carbon transition that
36
may curtail the activities of their fossil-fuel industries and render the value chains and economies
37
associated with the exploitation of fossil fuels unviable (Peszko et al. 2020).
38
39
Developing countries in Latin America and Africa that are reliant on revenue streams from fossil fuels
40
may not see these returns converted into much needed infrastructure and other social and economic
41
amenities that can reduce poverty. However, given the falling prices of renewables, developing
42
countries do not have to face the burden of retrofitting their infrastructure to align with new low-carbon
43
industries, since they can leapfrog technologies and shape a sustainable trajectory that is more resilient
44
and fit for the future.
45
46
However, the transition towards a carbon-neutral world is complex and non-linear, and it will likely
47
result in some disruptions, with manifest equality implications, given the scale of the transformation
48
envisaged. There are parallel movements that can be observed. On the one hand, divestment initiatives
49
are underway to move away from carbon-intensive investments. On the other hand, hydrocarbon-rich
50
countries in some parts of the developing world are identifying new opportunities to reduce the fiscal
51
loss associated with the loss of fossil-fuel revenues. Indeed, with global investment in energy expected
52
to shrink by 20% this year, this has created fiscal challenges for countries that are heavily reliant on
53
fossil-fuel products as their main source of revenue.
