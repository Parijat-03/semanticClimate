The energy sector is vulnerable to production losses caused mainly by heatwaves and droughts, whereas
1
coastal and fluvial floods are also responsible for a large relative share of the energy sector’s
2
vulnerability, as assessed by (Forzieri et al. 2018) for Europe in 2100. In total, heatwaves and droughts
3
will be responsible for 94% of the damage costs to the European energy system compared to 40% today.
4
Similarly, (Craig et al. 2018) show that, despite potentially minor spatiotemporally aggregated
5
differences for various energy-system components, such as demand, thermoelectric power, wind etc.,
6
the aggregated impact of climate change across these components will cause a significant impact on the
7
energy system, as currently exemplified by the USA. In terms of investments and management, it is
8
important to unravel these cross-component relations in light of the projected nature of the future
9

In the ongoing transition towards renewable sources of energy (see also Chapters 3, 4 and 6), the impact
11
of the hydro-climate on energy production continues to be highly relevant (Jones and Warner 2016). As
12
the shares of thermoelectric energy production in the energy grid go down along with the introduction
13
of thermoelectric cooling technologies using smaller amounts of water, new energy sources and
14
technologies are being introduced, and existing sources scaled up. Of these, hydropower, wind and solar
15
energy are the key energy sources currently and will be in the near future, making up 2.5% and 1.8%
16
of the total global primary energy supply in 2017 respectively (IEA 2019). Wind and solar energy are
17
directly independent of water in themselves, but are dependent on atmospheric conditions related to
18
processes that also drive the water balance and circulation. Hydropower, on the other hand, is directly
19
influenced by and dependent on the supply of water, while at the same time being an essential counter-
20
component to seasonality and climatological variation, as well as to current and future demand curves
21
and diurnal variations, as against wind and solar energy (De Barbosa et al. 2017).
22
23
Furthermore, policy instruments in power-system management, here exemplified by hydropower in a
24
climate-change scenario, have been shown to enhance energy production during droughts (Gjorgiev and
25
Sansavini 2018). The significant influence of variation in the planning of renewable energy for the 21
st
26
century has also been highlighted by (Bloomfield et al. 2016). At the same time, the integration of
27
renewables must account for lower thermoelectric efficiencies and capacities due to increases in
28
temperature (van Vliet et al. 2016), power-plant closures during extreme weather events due to a lack
29
of cooling capacity (Forzieri et al. 2018) and further efficiency reductions and penalties following the
30
implementation of CCS technologies in the effort to reach the GHG mitigation targets (Byers et al.
31
2015). However, more recent studies find more promising amounts of water being used for energy
32
conversion (IEAGHG 2020; Magneschi et al. 2017).

The extraction, distribution and wastewater processes of anthropogenic water-management systems
35
similarly use vast amounts of energy, making the proper management of water essential to reduce
36
energy usage and GHG emissions (Nair et al. 2014 and Chapter 11). One study reports that the water
37
sector accounts for 5% of total US GHG emissions (Rothausen and Conway 2011). Within the WEFN,
38
there is an obvious trade-off between water availability and food production, competing demands that
39
pose a risk to the supply of the basic commodities of food, energy and water in line with the SDGs
40
(Bleischwitz et al. 2018; Gao et al. 2019), all of which has the potential for inter-sectorial or inter-
41
regional conflicts (Froese and Schilling 2019). Currently, 24% of the global population live in regions
42
with constant water-scarce food production, and 19% experience occasional water scarcities (Kummu
43
et al. 2014). To counterbalance the demand for food and comestibles in regions that experience constant
44
or intermittent supplies, transportation is needed, which in itself requires suitable infrastructure, energy
45
supplies, a well-functioning trading environment and support policies. Of the 2.6 billion people who
46
experience constant or occasional water scarcities in food production, 55% rely on international trade,
47
21% on domestic trade and the remainder on water stocks (Kummu et al. 2014).

The relations between the influence of hydro-climatic variability, socio-economic conditions and
50
patterns of water scarcity have been addressed by (Veldkamp et al. 2015). A key finding of this study
51
was the ability of the hydro-climate and the socio-economy to interact, enforcing or attenuating each
52
other, though with the former acting as the key immediate driver, and the influence of the latter
53
emerging after six to ten years.
