There is a need to explore how accelerated mitigation – relative to NDCs and current policies – 12 could close both emission gaps, and implementation gaps. There is increasing understanding of the 13 technical content of accelerated mitigation pathways, differentiated by national circumstances, with 14 considerable, though uneven, literature at country-level (medium evidence, high agreement). 15 Transformative technological and institutional changes for the near-term include demand reductions 16 through efficiency and reduced activity, rapid decarbonisation of the electricity sector and low-carbon 17 electrification of buildings, industry and transport (robust evidence, medium agreement). A focus on 18 energy use and supply is essential, but not sufficient on its own – the land sector and food systems 19 deserve attention. The literature does not adequately include demand-side options and systems analysis, 20 and captures the impact from non-CO2 GHGs with medium confidence. {4.2.5} 21
If obstacles to accelerated mitigation are rooted in underlying structural features of society, then 22 transforming such structures can support emission reductions {4.2.6}. Countries and regions will 23 have different starting points for transition pathways. Some critical differences between countries 24 include climate conditions resulting in different heating and cooling needs, endowments with different 25 energy resources, patterns of spatial development, and political and economic conditions {4.2.5}. The 26 way countries develop determines their capacity to accelerate mitigation and achieve other sustainable 27 development objectives simultaneously (medium confidence) {4.3.1, 4.3.2}. Yet meeting ambitious 28 mitigation and development goals cannot be achieved through incremental change (robust evidence, 29 medium agreement). Though development pathways result from the actions of a wide range of actors, 30 it is possible to shift development pathways through policies and enhancing enabling conditions (limited 31 evidence, medium agreement). 32
Shifting development pathways towards sustainability offers ways to broaden the range of levers 33 and enablers that a society can use to accelerate mitigation and increases the likelihood of making 34 progress simultaneously on climate action and other development goals (Box TS.3) {Cross-35 Chapter Box 5 in Chapter 4, Figure 4.7, 4.3}. There are practical options to shift development 36 pathways in ways that advance mitigation and other sustainable development objectives, supporting 37 political feasibility, increase resources to meet multiple goals, and reduce emissions (limited evidence, 38 high agreement). Concrete examples, assessed in chapter 4 of this report, include high employment and 39 low emissions structural change, fiscal reforms for mitigation and social contract, combining housing 40 policies to deliver both housing and transport mitigation, and change economic, social and spatial 41 patterns of development of the agriculture sector provide the basis for sustained reductions in emissions 42 from deforestation. {4.4.1, 4.4, 1.10} 43
