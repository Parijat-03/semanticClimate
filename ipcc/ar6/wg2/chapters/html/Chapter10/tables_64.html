0 Box 10.6 | Loss and Damage Across Asia: Mapping the Evidence and Knowledge Gaps
1
2 Losses and damages are climate impacts after implementing adaptation and mitigation actions, signifying the presence of residual risks\n(Chapter 1; Kugler and Sariego, 2016; Mechler et al., 2019). These residual risks indicate that despite adaptation, there are soft and\nhard adaptation limits (Mechler et al., 2019). This box reviews the adaptation literature across 51 countries in Asia on loss and damage\n(L&D), and adaptation barriers and limits, and identifies knowledge and regional gaps. The key messages are that (a) climate-induced\nL&D is already occurring across Asia (medium evidence, high agreement), (b) these L&D are very likely to increase at higher warming\nlevels (medium evidence, high agreement) and (c) measuring and attributing non-economic and intangible L&D remains a challenge (low\nevidence, high agreement).
3 Findings on losses and damages in Asia: Evidence on climate-related L&D highlights tangible or material losses and damages such\nas loss to life, property, infrastructure and livelihoods (medium evidence, high agreement); and intangible or non-material losses and\ndamages such as increasing conflict and civil unrest, erosion of sociocultural practices and decreased well-being (low evidence, high\nagreement). The main constraint in assessing past and future L&D is that this terminology is not used prominently or consistently in the\ndisaster management and climate risk literature in Asia, which potentially leads to under-reporting. In contrast, there is robust evidence\n(high agreement) on adaptation constraints, notably on governance, informational and physical constraints, to adapting, but regional\nevidence is very uneven with gaps in Central, North and West Asia. 4 The knowledge gaps are as follows:
5
6 • Attribution studies linking anthropogenic climate change and L&D remain focused on rapid-onset extreme events, and evidence on\nL&D from slow-onset events, such as drought and water scarcity, is low (Pereira et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2021a).
7 • Regional evidence gaps in Central, North and West Asia; and low evidence of national-level projected L&D (Uchiyama et al., 2020;
8 Singh et al., 2021a).
9 • Disproportionate emphasis on economic L&D while intangible, non-economic L&D are relatively less measured and reported (Chiba
10 et al., 2017; Bahinipati, 2020). Economic loss estimates are largely approximations and therefore suffer from various methodological,\nassumption and data-related uncertainties.
11 • Insufficient literature differentiating L&D under future adaptation scenarios, which makes assessment of residual damages and future
12 L&D difficult. The L&D projections are constrained by limited understanding on how vulnerabilities will evolve with economic and\ndemographic changes. Most projected L&D are based on the population and GDP projections. More future projections are based on\nthe RCP scenarios, and the least number of studies were conducted on the combination of RCP and SSPs.
13 • Mitigation will have L&D and adaptation co-benefits (Kugler and Sariego, 2016; Toussaint, 2020), especially at the lower temperature
14 stabilisation 1.5°C (Nishiura et al., 2020), but the literature is currently insufficient to assess these L&D co-benefits of mitigation\nefforts.
15 • Negligible regional evidence on limits to adaptation.
16
17 Way forward: Developing robust metrics and institutions for measuring and reporting L&D at national and regional scales, especially\nnon-economic damages and L&D due to slow-onset events, is critical. In addition to vulnerability assessments, assessing L&D and limits\nto adaptation can inform adaptation prioritisation and enhance adaptation effectiveness (e.g., Craft and Fisher, 2016; Leiter et al., 2019).\nLessons are available from biodiversity and ecosystem services monitoring frameworks that have well-developed metrics and processes\n(e.g., Díaz et al., 2020).
