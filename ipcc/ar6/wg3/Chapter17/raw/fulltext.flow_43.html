There are several examples of cities that have developed plans for meeting both the SDGs and
15
mitigation, which demonstrates the feasibility of meeting these objectives jointly. Quito, Ecuador, a city
16
with large carbon footprints (Global Opportunity Explorer 2019) and climate vulnerabilities, has
17
adopted low-carbon plans that aim to achieve the climate goals while introducing net zero energy
18
buildings and reducing water stress (Ordoñez et al. 2019; Marcotullio et al. 2018). Several cities in
19
China, Indonesia and Japan have invested in green city initiatives by means of green infrastructural
20
investments, which is claimed to be a form of smart investment. Through this type of investment,
21
economic growth and greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reductions can be achieved in cities (Jupesta et
22
al. 2016). Multi-level governance arrangements, public-private cooperation and robust urban-data
23
platforms are among the factors enabling the pursuit of these objectives within countries (Corfee-Morlot
24
et al. 2009; Gordon 2015; Creutzig et al. 2019; Yarime 2017).

37

38

39
2030 Sustainable Development Agenda, including stand-alone SDG 11, “make cities and human
40
settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable” (United Nations 2015 p. 14). The following year,
41
170 countries agreed to the UN New Urban Agenda (NUA), a central part of which is recognizing the
42

43

44

45
