Policies can shift development pathways. There are examples of policies implemented in the 7 pursuit of overall societal development objectives, such as job creation, macro-economic stability, 8 economic growth, and public health and welfare. In some countries, such policies are framed as part 9 of a just transition (Box TS.3), however, they can have major influence on mitigative capacity, and 10 hence can be seen as tools to broaden mitigation options (medium confidence) {4.3.3}. Coordinated 11 policy mixes would need to orchestrate multiple actors – individuals, groups and collectives, corporate 12 actors, institutions and infrastructure actors – to deepen decarbonisation and shift pathways towards 13 sustainability. Shifts in one country may spill over to other countries. Shifting development pathways 14 can jointly support mitigation and adaptation {4.4.2}. Some studies explore the risks of high complexity 15 and potential delay attached to shifting development pathways. (Box TS.4, Figure TS.11) {4.4.3} 16
An increasing number of mitigation strategies up to 2050 (mid-term) have been developed by 17 various actors. A growing number of such strategies aim at net zero GHG or CO2 emissions, but 18 it is not yet possible to draw global implications due to the limited size of sample (medium 19 evidence; low agreement) {4.2.4}. Non-state actors are also engaging in a wide range of mitigation 20 initiatives. When adding up emission reduction potentials, sub-national and non-state international 21 cooperative initiatives could reduce emissions by up to about 20 GtCO2-eq in 2030 (limited evidence, 22 medium agreement) {4.2.3}. Yet perceived or real conflicts between mitigation and other SDGs can 23 impede such action.
