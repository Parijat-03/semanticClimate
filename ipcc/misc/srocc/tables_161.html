4 GHG emissions from fires
5 Emissions from wildfires and biomass burning are a significant source of GHGs (CO , CH , N O), carbon monoxide (CO), carbonaceous\n2 4 2
6 aerosols, and an array of other gases including non-methane volatile organic compounds (NMVOC) (Akagi et al. 2011; Van Der Werf\net al. 2010). GFED4s has updated fire-related carbon emission estimates biome-wise (regionally and globally), using higher resolution\ninput data gridded at 0.25˚, a new burned area dataset with small fires, improved fire emission factors (Akagi et al. 2011; Urbanski\n2014) and better fire severity characterisation of boreal forests (van der Werf et al. 2017). The estimates for the period 1997–2016 are\n2.2 GtC yr–1, being highest in the 1997 El Nino (3.0 GtC yr–1) and lowest in 2013 (1.8 GtC yr–1). Furthermore, fire emissions during\n1997–2016 were dominated by savanna (65.3%), followed by tropical forest (15.1%), boreal forest (7.4%), temperate forest (2.3%),\npeatland (3.7%) and agricultural waste burning (6.3%) (van der Werf et al. 2017).
7 Fires not only transfer carbon from land to the atmosphere but also between different terrestrial pools: from live to dead biomass\nto soil, including partially charred biomass, charcoal and soot constituting 0.12–0.39 GtC yr–1 or 0.2–0.6% of annual terrestrial NPP\n(Doerr and Santín 2016). Carbon from the atmosphere is sequestered back into regrowing vegetation at rates specific to the type of\nvegetation and other environmental variables (Loehman et al. 2014). Fire emissions are thus not necessarily a net source of carbon\ninto the atmosphere, as post-fire recovery of vegetation can sequester a roughly equivalent amount back into biomass over a time\nperiod of one to a few years (in grasslands and agricultural lands) to decades (in forests) (Landry and Matthews 2016). Fires from\ndeforestation (for land use change) and on peatlands (which store more carbon than terrestrial vegetation) obviously are a net\nsource of carbon from the land to the atmosphere (Turetsky et al. 2014); these types of fires were estimated to emit 0.4 GtC yr–1\nin recent decades (van der Werf et al. 2017). Peatland fires dominated by smouldering combustion under low temperatures and\nhigh moisture conditions can burn for long periods (Turetsky et al. 2014).
8 Fires, land degradation/desertification and land-atmosphere exchanges
9 Flammable ecosystems are generally adapted to their specific fire regimes (Bond et al. 2005). A fire regime shift alters vegetation
10 and soil properties in complex ways, both in the short- and the long-term, with consequences for carbon stock changes, albedo,\nfire-atmosphere-vegetation feedbacks and the ultimate biological capacity of the burnt land (Bond et al. 2004; Bremer and Ham 1999;\nMacDermott et al. 2016; Tepley et al. 2018; Moody et al. 2013; Veraverbeke et al. 2012) A fire-driven shift in vegetation from a forested\nstate to an alternative stable state such as a grassland (Fletcher et al. 2014; Moritz 2015) with much less carbon stock is a distinct\npossibility. Fires cause soil erosion through action of wind and water (Moody et al. 2013) thus resulting in land degradation (Chapter 4)\nand eventually desertification (Chapter 3). Fires also affect carbon exchange between land and atmosphere through ozone (which\nretards photosynthesis) and aerosol (which slightly increases diffuse radiation) emissions. The net effect from fire on global GPP during\n2002–2011 is estimated to be –0.86 ± 0.74 GtC yr–1 (Yue and Unger 2018).
11 Fires under future climate change
12 Temperature increase and precipitation decline would be the major driver of fire regimes under future climates as evapotranspiration
13 increases and soil moisture decreases (Pechony and Shindell 2010; Aldersley et al. 2011; Abatzoglou and Williams 2016; Fernandes et al.\n2017). The risk of wildfires in future could be expected to change, increasing significantly in North America, South America, central Asia,\nsouthern Europe, southern Africa and Australia (Liu et al. 2010). There is emerging evidence that recent regional surges in wildland\nfires are being driven by changing weather extremes, thereby signalling geographical shifts in fire proneness (Jolly et al. 2015). Fire\nweather season has already lengthened by 18.7% globally between 1979 and 2013, with statistically significant increases across\n25.3% but decreases only across 10.7% of Earth’s land surface covered with vegetation. Even sharper changes have been observed\nduring the second half of this period (Jolly et al. 2015).
