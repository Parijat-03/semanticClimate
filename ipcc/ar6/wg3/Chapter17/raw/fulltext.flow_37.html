Of these thermoelectric sources, the vast majority employ substantial amounts of water for cooling

The renewable energy conversion technologies that are currently dominant globally and are projected
26
to remain so are less vulnerable to water deficiencies than fossil-based technologies, since no cooling
27
is used. These renewable energy conversion sources include, e.g., wind, solar PV and wave energy. The
28
implementation of such sources will, in the longer run, have the potential to reduce water usage by the
29
energy sector substantially (Lohrmann et al. 2019). Also, an increasing share of renewables within
30
desalination, as well as improved irrigation efficiencies, have been shown to potentially improve the
31

32
on connection with desalination, as well as improved irrigation efficiencies, have been shown to
33
potentially improve the inter-sectorial WEFN water balance (Caldera and Breyer 2020). Some less
34
dominant renewable-energy technologies do use water for cooling, such as geothermal energy and solar
35
CSP, if wet cooling is employed. Despite the general detachment from water resources, wind and solar
36
PV, for example, are highly dependent on climate-change patterns, including variability depending on
37

38

39

40
