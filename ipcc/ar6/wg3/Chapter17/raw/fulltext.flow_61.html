Equity in a just transition 12
Energy justice, although increasingly being emphasized (Pellegrini-Masini et al. 2020), has been under-
13
represented in the literature on sustainability and in debates on energy transitions, and it remains a
14
contested term with multiple meanings (Green and Gambhir 2020). Energy justice includes
15
affordability, sustainability, equality (accessibility for current and future households) and respect
16
(ensuring that innovations do not impose further burdens on particular groups) (Fuso Nerini et al. 2019).
17
Furthermore, it suggests that a just transition is a shared responsibility among countries that are making
18
more rapid progress towards net negative emissions and those economies that are focused on pressing
19
development priorities related to improved health, well-being and prosperity (van den Berg et al. 2020).
20
21
Looking at climate change from a justice perspective means placing the emphasis on a) the protection
22
of vulnerable populations from the impacts of climate change; b) mitigating the effects of the
23
transformations themselves, including easing the transition for those whose livelihoods currently rely
24
on fossil fuel-based sectors; and c) envisaging an equitable decarbonized world. Neglecting issues of
25
justice risks a backlash against climate action generally, particularly from those who stand to lose from
26
such actions (Patterson et al. 2018), and it will also have implications for the pace, scale and quality of
27
the transition. Explicit interventions to promote sustainability transitions that integrate local spaces into
28
the whole development process are necessary but not sufficient in creating a just transition (Breukers et
29
al. 2017; Ehnert et al. 2018b).
30
31
Renewable energy transitions in rural, impoverished locations can simultaneously reinforce and disrupt
32
local power structures and inequalities. Policy interventions to help the most impoverished individuals
33
in a community gain access to the new energy infrastructure are critical in ensuring that existing
34
inequalities are not reinforced. Individuals who are empowered by energy development projects can
35
influence the onward extension of sustainable energy to other communities (Ahlborg 2017). In Denmark
36
in the 1970s, for example, grassroots windmill cooperatives opened a pathway to the creation of one of
37
the world’s largest wind-energy markets. The unique dynamics of grassroots-led changes mean that
38
new technologies and low-carbon initiatives develop strong foundations by being designed, tested and
39
improved in the early stages with reference to the socio-political contexts in which they will grow later
40
(Ornetzeder and Rohracher 2013).

42
Intersectional theory can shine a light on the hidden costs of resource extraction, as well as renewable
43
energy development (see, for instance, (Chatalova and Balmann 2017), which go beyond environmental
44
or health risks to include the socio-cultural impacts on both communities adjacent to these sites and
45
those who work in them (Daum 2018). Indeed, development decisions often do not properly integrate
46
the burdens and risks placed on marginalized groups, like indigenous peoples, while risk assessments
47
tend to reinforce existing power imbalances by failing to differentiate between how benefits and risks
48
might impact on certain groups (Healy et al. 2019; Kojola 2019). In some cases, such as the deployment
49
of small-scale solar power in Tanzania by a non-profit organization, an explicit gender lens on the
50
impacts of energy poverty revealed the significant socio-economic benefits of improving access to
51
renewable energy (Gray et al. 2019).
