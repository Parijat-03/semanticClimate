The extent to which countries increase the 2 ambition of their NDCs and ensure they are effectively implemented will depend in part on the 3 successful implementation of the support mechanisms in the Paris Agreement, and in turn will 4 determine whether the goals of the Paris Agreement are met (high confidence). {14.2, 14.3, 14.4} 5
International cooperation outside the UNFCCC processes and agreements provides critical 6 support for mitigation in particular regions, sectors and industries, for particular types of 7 emissions, and at the sub- and trans-national levels (high confidence). Agreements addressing ozone 8 depletion, transboundary air pollution, and release of mercury are all leading to reductions in the 9 emissions of specific greenhouse gases. Cooperation is occurring at multiple governance levels 10 including cities. Transnational partnerships and alliances involving non-state and sub-national actors 11 are also playing a growing role in stimulating low-carbon technology diffusion and emissions 12 reductions (medium confidence). Such transnational efforts include those focused on climate litigation; 13 the impacts of these are unclear but promising. Climate change is being addressed in a growing number 14 of international agreements operating at sectoral levels, as well as within the practices of many 15 multilateral organisations and institutions. Sub-global and regional cooperation, often described as 16 climate clubs, can play an important role in accelerating mitigation, including the potential for reducing 17 mitigation costs through linking national carbon markets, although actual examples of these remain 18 limited. {14.2, 14.4, 14.5, 14.6} 19
International cooperation will need to be strengthened in several key respects in order to support 20 mitigation action consistent with limiting temperature rise to well below 2°C in the context of 21 sustainable development and equity (high confidence). Many developing countries’ NDCs have 22 components or additional actions that are conditional on receiving assistance with respect to finance, 23 technology development and transfer, and capacity building, greater than what has been provided to 24 date. Sectoral and sub-global cooperation is providing critical support, and yet there is room for further 25 progress. In some cases, notably with respect to aviation and shipping, sectoral agreements have 26 adopted climate mitigation goals that fall far short of what would be required to achieve the long-term 27 temperature goal of the Paris Agreement. Moreover, there are cases where international cooperation 28 may be hindering mitigation efforts, namely evidence that trade and investment agreements, as well as 29 agreements within the energy sector, impede national mitigation efforts (medium confidence). 30 International cooperation is emerging but so far fails to fully address transboundary issues associated 31 with solar radiation modification and carbon dioxide removal. {14.2, 14.3, 14.4, 14.5, 14.6, Cross-32 Working Group Box 4 in Chapter 14} 33

Social equity reinforces capacity and motivation for mitigating climate change (medium 36 confidence). Impartial governance such as fair treatment by law-and-order institutions, fair treatment 37 by gender, and income equity, increases social trust, thus enabling demand-side climate policies. High 38 status (often high carbon) item consumption may be reduced by taxing absolute wealth without 39 compromising well-being. {5.2, 5.4.2, 5.6} 40 41 Policies that increase the political access and participation of women, racialised, and marginalised 42 groups, increase the democratic impetus for climate action (high confidence).
