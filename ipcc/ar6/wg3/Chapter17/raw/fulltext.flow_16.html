17.2.5 Conclusions 24
This section has surveyed several explanations for interventions that can give rise to transitions. The
25
review suggests that there are several differences between these various perspectives. Whether
26
individuals, organisations, markets or sociotechnical systems drive or undermine transitions is a key
27
distinction. These differences have implications for the evidence these claims draw on in support of
28
their arguments. For instance, some of the explanations tend to employ qualitative evidence to explain
29
changes in attitudes at the individual or community levels as paving the way for broader changes to
30
cultures and belief systems. Others assess how institutional arrangements can be reformed in order to
31
align climate with the sustainable development agenda to enable a transition.
32
33
While there are indeed significant differences between explanations, there are also important parallels.
34
Such parallels begin with a shared emphasis on synergies and trade-offs between climate and
35
sustainable development. Most explanations tend to underline the importance of synergies in aligning
36
the climate with broader sustainability agendas. Most importantly, many of the explanations are
37
complementary with the systems-level discussion in that they offer a broad framework, while economic,
38
psychological and governance theories offer more specific insights. Moving a transition forward will
39
often require drawing upon insights from multiple schools of thought. Though is unlikely that a one-
40
size-fits-all set of factors will drive a transition, there is a growing body of empirical evidence shedding
41
light on the factors that can strengthen synergies between climate and the broader sustainable
42
development agenda.
