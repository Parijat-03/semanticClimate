17.1
Introduction
2
This chapter focuses on the opportunities and challenges for “accelerating the transition in the context
3
of sustainable development.” The chapter suggests that accelerating transitions in the context of
4
sustainable development requires more than concentrating on speed. Rather, it involves expediting the
5
pace of change (speed) while also removing the underlying drivers of vulnerability and high emissions
6
(quality and depth) and aligning the interests of different communities, regions, sectors, stakeholders
7
and cultures (scale and breadth). One key to enabling deep and broad transitions is integrating the views
8
of different government agencies, businesses and non-governmental organizations (NGOs) in transition
9
processes. Another critical driver of deep and broad transitions is engaging and empowering workers,
10
youth, women, the poor, minorities and marginalized stakeholders in just, equitable and inclusive
11
processes. The result of such processes will be the transformation of large-scale socioeconomic systems
12
to restore the health and well-being of the planet and the people on it.
13
Section 17.1 begins by reviewing how climate and sustainability issues have been discussed in the
14
Intergovernmental Process on Climate Change (IPCC), as well as international climate change and
15
sustainable development processes at different levels. It further introduces key themes addressed in the
16
chapter’s remaining subsections. Section 17.2 provides an overview of how key theories understand
17
transitions and transformation, and notes a shared concern over leveraging synergies and managing
18
trade-offs between climate change and sustainable development across different disciplines. Section
19
17.3 provides an assessment of the mitigation options that can help achieve these synergies and avoid
20
trade-offs. 17.4 pulls together the theoretical and empirical aspects by detailing the essential elements
21
of an enabling environment that helps drive forward transitions that are quick, deep, broad and,
22
ultimately, sustainable.

17.1.1
Integrating Climate Change and Sustainable Development in International 25
Assessments 26
Climate change not only poses a profound challenge to sustainable development, it is inexorably linked
27
to it. From the early stages of the IPCC assessment process, this challenge and the inherent link between
28
climate change and sustainable development have been well recognized. For example, the First
29
Assessment Report (FAR) highlighted the relevance of sustainable development for climate policy. The
30
Second Assessment Report (SAR) went further to include equity issues in its presentation of sustainable
31
development. The Third Assessment Report (TAR) (Banuri et al. 2001) made the link even stronger,
32
noting that "parties have a right to and should promote sustainable development" (as stated in the text
33
of the UNFCCC 2015 (Article 3.4)), and offering an early review of studies integrating sustainable
34
development and climate change. The Fourth Assessment Report (AR4) (Sathaye et al. 2007) added an
35
additional perspective to these interconnections, acknowledging the existence of a two-way relationship
36
between sustainable development and climate change.
37
38
The Fifth Assessment Report (AR5) (Denton et al. 2014; Fleurbaey et al. 2014) and the Special Report
39
on Global Warming of 1.5C (IPCC 2018; Roy et al. 2018a) have arguably made the strongest links
40
between climate and sustainable development to date. One of the key messages of AR5 was that the
41
implementation of climate mitigation and adaptation actions could help promote sustainable
42
development, and it emphasized the need for transformational changes in this regard. AR5 also
43
concluded that the link between climate change and sustainable development is cross-cutting and
44
complex, and that thus the impacts of climate change are threatening the efforts being made to achieve
45
sustainable development. The IPCC special report on Global Warming of 1.5C helped systematize
46
these links by mapping the synergies and trade-offs between selected SDG indicators and climate
47
mitigation (IPCC 2018; Roy et al. 2018b) (see also sect. 17.3).
