The assessment of 19 demand-19 side mitigation options and 18 different constituents of well-being showed that positive impacts on well-20 being outweigh negative ones by a factor of 11. {5.2, 5.2.3, Figure 5.6} 21
Demand-side mitigation options bring multiple interacting benefits (high confidence). Energy 22 services to meet human needs for nutrition, shelter, health, etc. are met in many different ways with 23 different emissions implications that depend on local contexts, cultures, geography, available 24 technologies, and social preferences. In the near term, many less-developed countries, and poor people 25 everywhere, require better access to safe and low-emissions energy sources to ensure decent living 26 standards and increase energy savings from service improvements by about 20-25%. Wealthy individuals contribute disproportionately to higher emissions and have a high potential 34 for emissions reductions while maintaining decent living standards and well-being (high 35 confidence). Individuals with high socio-economic status are capable of reducing their GHG emissions 36 by becoming role models of low-carbon lifestyles, investing in low-carbon businesses, and advocating 37 for stringent climate policies. {5.4.1, 5.4.3, 5.4.4, Figure 5.14} 38
Demand-side solutions require both motivation and capacity for change (high confidence). 39 Motivation by individuals or households worldwide to change energy consumption behaviour is 40 generally low. Individual behavioural change is insufficient for climate change mitigation unless 41 embedded in structural and cultural change. Different factors influence individual motivation and 42 capacity for change in different demographics and geographies. These factors go beyond traditional 43 socio-demographic and economic predictors and include psychological variables such as awareness, 44 perceived risk, subjective and social norms, values, and perceived behavioural control.
