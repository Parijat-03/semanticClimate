2 FAQ 8.1 | How Does Land Use Change Alter the Water Cycle?
3
4 The ways in which humans use and change land cover, for example by converting fields to urban areas or clearing\nforests, can affect every aspect of the water cycle. Land-use changes can alter precipitation patterns and how\nwater is absorbed into the ground, flows into streams and rivers, or floods the land surface, as well as how\nmoisture evaporates back into the air. Changes in any of these aspects of the interconnected water cycle can
5 affect the entire cycle and the availability of freshwater resources.\nLand use describes the combination of activities and ground cover defining each area of the Earth’s continental
6 surface. Altering land use can modify the exchange of water between the atmosphere, soil and subsurface
7 (FAQ 8.1, Figure 1).\nFor instance, changes in land cover can affect the ability of soils to soak up surface water (infiltration). When soil
8 loses its capacity to soak up water, precipitation that would normally infiltrate and contribute to groundwater\nreserves will instead overflow, increasing surface water (runoff) and the likelihood of flooding. For example,\nchanging from vegetation to urban cover can cause water to flow rapidly over buildings, roads and driveways\nand into drains rather than soaking into the ground. Deforestation over wide areas can also directly reduce\nsoil moisture, evaporation and rainfall locally but can also cause regional temperature changes that affect
9 rainfall patterns.\nExtracting water from the ground and river systems for agriculture, industry and drinking water depletes
10 groundwater and can increase surface evaporation because water that was previously in the ground is now in
11 direct contact with the atmosphere, being available for evaporation.\nChanging land use can also alter how wet the soil is, influencing how quickly the ground heats up and cools
12 down and the local water cycle. Drier soils evaporate less water into the air but heat up more in the day. This can\nlead to warmer, more buoyant plumes of air that can promote cloud development and precipitation if there is
13 enough moisture in the air.\nChanges in land use can also modify the amount of tiny aerosol particles in the air. For instance, industrial and
14 domestic activities can contribute to aerosol emissions, as do natural environments such as forests or salt lakes.\nAerosols cool down global temperature by blocking out sunlight but can also affect the formation of clouds and
15 therefore the occurrence of precipitation (see FAQ 7.2).\nVegetation plays an important role in soaking up soil moisture and evaporating water into the air (transpiration)
16 through tiny holes (stomata) that allow the plants to take in carbon dioxide. Some plants are better at retaining\nwater than others, so changes in vegetation can affect how much water infiltrates into the ground, flows into
17 streams and rivers, or is evaporated.\nMore globally, land-use change is currently responsible for about 15% of the emissions of carbon dioxide
18 from human activities, leading to global warming, which in turn affects precipitation, evaporation, and plant\ntranspiration. In addition, higher atmospheric concentrations of carbon dioxide due to human activities can\nmake plants more efficient at retaining water because the stomata do not need to open so widely. Improved\nland and water management (e.g., reforestation, sustainable irrigation) can also contribute to reducing climate
19 change and adapting to some of its adverse consequences.\nIn summary, there is abundant evidence that changes in land use and land cover alter the water cycle globally,
20 regionally and locally, by changing precipitation, evaporation, flooding, groundwater, and the availability of\nfreshwater for a variety of uses. Since all the components of the water cycle are connected (and linked to the\ncarbon cycle), changes in land use trickle down to many other components of the water cycle and climate system.
