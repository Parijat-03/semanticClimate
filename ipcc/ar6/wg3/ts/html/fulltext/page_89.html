Between 2020 and 2050, mitigation measures in forests and other natural ecosystems provide the 3 largest share of the AFOLU mitigation potential (up to USD100 tCO2-eq-1), followed by 4 agriculture and demand-side measures (high confidence). In the global sectoral studies, the 5 protection, improved management, and restoration of forests, peatlands, coastal wetlands, savannas and 6 grasslands have the potential to reduce emissions and/or sequester 7.3 mean (3.9–13.1 range) GtCO2-7 eq yr-1. Agriculture provides the second largest share of the mitigation potential, with 4.1 (1.7–6.7) 8 GtCO2-eq yr-1 (up to USD100 tCO2-eq-1) from cropland and grassland soil carbon management, 9 agroforestry, use of biochar, improved rice cultivation, and livestock and nutrient management. 10 Demand-side measures including shifting to sustainable healthy diets, reducing food waste, building 11 with wood, biochemicals, and bio-textiles, have a mitigation potential of 2.2 (1.1–3.6) GtCO2-eq yr-1. 12 Most mitigation options are available and ready to deploy. Emissions reductions can be achieved 13 relatively quickly, whereas CDR needs upfront investment. Sustainable intensification in agriculture, 14 shifting diets, and reducing food waste could enhance efficiencies and reduce agricultural land needs, 15 and are therefore critical for enabling supply-side measures such as reforestation, restoration, as well as 16 decreasing CH4 and N2O emissions from agricultural production. In addition, emerging technologies 17 (e.g., vaccines or CH4 inhibitors) have the potential to substantially increase the CH4 mitigation 18 potential beyond current estimates. AFOLU mitigation is not only relevant in countries with large land 19 areas. Many smaller countries and regions, particularly with wetlands, have disproportionately high 20 levels of AFOLU mitigation potential density. {7.4, 7.5} 21
The economic and political feasibility of implementing AFOLU mitigation measures is hampered 22 by persistent barriers. Assisting countries to overcome barriers will help to achieve significant 23 short-term mitigation (medium confidence). Finance forms a critical barrier to achieving these gains 24 as currently mitigation efforts rely principally on government sources and funding mechanisms which 25 do not provide sufficient resources to enable the economic potential to be realised. Differences in 26 cultural values, governance, accountability and institutional capacity are also important barriers. 27 Climate change itself could reduce the mitigation potential from the AFOLU sector, although an 28 increase in the capacity of natural sinks could occur despite changes in climate (medium confidence) 29 {WG I Figure SPM7 and Sections 7.4 and 7.6}. The continued loss of biodiversity makes ecosystems 30 less resilient to climate change extremes and this may further jeopardise the achievement of the AFOLU 31 mitigation potentials indicated in this chapter (high confidence).
