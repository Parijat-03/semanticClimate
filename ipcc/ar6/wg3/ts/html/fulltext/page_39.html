The literature identifies a broad set of enabling conditions that can both foster shifting 5 development pathways and accelerated mitigation (medium evidence, high agreement). Policy 6 integration is a necessary component of shifting development pathways, addressing multiple objectives. 7 To this aim, mobilising a range of policies is preferable to single policy instruments (high confidence). 8 {4.4.1}. Governance for climate mitigation and shifting development pathways is enhanced when 9 tailored to national and local contexts. Improved institutions and effective governance enable ambitious 10 action on climate and can help bridge implementation gaps (medium evidence, high agreement). Given 11 that strengthening institutions may be a long-term endeavour, it needs attention in the near-term {4.4.1}. 12 Accelerated mitigation and shifting development pathways necessitates both re-directing existing 13 financial flows from high- to low-emissions technologies and systems and to provide additional 14 resources to overcome current financial barriers (high confidence) {4.4.1}. Opportunities exist in the 15 near-term to close the finance gap {15.2.2}. At the national level, public finance for actions promoting 16 sustainable development helps broaden the scope of mitigation (medium confidence). Changes in 17 behaviour and lifestyles are important to move beyond mitigation as incremental change, and when 18 supporting shifts to more sustainable development pathways will broaden the scope of mitigation 19 (medium confidence). {4.4.1, Figure 4.8} 20
Some enabling conditions can be put in place relatively quickly while some others may take time 21 to establish underscoring the importance of early action (high confidence). Depending on context, 22 some enabling conditions such as such as promoting innovation may take time to establish. Other 23 enabling conditions, such as improved access to financing, can be put in place in a relatively short time 24 frame, and can yield rapid results {4.4, Figure 5.14, 13.9, 14.5, 15.6, 16.3, 16.4, 16.5, Cross-Chapter 25 Box 12 in Chapter 16}. Focusing on development pathways and considering how to shift them may also 26 yield rapid results by providing tools to accelerate mitigation and achieve other sustainable development 27 goals. {4.4.1}. Equity can be an important enabler, increasing the level of ambition for accelerated mitigation 30 (high confidence) {4.5}. Equity deals with the distribution of costs and benefits and how these are 31 shared, as per social contracts, national policy and international agreements. Transition pathways have 32 distributional consequences such as large changes in employment and economic structure (high 33 confidence). The just transition concept has become an international focal point tying together social 34 movements, trade unions, and other key stakeholders to ensure equity is better accounted for in low-35 carbon transitions (Box TS.4). The effectiveness of cooperative action and the perception of fairness of 36 such arrangements are closely related in that pathways that prioritise equity and allow broad stakeholder 37 participation can enable broader consensus for the transformational change implicit in the need for 38 deeper mitigation (robust evidence, medium agreement).
