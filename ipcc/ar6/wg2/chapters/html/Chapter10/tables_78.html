3 FAQ 10.2 | What are the current and emerging adaptation options across Asia?\nMirroring the heterogeneity across Asia, different countries and communities are undertaking a range of reactive and proactive strategies to
4 manage risk in various sectors. Several of these adaptation actions show promise, reducing vulnerability and improving societal well-being.\nHowever, challenges remain around scaling up adaptation actions in a manner that is effective and inclusive while simultaneously meeting
5 national development goals.\nAsia exhibits tremendous variation in terms of ecosystems, economic development, cultures and climate risk
6 exposure. Mirroring this variation, households, communities and governments have a wide range of coping and\nadaptation strategies to deal with changing climatic conditions, with co-benefits for various non-climatic issues such
7 as poverty, conflict and livelihood dynamics.\nCurrently, Asian countries have rich evidence on managing risk, drawing on long histories of dealing with change.
8 For example, to deal with erratic rainfall and shifting monsoons, farmers make incremental shifts such as changing\nwhat and when they grow or adjusting their irrigation practices. Communities living in coastal settlements are\nusing Early warning systems to prepare for cyclones or raising the height of their houses to minimise flood impacts.\nThese types of strategies, seen across all Asian sub-regions, based on local social and ecological contexts, are termed
9 autonomous adaptations that occur incrementally and help people manage current impacts.\nCurrently and in the future, Asia is identified as one of regions most vulnerable to climate change, especially
10 on extreme heat, flooding, sea level rise and erratic rainfall. All these climatic risks, when overlaid on existing\ndevelopment deficits, show us that incremental adaptation will not be enough; transformational change is\nrequired. Recognising this, at subnational and national levels, government and non-governmental actors are also\nprioritising planned adaptation strategies which include interventions like ‘climate-smart agriculture’ as seen in South\nand Southeast Asian countries, or changing labour laws to reduce exposure to heat as seen in West Asia. These are\noften sectoral priorities governments lay out through national or subnational policies and projects, drawing on\nvarious sources of funding: domestic, bilateral and international. Apart from these planned adaptation strategies in\nsocial systems, Asian countries also report and invest in adaptation measures in natural systems such as expanding\nnature reserves to enable species conservation or setting up habitat corridors to facilitate landscape connectivity
11 and species movements across climatic gradients.\nOverall, the fundamental challenges that Asia will see exacerbated under climate change are around water and
12 food insecurity, poverty and inequality, and increased frequency and severity of extreme events. In some places\nand for some people, climate change, even at 1.5°C and more so at 2°C, will significantly constrain the functioning\nand well-being of human and ecological systems. Asian cities, villages and countries are rising to this current and
13 projected challenge, albeit somewhat unevenly.\nSome examples of innovative adaptation actions are China’s ‘Sponge Cities’ which are trying to protect ecosystems
14 while reducing risk for people, now and in the future. Another example is India’s Heat Action Plans that are using\n‘cool roofs’ technologies and awareness-building campaigns to reduce the impacts of extreme heat. Across South\nand Southeast Asia, climate-smart agriculture programmes are reducing GHG emissions associated with farming\nwhile helping farmers adapt to changing risks. Each country is experimenting with infrastructural, nature-based,\ntechnological, institutional and behavioural strategies to adapt to current and future climate change with local\ncontexts shaping both the possibility of undertaking such actions as well as the effectiveness of these actions to\nreduce risk. What works for ageing cities in Japan exposed to heatwaves and floods may not work for pastoral\ncommunities in the highlands of Central Asia, but there is progress on understanding what actions work and for\nwhom. The challenge is to scale current adaptation action, especially in the most exposed areas and for the most\nvulnerable populations, as well as move beyond adapting to single risks alone (i.e., adapt to multiple coinciding\nrisks such as flooding and water scarcity in coastal cities across South Asia or extreme heat and flash floods in\nWest Asia). In this context, funding and implementing adaptation is essential, and while Asian countries are\nexperimenting with a range of autonomous and planned adaptation actions to deal with these multiple and often\nconcurrent challenges, making current development pathways climate resilient is necessary and, some might argue,\nunavoidable.
