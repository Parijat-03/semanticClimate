3 Finally, a warmer Arctic climate can, without any additional changes in atmospheric dynamics, reduce cold extremes in winter\ndue to advection of increasingly warmer air from the Arctic into the mid-latitudes (Screen, 2014; Ayarzagüena and Screen, 2016;\nAyarzagüena et al., 2018).
4 Summarizing, different hypotheses have been developed about the influence of recent Arctic warming on the mid-latitudes in both\nwinter and summer. Although some of the proposed mechanisms seem to be supported by various studies, the underlying mechanisms\nand relative strength compared to internal climate variability have been questioned. A recent review (Cohen et al., 2020) states that\ndivergent conclusions between model and observational studies, and also between different model studies, continue to obfuscate\na clear understanding of how Arctic warming is influencing mid-latitude weather. In this context, Shepherd (2016b) stresses the need\nfor collaboration between scientists with different viewpoints for further understanding that could be achieved by carefully designed,\nmulti-investigator, coordinated, multi-model simulations, data analyses and diagnostics (Overland et al., 2016). In agreement with\nBox 3.2 of SROCC, there is low to medium confidence in the exact role and quantitative effect of historical Arctic warming and sea ice\nloss on mid-latitude atmospheric variability.
5 Regarding future climate, it is important to note that mid-latitude variability is also affected by many drivers other than the Arctic\nchanges and that those drivers as well as the linkages to mid-latitude variability might change in a warmer world. The AMV, PDV,\nENSO (see Annex IV), upper tropospheric tropical heating, polar stratospheric vortex, and land surface processes associated with soil\nmoisture (Miralles et al., 2014; Hauser et al., 2016) and snow cover (Nakamura et al., 2019; Sato and Nakamura, 2019) are a few\nexamples. A considerable body of literature has shown that changes to the NAO/AO on seasonal and climate change time scales\ncan be driven by variations in the wavelength and amplitude of Rossby waves, mainly of tropical origin (Fletcher and Kushner, 2011;\nCattiaux and Cassou, 2013; Ding et al., 2014; Goss et al., 2016). The influence of future Arctic warming on mid-latitude circulation\nis difficult to disentangle from the effect of such a plethora of drivers (Blackport and Kushner, 2017; F. Li et al., 2018). One of the\nconsequences of climate change is a poleward shift of the jet induced by the tropical warming (Barnes and Polvani, 2013), which is\nless obvious in winter especially over the North Atlantic (Peings et al., 2018; Oudar et al., 2020), and the increase of the meridional\ntemperature gradient in the upper troposphere, which increases storm track activity (Barnes and Screen, 2015; Parding et al., 2019).\nAlthough climate models indicate that future Arctic warming and the associated equator–pole temperature gradient decrease could\naffect mid-latitude climate and variability (Haarsma et al., 2013a; McCusker et al., 2017; Zappa et al., 2018), and even the tropics and\nsubtropics (Deser et al., 2015; Cvijanovic et al., 2017; K. Wang et al., 2018; England et al., 2020; Kennel and Yulaeva, 2020), they do\nnot reveal a strong influence on extreme weather (Woollings et al., 2014).
6 In conclusion, there is low confidence in the relative contribution of Arctic warming to mid-latitude atmospheric changes compared to\nother drivers. Future climate change could affect mid-latitude variability in a number of ways that are still to be clarified, and which\nmay also include the influence of Arctic warming. The linkages between the Arctic warming and the mid-latitude circulation are an\nexample of contrasting lines of evidence that cannot yet be reconciled (Section 10.5).
