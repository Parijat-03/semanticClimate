Accelerating mitigation to prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system 6 will require the integration of broadened assessment frameworks and tools that combine multiple 7 perspectives, applied in a context of multi-level governance (high confidence). Analysing a 8 challenge on the scale of fully decarbonising our economies entails integration of multiple analytic 9 frameworks. Approaches to risk assessment and resilience, established across IPCC Working Groups, 10 are complemented by frameworks for probing the challenges in implementing mitigation. Aggregate 11 frameworks include cost-effectiveness analysis towards given objectives, and cost-benefit analysis, 12 both of which have been developing to take fuller account of advances in understanding risks and 13 innovation, the dynamics of sectors and systems and of climate impacts, and welfare economic theory 14 including growing consensus on long-term discounting. Ethical frameworks consider the fairness of 15 processes and outcomes which can help ameliorate distributional impacts across income groups, 16 countries and generations. Transition and transformation frameworks explain and evaluate the 17 dynamics of transitions to low-carbon systems arising from interactions amongst levels. Psychological, 18 behavioural and political frameworks outline the constraints (and opportunities) arising from human 19 psychology and the power of incumbent interests. A comprehensive understanding of climate mitigation 20 must combine these multiple frameworks. Together with established risk frameworks, these collectively 21 help to explain potential synergies and trade-offs in mitigation, implying a need for a wide portfolio of 22 policies attuned to different actors and levels of decision-making, and underpin ‘just transition’ 23 strategies in diverse contexts. {1.2.2, 1.7, 1.8, Figure 1.7} 24
The speed, direction, and depth of any transition will be determined by choices in the 25 environmental, technological, economic, socio-cultural and institutional realms (high confidence). 26 Transitions in specific systems can be gradual or can be rapid and disruptive. The pace of a transition 27 can be impeded by ‘lock-in’ generated by existing physical capital, institutions, and social norms. The 28 interaction between politics, economics and power relationships is central to explaining why broad 29 commitments do not always translate to urgent action. At the same time, attention to, and support for, 30 climate policies and low carbon societal transitions has generally increased, as the impacts have become 31 more salient. Both public and private financing and financial structures strongly affect the scale and 32 balance of high and low carbon investments. Societal and behavioural norms, regulations and 33 institutions are essential conditions to accelerate low carbon transitions in multiple sectors, whilst 34 addressing distributional concerns endemic to any major transition. The COVID-19 pandemic has also 35 had far-reaching impacts on the global economic and social system, and recovery will present both 36 challenges and opportunities for climate mitigation. Achieving the global transition to a low-carbon, climate-resilient and sustainable world requires 39 purposeful and increasingly coordinated planning and decisions at many scales of governance 40 including local, subnational, national and global levels (high confidence). Accelerating mitigation 41 globally would imply strengthening policies adopted to date, expanding the effort across options, 42 sectors, and countries, and broadening responses to include more diverse actors and societal processes 43 at multiple – including international – levels. The effective governance of climate change entails strong 44 action across multiple jurisdictions and decision-making levels, including regular evaluation and 45 learning. Choices that cause climate change as well as the processes for making and implementing 46 relevant decisions involve a range of non-nation state actors such as cities, businesses, and civil society 47 organisations.
