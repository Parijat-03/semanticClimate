How to shift development pathways? 1
Shifting development paths is complex. Changes that involve ‘dissimilar, unfamiliar and more complex 2 science-based components’ take more time, acceptance and legitimation and involve complex social 3 learning, even when they promise large gains. Despite the complexities of the interactions that result in 4 patterns of development, history also shows that societies can influence the direction of development 5 pathways based on choices made by decision-makers, citizens, the private sector, and social 6 stakeholders. Shifts in development pathways result from both sustained political interventions and 7 bottom-up changes in public opinion. Collective action by individuals as part of social movements or 8 lifestyle changes underpins system change. {5.2.3, 5.4.1, 5.4.5}. 9
Sectoral transitions that aim to shift development pathways often have multiple objectives and deploy 10 a diverse mix of policies and institutional measures. Context specific governance conditions can 11 significantly enable or disable sectoral transitions {Cross-Chapter Box 12 in Chapter 16}. 12
The necessary transformational changes are anticipated to be more acceptable if rooted in the 13 development aspirations of the economy and society within which they take place and may enable a 14 new social contract to address a complex set of inter-linkages across sectors, classes, and the whole 15 economy. Taking advantage of windows of opportunity and disruptions to mindsets and socio-technical 16 systems could advance deeper transformations. 17
How can shifts in development pathways be implemented by actors in different contexts? 18
Shifting development pathways to increased sustainability is a shared aspiration. Yet since countries 19 differ in starting points (e.g., social, economic, cultural, political) and historical backgrounds, they have 20 different urgent needs in terms of facilitating the economic, social, and environmental dimensions of 21 sustainable development and, therefore, give different priorities {4.3.2, 17.1}. The appropriate set of 22 policies to shift development pathways thus depends on national circumstances and capacities. 23
Shifting development pathways towards sustainability needs to be supported by multilateral 24 partnerships to strengthen suitable capacity, technological innovation (TS 6.5), and financial flows (TS. 25 6.4). The international community can play a particularly key role by helping ensure the necessary broad 26 participation in climate-mitigation efforts, including by countries at different development levels, 27 through sustained support for policies and partnerships that support shifting development pathways 28 towards sustainability while promoting equity and being mindful of different transition capacities. 29 {Chapter 4.3, 16.5, 16.6} 30
