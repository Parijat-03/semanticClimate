The creation and growth of sustainable energy and clean-tech clusters enable economic development
14
and transformation on regional scales. Such clusters can put pressure on incumbent technologies and
15
rules to accelerate energy transitions. Successful clusters are nurtured by multi-institutional and multi-
16
stakeholder actors building institutional support networks, facilitating collaboration between sectors
17
and actors, and promoting learning and social change. Notably, regional economic clusters generate a
18
buzz, which can have a strong influence on public acceptance, support and enthusiasm for
19
sociotechnical transitions (McCauley and Stephens 2012).

In Norway, many incumbent energy firms have already expanded their operations into the alternative
22
energy sector as both producers and suppliers (who often follow the lead of producers). Producers are
23
responding to perceptions of larger-scale changes in the energy landscape (e.g., the green shift), along
24
with uncertainties in their own sectors, and innovation can spill across actors in multiple sectors
25
(Koasidis et al. 2020). While these firms are expanding out of self-interest, the expansion provides more
26
legitimacy to new forms of technology and enables transfers of knowledge and resources to be
27
introduced within this developing niche (Steen and Weaver 2017). Many large, well-established firms
28
are pursuing sustainability agendas and opting for transparency with regard to their greenhouse gas
29
emissions (Guenther et al. 2016; Kolk et al. 2008), supply chain management (Formentini and Taticchi
30
2016) and sustainable technology or service development (Dangelico et al. 2016).

Experiments with the transition open up pathways that can lead to energy transitions on broader scales.
33
Experiments can build capacity by developing networks and building bridges between diverse actors,
34
leveraging capital from government funds, de-risking private- and public-sector investment, and acting
35
as hubs for public education and engagement (Rosenbloom et al. 2018).

Material barriers and spatial dynamics (Coenen et al. 2012; Hansen and Coenen 2015) are other critical
38
obstacles to innovation: often, infrastructure and built environments change more slowly than policies
39
and institutions due to the inherently long lifespans of fixed assets (Turnheim and Nykvist 2019). The
40
example of transport infrastructure in Ontario, Canada, illustrates the need to integrate climate change
41
into these infrastructural decisions in the very short term to combat the risk of being left with
42
unsustainable planning features long into the future, especially combustion engines, significant road
43
networks and suburbanization (Birch 2016).

17.4.3
Financial systems and economic instruments 46
Market-oriented policies, such as carbon taxes and green finance, can promote low-carbon technology
47
and encourage both private and public investment in enabling transitions. Policies that are currently
48
being tested include loan guarantees for renewable energy investments in Mali, policy insurance to
49
reduce credit defaults within the feed-in tariff regime in Germany, or pledged funding to fully finance
50
or partner private firms in order to advance renewable energy projects (Roy et al. 2018a). However,
51
there may be some limitations in using carbon-pricing alone (rather than in combination with flexible
52
regulations and incentives) where market failures hinder low-carbon investments (Campiglio 2016;
53
World Bank 2019) and high political costs are incurred (Van Der Ploeg 2011).
