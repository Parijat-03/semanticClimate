2 FAQ 8.2 | Will Floods Become More Severe or More Frequent as a Result of Climate Change?
3
4 A warmer climate increases the amount and intensity of rainfall during wet events, and this is expected to\namplify the severity of flooding. However, the link between rainfall and flooding is complex, so while the most
5 severe flooding events are expected to worsen, floods could become rarer in some regions.\nFloods are a natural and important part of the water cycle but they can also threaten lives and safety, disrupt
6 human activities, and damage infrastructure. Most inland floods occur when rivers overtop their banks (fluvial\nflooding) or when intense rainfall causes water to build up and overflow locally (pluvial flooding). Flooding is\nalso caused by coastal inundation by the sea, rapid seasonal melting of snow, and the accumulation of debris,
7 such as vegetation or ice, that stops water from draining away.\nClimate change is already altering the location, frequency and severity of flooding. Close to the coasts, rising
8 sea levels increasingly cause more frequent and severe coastal flooding, and the severity of these floods is\nexacerbated when combined with heavy rainfall. The heavy and sustained rainfall events responsible for most\ninland flooding are becoming more intense in many areas as the climate warms because air near Earth’s surface\ncan carry around 7% more water in its gas phase (vapour) for each 1°C of warming. This extra moisture is drawn
9 into weather systems, fueling heavier rainfall (FAQ 8.2, Figure 1).\nA warming climate also affects wind patterns, how storms form and evolve, and the pathway those storms
10 usually travel. Warming also increases condensation rates, which in turn releases extra heat that can energize\nstorm systems and further intensify rainfall. On the other hand, this energy release can also inhibit the uplift\nrequired for cloud development, while increases in particle pollution can delay rainfall but invigorate storms.\nThese changes mean that the character of precipitation events (how often, how long lasting and how heavy they
11 are) will continue to change as the climate warms.\nIn addition to climate change, the location, frequency and timing of the heaviest rainfall events and worst
12 flooding depend on natural fluctuations in wind patterns that make some regions unusually wet or dry for\nmonths, years, or even decades. These natural variations make it difficult to determine whether heavy rainfall\nevents are changing locally as a result of global warming. However, when natural weather patterns bring heavy
13 and prolonged rainfall in a warmer climate, the intensity is increased by the larger amount of moisture in the air.\nAn increased intensity and frequency of record-breaking daily rainfall has been detected for much of the land
14 surface where good observational records exist, and this can only be explained by human-caused increases in\natmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations. Heavy rainfall is also projected to become more intense in the future\nfor most places. So, where unusually wet weather events or seasons occur, the rainfall amounts are expected to
15 be greater in the future, contributing to more severe flooding.\nHowever, heavier rainfall does not always lead to greater flooding. This is because flooding also depends
16 upon the type of river basin, the surface landscape, the extent and duration of the rainfall, and how wet the\nground is before the rainfall event (FAQ 8.2, Figure 1). Some regions will experience a drying in the soil as the\nclimate warms, particularly in subtropical climates, which could make floods from a rainfall event less probable\nbecause the ground can potentially soak up more of the rain. On the other hand, less frequent but more intense\ndownpours can lead to dry, hard ground that is less able to soak up heavy rainfall when it does occur, resulting\nin more runoff into lakes, rivers and hollows. Earlier spring snowmelt combined with more precipitation falling\nas rain rather than snow can trigger flood events in cold regions. Reduced winter snow cover can, in contrast,\ndecrease the chance of flooding arising from the combination of rainfall and rapid snowmelt. Rapid melting of\nglaciers and snow in a warming climate is already increasing river flow in some regions, but as the volumes of ice\ndiminish, flows will peak and then decline in the future. Flooding is also affected by changes in the management\nof the land and river systems. For example, clearing forests for agriculture or building cities can make rainwater\nflow more rapidly into rivers or low-lying areas. On the other hand, increased extraction of water from rivers can\nreduce water levels and the likelihood of flooding.
