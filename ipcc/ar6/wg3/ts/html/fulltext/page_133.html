Although some initiatives have mobilised investments in developing countries, gaps in innovation 5 cooperation remain, including in the Paris Agreement instruments. These gaps could be filled by 6 enhancing financial support for international technology cooperation, by strengthening 7 cooperative approaches, and by helping build suitable capacity in developing countries across all 8 technological innovation system functions (high confidence). The implementation of current 9 arrangements of international cooperation for technology development and transfer, as well as capacity 10 building, are insufficient to meet climate objectives and contribute to sustainable development. For 11 example, despite building a large market for mitigation technologies in developing countries, the lack 12 of a systemic perspective in the implementation of the Clean Development Mechanism, operational 13 since the mid-2000s, has only led to some technology transfer, especially to larger developing countries, 14 but limited capacity building and minimal technology development (medium confidence). In the current 15 climate regime, a more systemic approach to innovation cooperation could be introduced by linking 16 technology institutions, such as the Technology Mechanism, and financial actors, such as the financial 17 mechanism. {16.5.3} 18
Countries are exposed to sustainable development challenges in parallel with the challenges that 19 relate to climate change. Addressing both sets of challenges simultaneously presents multiple and 20 recurrent obstacles that systemic approaches to technological change could help resolve, provided 21 they are well managed (high confidence). Obstacles include both entrenched power relations 22 dominated by vested interests that control and benefit from existing technologies, and governance 23 structures that continue to reproduce unsustainable patterns of production and consumption (medium 24 confidence). Studies also highlight the potential of cultural factors to strongly influence the pace and 25 direction of technological change. Sustainable solutions require adoption and mainstreaming of locally 26 novel technologies that can meet local needs, and simultaneously address the SDGs. Acknowledging 27 the systemic nature of technological innovation – which involve many levels of actors, stages of 28 innovation and scales – can lead to new opportunities to shift development pathways towards 29 sustainability. {16.4, 16.5, 16.6} 30
Strategies for climate change mitigation can be most effective in accelerating transformative 31 change when actions taken to strengthen one set of enabling conditions also reinforce and 32 strengthen the effectiveness of other enabling conditions (medium confidence). Applying transition 33 or system dynamics to decisions can help policy makers take advantage of such high-leverage 34 intervention points, address the specific characteristics of technological stages, and respond to societal 35 dynamics. Inspiration can be drawn from the global unit cost reductions of solar PV, which were 36 accelerated by a combination of factors interacting in a mutually reinforcing way across a limited group 37 of countries (high confidence). {Box 16.2, Cross-Chapter Box 12 in Chapter 16}.
