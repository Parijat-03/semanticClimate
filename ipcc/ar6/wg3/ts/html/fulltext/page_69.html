Urban green and blue infrastructure can mitigate climate change through carbon sinks, avoided 3 emissions, and reduced energy use while offering multiple co-benefits (high confidence). Urban 4 green and blue infrastructure, including urban forests and street trees, permeable surfaces, and green 5 roofs offer potentials to mitigate climate change directly through storing carbon, and indirectly by 6 inducing a cooling effect that reduces energy demand and reducing energy use for water treatment. 7 Globally, urban trees store approximately 7.4 billion tonnes of carbon, and sequester approximately 217 8 million tonnes of carbon annually, although carbon storage is highly dependent on biome. Among the 9 multiple co-benefits of green and blue infrastructure are reducing the urban heat island (UHI) effect and 10 heat stress, reducing stormwater runoff, improving air quality, and improving the mental and physical 11 health of urban dwellers. Many of these options also provide benefits to climate adaptation. {8.2, 8.4.4} 12
The potentials and sequencing of mitigation strategies to reduce GHG emissions will vary 13 depending on a city’s land use and spatial form and its state of urbanisation, whether it is an 14 established city with existing infrastructure, a rapidly growing city with new infrastructure, or 15 an emerging city with infrastructure build-up (medium confidence). The long lifespan of urban 16 infrastructures locks in behaviour and emissions. Urban infrastructures and urban form can enable 17 socio-cultural and lifestyle changes that can significantly reduce carbon footprints. Rapidly growing 18 cities can avoid higher future emissions through urban planning to co-locate jobs and housing to achieve 19 compact urban form, and by leapfrogging to low-carbon technologies. Established cities will achieve 20 the largest GHG emissions savings by replacing, repurposing, or retrofitting the building stock, strategic 21 infilling and densifying, as well as through modal shift and the electrification of the urban energy 22 system. New and emerging cities have unparalleled potential to significantly reduce GHG emissions 23 while achieving high quality of life by creating compact, co-located, and walkable urban areas with 24 mixed land use and transit-oriented design, that also preserve existing green and blue assets. {8.2, 8.4, 25 8.6} 26
With over 880 million people living in informal settlements, there are opportunities to harness 27 and enable informal practices and institutions in cities related to housing, waste, energy, water, 28 and sanitation to reduce resource use and mitigate climate change (low evidence, medium 29 agreement). The upgrading of informal settlements and inadequate housing to improve resilience and 30 well-being offers a chance to create a low-carbon transition. However, there is limited quantifiable data 31 on these practices and their cumulative impacts on GHG emissions. {8.1.4, 8.2.2, Cross-Working Group 32 Box 2 in Chapter 8, 8.3.2, 8.4, 8.6, 8.7} 33
Achieving transformational changes in cities for climate change mitigation and adaptation will 34 require engaging multiple scales of governance, including governments and non-state actors, and 35 in connection with substantial financing beyond sectoral approaches (very high confidence). Large 36 and complex infrastructure projects for urban mitigation are often beyond the capacity of local 37 municipality budgets, jurisdictions, and institutions. Partnerships between cities and international 38 institutions, national and region governments, transnational networks, and local stakeholders play a 39 pivotal role in mobilizing global climate finance resources for a range of infrastructure projects with 40 low-carbon emissions and related spatial planning programs across key sectors. {8.4, 8.5} 41
