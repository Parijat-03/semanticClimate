FAQ 17.2 What role do considerations of justice and inclusivity play in the transition towards
22
sustainable development?
23
Negative economic and social impacts in some regions could emerge as a consequence of ambitious
24
climate change mitigation policies if these are not aligned with key sustainable development aspirations
25
such as those represented by the SDGs on ‘no poverty, energy-, water- and food access’ etc., which
26
could in turn slow down the transition process. Nonetheless, many climate change mitigation policies
27
could generate incomes, new jobs and other benefits. Capturing these benefits could require specific
28
policies and investments to be targeted directly towards including all parts of society in the new
29
activities and industries created by the climate change mitigation policies, and that activities that are
30
reduced in the context of transitions to a low carbon future, including industries and geographical areas,
31
are seeing new opportunities. Poor understanding of how governance at multiple levels can meet these
32
challenges to the transition may fail to make significant progress in relation to national policies and a
33
global climate agreement. It may therefore either support or weaken the climate architecture, thus
34
constituting a limiting factor.

FAQ 17.3 How critical are the roles of institutions in accelerating the transition and what can
37

38
39
Institutions are critical in accelerating the transition towards sustainable development: they can help to
40
shape climate change response strategies in terms of both adaptation and mitigation. Local institutions
41
are the custodians of critical adaptation services, ranging from the mobilisation of resources, skills
42
development and capacity-building to the dissemination of critical strategies. Transitions towards
43
sustainable development are mediated by actors within particular institutions, the governance
44
mechanisms they use as implementing tools and the political coalitions they form to enable action.
45
Patterns of production and consumption have implications for a low-carbon development, and many of
46
these patterns can act as barriers or opportunities towards sustainable development. Trade policies,
47
international economic issues and international financial flows can positively support the speed and
48
scale of the transition; alternatively, they can have negative impacts on policies that may inhibit the
49
process. Nonetheless, contextual factors are a fundamental part of the change process, and institutions
50
and their governance systems provide pathways that can influence contextual realities on the ground.
