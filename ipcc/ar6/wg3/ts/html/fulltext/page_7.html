The report draws from literature on broad and diverse analytic frameworks across multiple disciplines. 3 These include, inter alia: economic and environmental efficiency {1}; ethics and equity {4, 5, 17}; 4 innovation and the dynamics of socio-technical transitions {16}; and, socio-political-institutional 5 frameworks {1, 5, 13, 14, 17}. These help to identify synergies and trade-offs with sustainable 6 development goals, challenges and windows of opportunity for action including co-benefits, and 7 equitable transitions at local, national and global scales. {1, 5, 13, 14, 16}. 8
This Technical Summary (TS) of the WG III contribution to the IPCC’s Sixth Assessment Report 9 broadly follows the report chapter order and is structured as follows. 10
• TS Section 2 sets out how the global context for mitigation has changed and summarises signs of 11 progress and continuing challenges. 12 • TS Section 3 evaluates emission trends and drivers including recent sectoral, financial, 13 technological and policy developments. 14 • TS Section 4 identifies mitigation and development pathways in the near and medium- term to 15 2050, and in the longer term to 2100. This section includes an assessment of how mitigation 16 pathways deploying different portfolios of mitigation responses are consistent with limiting global 17 warming to different levels. 18 • TS Section 5 summarises recent advances in knowledge across sectors and systems including 19 energy, urban and other settlements, transport, buildings, industry, and agriculture, forestry and 20 other land use. 21 • TS Section 6 examines how enabling conditions including behaviour and lifestyle, policy, 22 governance and institutional capacity, international cooperation, finance, and innovation and 23 technology can accelerate mitigation in the context of sustainable development 24 • TS Section 7 evaluates how mitigation can be achieved in the context of sustainable development, 25 while maximising co-benefits and minimising risks. 26

FOOTNOTE 2Each finding is grounded in an evaluation of the underlying evidence, typeset in italics. The validity of a finding is evaluated in terms of the evidence quality – ‘limited’, ‘medium’, ‘robust’ – and the degree of agreement between sources – ‘low’, ‘medium’, ‘high’. A level of confidence is expressed using five qualifiers: very low, low, medium, high and very high. Generally, the level of confidence is highest where there is robust evidence from multiple sources and high agreement. For findings with, for example, ‘robust evidence, medium agreement’, a confidence statement may not always be appropriate. The assessed likelihood of an outcome or a result is described as: virtually certain 99–100% probability, very likely 90–100%, likely 66–100%, about as likely as not 33–66%, unlikely 0–33%, very unlikely 0–10%, exceptionally unlikely 0–1%. Additional terms may also be used when appropriate, consistent with the IPCC uncertainty guidance: https://www.ipcc.ch/site/assets/uploads/2018/05/uncertainty-guidance-note.pdf.
