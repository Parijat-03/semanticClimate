Box TS.3: Shifting development pathways to increase sustainability and broaden mitigation 2 options 3
In this report, development pathways refer to the patterns of development resulting from multiple 4 decisions and choices made by many actors in the national and global contexts. Each society whether 5 in developing or developed regions follows its own pattern of growth (Figure TS.13). Development 6 pathways can also be described at smaller scales (e.g., for regions or cities) and for sectoral systems. 7
Development pathways are major drivers of GHG emissions {1, 2}. There is compelling evidence to 8 show that continuing along existing development pathways will not achieve rapid and deep emission 9 reductions. In the absence of shifts in development pathways, conventional mitigation policy 10 instruments may not be able to limit global emissions to a degree sufficient to meet ambitious mitigation 11 goals or they may only be able to do so at very high economic and social costs. 12
Policies to shift development pathways, on the other hand, make mitigation policies more effective. 13 Shifting development pathways broadens the scope for synergies between sustainable development 14 objectives and mitigation. Development pathways also determine the enablers and levers available for 15 adaptation {AR6 WG II TS E.1.2} and for achieving other SDGs. 16
There are many instances in which reducing GHG emissions and moving towards the achievement of 17 other development objectives can go hand in hand {Chapter 3, Fig 3.33, Chapters 6-12, 17}. Integrated 18 policies can support the creation of synergies between action to combat climate change and its impacts 19 (SDG 13) and other SDGs. For example, when measures promoting walkable urban areas are combined 20 with electrification and clean renewable energy, there are several co-benefits to be attained. These 21 include reduced pressures on agricultural land from reduced urban growth, health co-benefits from 22 cleaner air and benefits from enhanced mobility {8.2, 8.4, 4.4.1}. Energy efficiency in buildings and 23 energy poverty alleviation through improved access to clean fuels also deliver significant health 24 benefits. {9.8.1 and 9.8.2} 25
However, decisions about mitigation actions, and their timing and scale, may entail trade-offs with the 26 achievement of other national development objectives in the near-, mid- and long-term {Chapter 12}. 27 In the near-term, for example, regulations may ban vehicles from city centres to reduce congestion and 28 local air pollution but reduce mobility and choice. Increasing green spaces within cities without caps 29 on housing prices may involve trade-offs with affordable housing and push low-income residents 30 outside the city {8.2.2}. In the mid- and long-term, large-scale deployment of biomass energy raises 31 concerns about food security and biodiversity conservation {3.7.1, 3.7.5, 7.4.4, 9.8.1, 12.5.2, 12.5.3}. 32 Prioritising is one way to manage these trade-offs, addressing some national development objectives 33 earlier than others. Another way is to adopt policy packages aimed at shifting development pathways 34 towards sustainability (SDPS) as they expand the range of tools available to simultaneously achieve 35 multiple development objectives and accelerate mitigation. What does shifting development pathways towards sustainability entail? 37
Shifting development pathways towards sustainability implies making transformative changes that 38 disrupt existing developmental trends. Such choices would not be marginal, but include technological, 39 systemic and socio-behavioural changes {4.4}. Decision points also arise with new infrastructure, 40 sustainable supply chains, institutional capacities for evidence-based and integrated decision-making, 41 financial alignment towards low-carbon socially responsible investments, just transitions and shifts in 42 behaviour and norms to support shifts away from fossil fuel consumption. Adopting multi-level 43 governance modes, tackling corruption where it inhibits shifts to sustainability, and improving social 44 and political trust are also key for aligning and supporting long-term environmentally just policies and 45 processes. {4.4, Cross-Chapter Box 5 in Chapter 4} 46
