The complementarity and interdependency of measures leads to cost reductions, while 14 optimising the mitigation potential achieved and avoiding the lock-in-effect (medium confidence). {9.6, 15 9.9} 16
The decarbonisation of buildings is constrained by multiple barriers and obstacles as well as 17 limited finance flows (high confidence). The lack of institutional capacity, especially in developing 18 countries, and appropriate governance structures slow down the decarbonisation of the global 19 building stock (medium confidence). The building sector is highly heterogenous with many different 20 building types, sizes, and operational uses. The sub-segment representing rented property faces 21 principal/agent problems where the tenant benefits from the decarbonisation’s investment made by the 22 landlord. The organisational context and the governance structure could trigger or hinder the 23 decarbonisation of buildings. Global investment in the decarbonisation of buildings was estimated at 24 USD164 billion in 2020. However, this is not enough by far to close the investment gap (high 25 confidence). {9.9} 26
Policy packages could grasp the full mitigation potential of the global building stock. Building 27 energy codes represent the main regulatory instrument to reduce emissions from both new and 28 existing buildings (high confidence). The most advanced building energy codes include requirements 29 on each of the three pillars of the SER framework in the use and construction phase of buildings. 30 Building energy codes have proven to be effective if compulsory and combined with other regulatory 31 instruments such as minimum energy performance standard for appliances and equipment, if the 32 performance level is set at the level of the best available technologies in the market (high confidence). 33 Market-based instruments such as carbon taxes with recycling of the revenues and personal or building 34 carbon allowances could also contribute to fostering the decarbonisation of the building sector (medium 35 confidence). {9.9} 36
Adapting buildings to future climate while ensuring well-being for all requires action. Expected 37 heatwaves will inevitably increase cooling needs to limit the health impacts of climate change 38 (medium confidence). Global warming will impact cooling and heating needs but also the performance, 39 durability and safety of buildings, especially historical and coastal ones, through changes in 40 temperature, humidity, atmospheric concentrations of CO2 and chloride, and sea level rise. Adaptation 41 measures to cope with climate change may increase the demand for energy and materials leading to an 42 increase in GHG emissions if not mitigated. Sufficiency measures which anticipate climate change, and 43 include natural ventilation, white walls, and nature-based solutions (e.g. green roofs) will decrease the 44 demand for cooling. Shared cooled spaces with highly efficient cooling solutions are among the 45 mitigation strategies which can limit the effect of the expected heatwaves on people’s health. {9.7, 9.8} 46
