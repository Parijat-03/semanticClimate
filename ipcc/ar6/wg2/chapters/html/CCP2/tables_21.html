3 FAQ CCP2.2 | What actions can be taken by coastal cities and settlements to reduce climate change risk?\nSea level rise (SLR) responds to climate change over long timeframes and will continue even after successful mitigation. However, rapid global
4
5 mitigation of greenhouse gases significantly reduces risks to coastal cities and settlements (C&S), and, crucially, buys time for adaptation.\nAppropriate actions to reduce climate change risks in coastal C&S depend on the scale and speed of coastal change
6
7 interacting with unfolding local circumstances, reflecting the hazards, exposure, vulnerability and response to risks.\n‘Hard’ protection, like dikes and seawalls, can reduce the risk of flooding for several metres of SLR in some coastal
8 C&S. These are most cost effective for densely populated cities and some islands, but may be unaffordable for\npoorer regions. Although these measures reduce the likelihood of coastal flooding, residual risk remains, and hard\nprotection typically has negative consequences for natural systems. In low-lying protected coastal zones, draining\nriver and excess water will increasingly be hampered, eventually requiring pumping or transferring to alternative
9 strategies.\nWhereas structures can disrupt natural beach morphology processes, sediment-based protection replenishes
10 beaches. These have lower impact on adjacent beaches and coastal ecology and lower costs for construction and\nmaintenance compared to hard structures. Another form of ‘soft’ protection involves establishing, rehabilitating\nand preserving coastal ecosystems, like marshes, mangroves, seagrass, coral reefs and dunes, providing ‘soft’\nprotection against storm surges, reducing coastal erosion and offering additional benefits including food, materials\nand carbon sequestration. However, these are less effective where there is limited space in the coastal zone, limited
11 sediment supply and under higher rates of SLR.\nCoastal settlements can ‘avoid’ new flood and erosion risks by preventing development in areas exposed to
12 current and future coastal hazards. Where development already exists, settlements can ‘accommodate’ climate\nchange impacts through, among other things, land-use zoning, raising ground or buildings above storm surge\nlevels, installing flood-proofing measures within and outside properties, and early warning systems. Improving the\ncapacity of urban drainage, incorporating nature-based solutions within urban areas and managing land upstream\nof settlements to reduce runoff from the hinterland reduces the risk of compound flood events. More radically,\nland can also be reclaimed from the sea, which offers opportunities for further development but has impacts on the
13 natural system and wider implications for the trajectory of development.\nCoastal risks and impacts such as floods, loss of fisheries or tourism, or salinization of groundwater require people
14 to change behaviour to adapt, such as diversifying livelihoods or moving away from low-lying areas. Currently,\nmost of these practices are reactive and help people adjust to/cope with current impacts. While a critical part of\ncoastal adaptation, changing behaviour can be enabled by supportive policies and financial structures aligned with
15 sociocultural values and worldviews.\nWhere risks are very high or resources are insufficient to manage risks, submergence or erosion of coastal C&S will
16 be inevitable, requiring ‘retreat’ from the coastline. This is the outlook for millions of people in the coming decades,\nincluding those living in river deltas, Arctic communities, small islands and low-lying small settlements in poor and\nwealthy nations. Whilst the impacts of retreat on communities can be devastating, the prospect of many C&S and\neven whole nations being permanently inundated in the coming centuries underscores the imperative for urgent
17 action.\nCrucial to making choices about how to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions and adapt to climate change in
18 coastal C&S is to establish institutions and governance practices supporting climate resilient development—a mix\nand sequence of mitigation and adaptation actions—that are fair, just and inclusive as well as technically and\neconomically effective across successive generations.
