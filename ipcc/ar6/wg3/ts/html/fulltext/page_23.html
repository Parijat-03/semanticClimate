However, GWP100 is not well suited to estimate the cumulative effect on climate from sustained SLCF 3 emissions and the resulting warming at specific points in time. This is because the warming caused by 4 an individual SLCF emission pulse is not permanent, and hence, unlike CO2, the warming from 5 successive SLCF emission pulses over multiple decades or centuries depends mostly on their ongoing 6 rate of emissions rather than cumulative emissions. Recently developed step/pulse metrics such as the 7 CGTP (Combined Global Temperature Change Potential) and GWP* (referred to as GWP-star and 8 indicated by asterisk) recognise that a sustained increase/decrease in the rate of SLCF emissions has 9 indeed a similar effect on global surface temperature as one-off emission/removal of CO2. These metrics 10 use this relationship to calculate the CO2 emissions or removals that would result in roughly the same 11 temperature change as a sustained change in the rate of SLCF emissions (CGTP) over a given time 12 period, or as a varying time series of CH4 emissions (GWP*). From a mitigation perspective, this makes 13 these metrics well suited in principle to estimate the effect on the remaining carbon budget from more, 14 or less, ambitious SLCF mitigation over multiple decades compared to a given reference scenario (high 15 confidence). However, potential application in wider climate policy (e.g., to inform equitable and 16 ambitious emission targets or to support sector-specific mitigation policies) is contested and relevant 17 literature still limited. 18
All metrics have limitations and uncertainties, given that they simplify the complexity of the physical 19 climate system and its response to past and future GHG emissions. For this reason, the WG III 20 contribution to the AR6 reports emissions and mitigation options for individual gases where possible; 21 CO2-equivalent emissions are reported in addition to individual gas emissions where this is judged to 22 be policy-relevant. This approach aims to reduce the ambiguity regarding actual climate outcomes over 23 time arising from the use of any specific GHG emission metric. {Cross-Chapter Box 2 in Chapter 2, 24 Supplementary Material 2.3, Annex II Part II Section 8; WG I Chapter 7.6} 25
