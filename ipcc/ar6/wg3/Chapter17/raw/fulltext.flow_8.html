17.1.2 Integrating Climate Change and Sustainable Development in International 10
Policymaking Processes 11
Among the reasons for the growing realization of these interdependencies are milestones in
12
international climate and sustainable development processes. As outlined in Chapter 14, the year 2015
13
was a turning point due to two agreements: 1) the Paris Agreement; and 2) the 2030 Agenda on
14
Sustainable Development and its seventeen Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) (Farzaneh et al.
15
2021).
16
17
Following a long history of references to sustainable development in the UNFCCC and related
18
agreements, the Paris Agreement helped to strengthen the links between climate and sustainable
19
development by emphasizing that sustainability is related to its objectives (Sindico 2016; UNFCCC
20
2016). One of the ways that it helped tighten this link is by institutionalizing bottom-up pledges and the
21
review architecture. Toward this end, the Paris Agreement instituted nationally determined
22
contributions (NDCs) as vehicles through which countries make pledges and demonstrate their
23
commitment to climate action. Although there was no clear guidance on what should be included in the
24
NDCs, some of the requirements were elaborated in the Paris Rule Book (see above, Chapter 14). Some
25
of the submitted NDCs included only mitigation efforts, but others set out mitigation and adaptation
26
goals aligning NDC commitments to national planning processes, while yet others mentioned links with
27
the SDGs.
28
29
Another way that the Paris Agreement and the NDCs could strengthen their links to sustainable
30
development is to update country-specific climate pledges. Countries are free to choose their targets
31
and the means and instruments with which to implement them. A core feature of the NDCs was that
32
countries submit NDCs every five years, giving them an opportunity to assess themselves relative to
33
other countries, raise their ambitions and learn from their peers. Moreover, it was emphasized that
34
countries should not “backslide” in subsequent NDCs, thus ensuring that countries should always be
35
forward-looking in respect of increasing their ambitions to deliver the Paris Goals. Höhne et al. (2017)
36
found that, in developing countries especially, the NDC preparation process has improved national
37
climate policy-making.
38
39
Despite some favourable reviews, several assessments of specific countries’ NDCs (Andries et al. 2017;
40
Rogelj et al. 2016; Vandyck et al. 2016) have assessed that those submitted for 2020-2030 are
41
insufficient for delivering on the Paris goals. Updated and/or new NDCs were therefore submitted by
42
end of 2020. However, an assessment of those NDCs revealed that the level of ambition was
43
significantly lower than the goals of the Paris Agreement (UNFCCO 2020; see also this Chapter). One
44
of the urgent calls in Paris was to assess the impacts and efforts that need to be undertaken to keep
45
global warming well below 2°C in relation to pre-industrial levels and evaluate related global
46
greenhouse-gas emission pathways (UNFCCC 2015). Although the initial NDCs fell short of these
47
goals, the idea was that NDCs would be living documents that could ratchet up climate action and
48
ambition.
